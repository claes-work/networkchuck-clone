# Certifications & Career — hub

CCNA/A+ and other certs, IT-career advice, and "hack your IT career" — how to break
into and level up in IT. This is Chuck's founding domain: the pre-2020 NetworkChuck
channel was overwhelmingly cert-study and career coaching aimed at aspiring network
engineers.

Related hubs: [[../networking/networking]] · [[../cloud-devops/cloud-devops]] ·
[[../linux-terminal/linux-terminal]].

## Key ideas & frameworks

_All positions are dated to the source that established them and paraphrased unless
quoted on the source page. Chuck's views on Cisco cert structure are explicitly
version-dated because Cisco overhauled the program in Feb 2020._

### Certs + experience over a degree

- **CCNA beats a 4-year degree for breaking into IT fast** — cheaper (~$2k self-study
  vs ~$12k+ for a bachelor's), faster (~6 months vs 2–4 years), and more current, since
  universities lag the workforce while vendor certs update quickly (2017-02-24,
  [[../../sources/2017-02-24-yt-dmthwYTEL0M]]). A degree realistically only lands the
  same entry-level help desk role a high-school grad can get; it doesn't by itself land
  a network-admin job.
- **Experience is king.** Nothing guarantees an interview like experience in the role
  you're applying for; escape the "need experience to get experience" trap by starting
  at the bottom on a help desk at a small-to-medium company (big enough to run
  Cisco/Microsoft enterprise gear, small enough to avoid red tape and get hands-on)
  (2017-02-24, [[../../sources/2017-02-24-yt-dmthwYTEL0M]]).
- College is still genuinely valuable and legitimate (especially straight out of high
  school), and some schools (WGU, University of Phoenix) now bundle certs into degrees —
  but for breaking in fast without heavy debt, certifications win (2017-02-24,
  [[../../sources/2017-02-24-yt-dmthwYTEL0M]]).

### Self-study first

- **Self-study is the best path for most people** — it's the cheapest option and it
  builds the exact skills IT demands day-to-day: discipline, grit, organization, and the
  ability to teach yourself. IT is "a constant ongoing self-study session"; the skill of
  teaching yourself is itself the job (the "X-factor") (2017-10-22,
  [[../../sources/2017-10-22-yt-MdYhc4O3G7w]]).
- The "dark side": with no external deadline it can drag on indefinitely — Chuck admits
  his own CCNP was in progress for nearly three years. People who need accountability can
  use a classroom or company-paid boot camp, as long as they eventually learn to teach
  themselves (2017-10-22, [[../../sources/2017-10-22-yt-MdYhc4O3G7w]]).

### Study resources: video training + labs > books

- **The standard exam-prep formula = a good video course + a good lab + a good book**
  (any cert) (2018-04-25, [[../../sources/2018-04-25-yt-6HsgCor5I28]]; reaffirmed with
  practice exams as an optional fourth in 2019-06-23,
  [[../../sources/2019-06-23-yt-f4p107p3vQg]]).
- **Labbing is the single most important thing** you can do to advance an IT career and
  ace the exam; if you lack confidence, "lab it up" (2019-11-28,
  [[../../sources/2019-11-28-yt-cxGCndru_c0]]; 2017-06-29,
  [[../../sources/2017-06-29-yt-woJX0eBOdnA]]). Packet Tracer and GNS3 are both great, but
  physical hardware can't be beaten (2017-12-16,
  [[../../sources/2017-12-16-yt-MN0KzfzlRio]]).
- **Stop buying individual cert books** — not because books are bad (he still uses them),
  but because a subscription library (Safari Books Online, "the Netflix for cert books")
  gives unlimited access for ~one book/month (2018-04-25,
  [[../../sources/2018-04-25-yt-6HsgCor5I28]]).
- Recommended video training is CBT Nuggets (where Chuck became a trainer) plus cheap
  Udemy courses; recommended books are Wendell Odom's OCG and Todd Lammle; practice exams
  are Boson ExSim and AlphaPrep (2019-06-23,
  [[../../sources/2019-06-23-yt-f4p107p3vQg]]).

### The "unicorn" skill stack: networking + coding + Linux

- **CCNA-or-Python is a false choice — be a "unicorn"** who knows networking *and* a
  little programming. Recruiters hunt for unicorns because they're rare. The CCNA remains
  extremely valuable (he flatly rejects the "CCNA is worthless" take), but programming is
  the future as networking shifts from manual CLI config toward controllers, scripting,
  and automation (2017-06-29, [[../../sources/2017-06-29-yt-woJX0eBOdnA]]).
- **Linux belongs on every network engineer's list** — most newer Cisco gear (Firepower,
  Collaboration/Call Manager, Nexus) and most web servers run on Linux. Networking +
  Python + Linux is "the next-generation network engineer" skill set (2017-12-19,
  [[../../sources/2017-12-19-yt-TCQdJwLMqfY]]). See [[../linux-terminal/linux-terminal]].
- The valuable engineer has **both sides**: core networking fundamentals (CCNA/CCNP,
  maybe CCIE) *and* the ability to build networks in the cloud and with containers — many
  cloud/container networks today were built by people lacking networking fundamentals.
  Practical on-ramp: learn basic Python syntax → GitHub → data formats/REST APIs →
  Linux/Docker (2018-10-30, [[../../sources/2018-10-30-yt-eAdrnTOcPOg]]). See
  [[../cloud-devops/cloud-devops]].

### Entry-cert sequencing: CompTIA vs Cisco (position evolved 2017 → 2019)

> ⚠️ CONTRADICTION / POSITION EVOLVED: Chuck's stance on CompTIA Network+ softened
> between 2017 and 2019. Present the 2019 view as current.

- **2017 position:** Skip CompTIA (including Network+ and Server+) if you're aiming above
  help desk and go straight for a vendor cert (Cisco CCENT/CCNA or Microsoft MCSA) —
  vendor certs teach the same fundamentals *plus* vendor skills and rank far higher on a
  resume. The **only** justification for the A+ is landing your very first IT job with
  zero experience; if you already have a help desk job, the A+ is "pointless" (it's
  designed to get you the job you already have) (2017-08-30,
  [[../../sources/2017-08-30-yt-EywIAz8fPnY]]).
- **2019 revisit (softened):** He still prefers CCENT for aspiring network engineers
  (it's half of the CCNA, cheaper at $165 vs $319, and reads better to a hiring manager),
  but now **openly recommends Network+** for people who don't yet know their path or are
  heading toward cloud (AWS/Azure/GCP) — it's a broad "sampler platter." He credits the
  change to having built the Network+ course on CBT Nuggets and to fresh job-board
  research. The blanket "Network+ is a no-go" line is retracted; the CCENT-first core
  recommendation is retained (2018-12-19,
  [[../../sources/2018-12-19-yt-7CP4__3t8go]]).
- Consistent across both: the A+ is "killer" for a first help-desk job but useless once
  you're on the help desk; Security+ is worth it purely for DoD/government job
  requirements (2017-08-30 / 2018-12-19).

### The canonical "how to get a job with a CCNA" playbook

Chuck's reproducible job-hunting playbook (2018-12-12,
[[../../sources/2018-12-12-yt-Azm4woyCNBA]]; delivered via an interview with his brother
Cameron, who landed a $60–70k network-admin job on just an A+, a CCNA, ~1 year of help
desk, and no degree — attribute the *coaching* to Chuck, the specific job narrative to
Cameron):

1. **Fix the resume** — certs at the top; list your *next* cert with "(in progress)" so
   the HR resume-scanning software passes you through and the manager reads you as a
   continual learner.
2. **Get visible on LinkedIn** — professional photo, clear target role, and (vital) turn
   on "career interests" so recruiters find you. Chuck says he personally got 2–3 jobs
   from LinkedIn.
3. **Apply broadly** — 30+ applications is normal; apply to anything you're remotely
   qualified for.
4. **Employers want fresh CCNAs** — less experience means fewer bad habits to untrain and
   signals you're ready to learn; a recruiter may find *you* off your fresh CCNA.
5. **Interview honesty** — if you don't know something, say so; faking is obvious and
   reads as dishonest. Communication/explaining is a tested, rare, valuable IT skill.
6. **In-person** — dress the part, firm handshake, stand and shake everyone's hand, open
   body language, project real confidence. Expect a whiteboard exercise one level deeper
   than the exam.
7. **What seals it: demonstrated passion** — talk about your home lab. Chuck's own prep
   ritual is walking somewhere alone ~30 min before and talking technologies out loud.
- Underlying principle (also 2018-12-19): "half the battle in getting a job in IT is just
  having enough keywords on your resume that it gets scanned and shows up on someone's
  desk"; reverse-engineer your cert plan from the job you *aspire* to, not the one you
  already qualify for (2018-12-19, [[../../sources/2018-12-19-yt-7CP4__3t8go]]).

### CCNP after the CCNA

- **Routing & Switching is the backbone of every Cisco track**, so CCNP R&S is the
  default next step for anyone who wants to stay well-rounded and doesn't have a role
  pulling them toward a specialty (endorsing Jeremy Cioara's advice). Always finish the
  full CCNA R&S first; don't branch from CCENT straight into a specialty. Taking a break
  after CCNA to learn Python is also a smart choice (2017-12-17,
  [[../../sources/2017-12-17-yt-M3da0R-m_Ww]]).

### Salary & job-hopping

- **How much a network engineer makes "depends"** (location, experience, certs, how well
  you market yourself). Chuck's own progression: junior help desk → senior network
  engineer at $100k over ~5–6 years, hitting $70k at age 25. **The real money comes from
  changing companies**, not from incremental annual raises at one stagnant job — even
  though switching is harder on you (2017-12-21,
  [[../../sources/2017-12-21-yt-dVXHibpRmaA]]). At the time he'd turned down higher offers
  that didn't fit the work-life balance he wanted.

### Network vs systems vs VMware — choosing a role

- Network engineer and systems engineer are the two core "IT operations / infrastructure"
  roles; each forks into ~15 sub-paths and you often cross into the other anyway. **Don't
  commit firmly yet — lab up both and chase a cert in each** (CCNA for networking; MCSA or
  VMware VCA for systems) and see what you like. At small companies you're a
  jack-of-all-trades; at big enterprises roles silo — and **"the big money comes when you
  specialize"** (2017-12-23, [[../../sources/2017-12-23-yt-vN5a-ZjiFmI]]). VMware is a
  distinct systems specialty (2018-12-22, [[../../sources/2018-12-22-yt-FpsW-1Zuhp8]]).

### The Feb-2020 Cisco cert overhaul (version-dated)

> ⚠️ VERSION-DATE: The following describes the Cisco program changes announced mid-2019
> and effective **February 24, 2020** ("certpocalypse," Feb 23 2020). These are specific
> to the 2020 restructure and may have been superseded by later Cisco changes. Do not
> present the pre-2020 track structure as current.

- **One single CCNA (200-301)** replaces the ~10 old specialized tracks (Security,
  Wireless, Collaboration, etc.); it becomes the single gateway, absorbing the old CCENT
  entry stage plus wireless/security fundamentals, with ~60–70% overlap with the old R&S
  CCNA and **~20% automation** (DNA Center, REST/JSON, Ansible/Puppet/Chef) — but **no
  Python required at CCNA level** (2019-06-10,
  [[../../sources/2019-06-10-yt-nD7zD-cpQG0]]; 2019-06-23,
  [[../../sources/2019-06-23-yt-f4p107p3vQg]]).
- **CCNP restructures into five tracks** (Enterprise, Service Provider, Data Center,
  Security, Collaboration), each earned via **one core exam + one concentration exam**
  (down from a three-exam minimum). Each concentration exam is *also its own standalone
  specialist certification* (2019-06-10 / 2019-07-09,
  [[../../sources/2019-07-09-yt-fqtr_Yyy0a4]]).
- **CCNP no longer requires the CCNA** — with no certs at all you can jump straight to any
  Cisco exam (except the CCIE lab). A brand-new **DevNet** track certifies
  software/programmability. Chuck's time-bound advice at the time: *get your CCNP/CCNA
  before Feb 2020*, because completing the old cert converted favorably into multiple new
  ones, while partial progress "turned to dust" (2019-07-09,
  [[../../sources/2019-07-09-yt-fqtr_Yyy0a4]]; 2019-06-23,
  [[../../sources/2019-06-23-yt-f4p107p3vQg]]).

### Study frameworks (how to actually get it done)

- **Pomodoro is Chuck's core study method** — one task, a 25-min uninterrupted focused
  session, write down (don't act on) stray distractions, then a 5-min break; run ~4
  cycles then a longer reward break. "Anybody can do something for 25 minutes." The real
  battle in certifying is mental — beating procrastination and overwhelm — not the
  technical content (2017-04-01, [[../../sources/2017-04-01-yt-wMyYCJBlZTQ]]; reused in
  2019-11-28, [[../../sources/2019-11-28-yt-cxGCndru_c0]]).
- **Build a system, not willpower (Atomic Habits).** Applying James Clear's habit loop
  (cue/craving/response/reward and the four laws) to cert study: the fix for lost
  motivation isn't "try harder / discipline" but tiny habits that compound over the
  ~6-month grind — implementation intentions, habit stacking, environment priming
  (dedicated study space; don't study where you eat/sleep), temptation bundling, reducing
  friction with a study plan, and immediate rewards. Chuck's signature habit is his coffee
  ritual as a "motivation ritual." Framework credited to James Clear; the IT-study
  applications are Chuck's (2019-04-24,
  [[../../sources/2019-04-24-yt-bbkBJxOo4gg]]).
- **The 84-day CCNA plan** — compress CBT Nuggets' 22 weeks of ICND1+ICND2 plans into ~12
  weeks by "doubling up," use practice exams as readiness check-ins, add spaced repetition
  via Anki flashcards, and schedule the exam now to commit to a date. From zero expect
  ~3–4 hrs/day (2019-11-28, [[../../sources/2019-11-28-yt-cxGCndru_c0]]).
- Recurring study-discipline content also covers procrastination, study slumps, studying
  with kids/at social gatherings, and annual goal-setting (see the 2015–2018 study-tips
  cluster in Source pages).

### Imposter syndrome & "never too late" encouragement

- **Imposter syndrome never fully goes away** — it hits early-career, mid-career, and
  25-year veterans alike. Coping tactics: keep refreshing knowledge, lean on your ability
  to *research and figure things out* (that's what most of IT actually is), use certs to
  build confidence, join user groups, and reframe competition into teamwork. "If you're
  not feeling stupid at least some of the time, you're not surrounding yourself with
  enough smart people" (2018-12-16, [[../../sources/2018-12-16-yt-iP9kuR2U8-o]]).
- **You are never too old.** In your 30s/40s/50s you can still change careers; older
  candidates bring life experience, patience, and customer-service **soft skills** — the
  real "X-factor" for a network engineer. Perceived age discrimination is often really a
  lack of drive (2017-12-20, [[../../sources/2017-12-20-yt-SlHfqJyqsmo]]). Chuck's
  throughline across the whole domain: hustle, keep learning, and "let's get certified
  together."

### The ONE skill in IT = people / customer-service skills

- **If there's a single most-important skill in IT, it's people / customer-service
  skills** — not a technical one. The soft skills of communicating, being pleasant to
  work with, and serving the customer are what actually differentiate a career, echoing
  his "X-factor" / soft-skills throughline elsewhere in this domain (2018-12-14,
  [[../../sources/2018-12-14-yt-T1-r_feNNnw]]).
