# How do touchscreens work?

## Summary

A short hardware explainer contrasting old **resistive** touchscreens (detect
physical pressure) with modern **capacitive** touchscreens (use the electrical
charge from your body). Chuck notes iPhones and ~99% of modern displays are
capacitive, which is why gloves or wet hands break touch input. He frames the
lesson as something he "just learned" from the NetworkChuck Academy A+ course on
laptops and mobile devices — tying the explainer to CompTIA A+ study material.

## Key points

- 2024-08-08 — Resistive touchscreens detect physical pressure on the screen;
  practical use often needed a stylus.
- 2024-08-08 — Apple wanted no stylus for the first iPhone, so displays went
  capacitive; ~99% of modern displays are capacitive, not resistive.
- 2024-08-08 — Capacitive touchscreens use the electrical charge from your body,
  which is why gloves or wet hands degrade touch response.
- 2024-08-08 — Framed as content from the NetworkChuck Academy "laptops and
  mobile devices" course, part of A+ (certification prep).

## Notable verbatim quotes

- "capacitive touchscreens actually use the electrical charge from your body to detect touch and that's why when you have gloves on or you have wet hands your touch screen won't work as well"
- "when Apple created the first smartphone they knew they needed something better because nobody wants a stylus so let's not use a stylus"
