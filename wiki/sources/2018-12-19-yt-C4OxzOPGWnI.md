# 5 Reasons You Shouldn't Become a Network Engineer | CCNA | Information Technology

## Summary

A light-hearted countdown in which Chuck brings his wife Abby on camera to list the
downsides of being a network engineer — framed mostly from the perspective of the
spouse and family who live with an obsessed IT person. Despite the title, Chuck
repeatedly reaffirms that he thinks everyone should become a network engineer; the
"reasons not to" are really the personal costs (talking about IT constantly, hoarding
gear, endless studying, breaking the home internet, and marathon maintenance windows).
Toward the end, his brother Cameron ("Network Cameron") adds a genuinely serious note:
the concern that traditional network engineering could shrink over the next decade
unless you keep adapting and learning. The video is part of Chuck's "12 days"–style
giveaway series (giving away Boson NetSim for CCNA and a David Bombal Python-for-
network-engineers course).

## Key claims (dated — the 5 reasons)

All claims dated 2018-12-19; source: this video.

1. **You never stop talking about IT.** Chuck concedes he talks about IT constantly,
   especially when excited about something new — and it worsens around his IT family
   (brother and dad), where he and his brother tune everyone else out to talk Cisco.
   He reframes this as actually a *reason to* become a network engineer: you get to be
   passionate about your work.
2. **You accumulate and leave IT gear everywhere.** Over the years Chuck amassed
   desktops, servers, routers, and switches — filling closets, and previously wiring an
   apartment with Ethernet phones in every room. He notes a good IT spouse eventually
   comes to like the hum/whir of switches (Abby found it warm and soothing).
3. **You're always studying.** Chuck studied heavily early in his career (CCNA, then
   CCNP), bringing his official certification guide to family occasions (e.g. 4th of
   July) and doing flashcards in spare moments. He mentions using the **Pomodoro
   method** (25-minute focused study, 5-minute break) — which Abby dislikes because his
   breaks interrupt conversations. His stance: studying pays off, but find balance
   (which he admits he hasn't yet, as he was studying for CCIE troubleshooting).
4. **The (home) internet randomly stops working.** Because he's constantly tinkering
   with his own router / Cisco router / pfSense, he takes the home internet down —
   sometimes for hours, especially while learning. Anecdotes: shorting out a Paris
   apartment's electricity on the first night with US-plug lab gear, and spending
   ~3 hours failing to fix his parents' DSL (pppoe authentication) trying to extend a
   DMVPN connection between family houses.
5. **Maintenance windows / maintenance weekends (the #1, worst reason).** Production
   maintenance windows (installing routers, big config changes, server upgrades) run
   far longer than promised — Chuck says he has never once finished on time; a
   "couple hours" turns into being out until 6 a.m., wrecking the weekend. Generalized
   takeaway: everything in IT always takes longer than you say.

**Bonus (Cameron's serious point):** Cameron raises the concern he's been told —
that network engineering "could be going away" in the next 10 years. Both agree the
mitigation is to keep adapting and learning, since the field has always been changing;
those who keep learning will be fine.

**Upside (Abby's counterpoint):** the reason it's worth it — you can make good money in
IT if you work and study hard, enough that Abby doesn't have to work.
