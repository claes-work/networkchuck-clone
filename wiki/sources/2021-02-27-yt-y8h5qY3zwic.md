# forcing my kids to make Ethernet cables // FREE CCNA // EP 11

## Summary

Episode 11 of NetworkChuck's FREE CCNA series, covering copper Ethernet cabling as a
hands-on rite of passage for IT/CCNA students (maps to CCNA exam objective 1.3). Chuck
frames the video as teaching his daughters (unnamed, self-reported family context) to
terminate Ethernet cables while the viewer follows along.

The video covers: why you make your own cables (custom lengths for home/business runs
are the most economical reason; store-bought pre-made cables are the recommended
default); required supplies (Cat5e UTP cable, crimper, RJ45 heads, an optional cable
tester); the physical termination process (stripping ~half an inch of the sheath by
scoring/scratching without nicking the copper, untwisting and straightening pairs,
arranging colors in T568B order, trimming flat, inserting into the RJ45 head clip-down,
crimping, testing). Between the hands-on steps Chuck does a deep "how it works" dive:
UTP vs STP shielding, why pairs are twisted (defeating EMI and crosstalk), plenum vs
regular jackets, how voltage changes on copper encode binary 1s and 0s, the historical
progression of standards (10BASE-T with Cat3, 100BASE-TX / Fast Ethernet with Cat5,
1000BASE-T / gigabit with Cat5e), transmit/receive pin assignments, straight-through vs
crossover wiring, auto-MDI-X making crossover cables obsolete on modern gear, the 100 m
copper distance limit (late collisions beyond it), and a look ahead to Cat6a
(10GBASE-T) and Cat8 (40GBASE-T). Ends with a quiz for his daughters and a sponsor
practice-exam question about late collisions.

## Key claims (dated — cabling concepts)

All from 2021-02-27, paraphrased unless quoted:

- "Making an Ethernet cable" really means putting new RJ45 heads on the ends of the
  cable — terminating the ends. Store-bought pre-made cables are the recommended way;
  the main legitimate reason to make your own is custom lengths for home/business runs,
  which is the most economical option.
- Supplies needed: at minimum Cat5e UTP cable (the most common cable), a crimper (cuts
  cable and crimps the heads), RJ45 Ethernet heads, and optionally a cable tester
  (worth it, saves headaches).
- Strip about half an inch of the outer sheath (jacket); an inch is fine for a first
  attempt. Strip by lightly scoring/scratching the sheath (not cutting through), then
  bending until it separates — scoring too deep risks nicking the copper wires inside.
- A standard cable has four twisted pairs = eight colored copper wires. Copper is used
  because it conducts electricity well.
- The sheath protects the wires but does little against EMI (electromagnetic
  interference), emitted by any device with swiftly changing electrical currents.
  Twisting the wires into pairs protects against both EMI and crosstalk (interference
  between parallel signals).
- UTP = unshielded twisted pair (jacket + twists only). STP = shielded twisted pair,
  which adds an extra shielding layer against EMI; STP is used in high-EMI environments
  like factories. Home/business runs mostly use UTP.
- Jacket types matter: regular jackets emit toxic chemicals if burned; plenum-rated
  cable uses a jacket designed for fire-hazard spaces like ceilings/air-handling runs.
- Cat3/Cat5/Cat5e/Cat6 refer to the individual copper wires inside the cable, not the
  whole cable.
- Voltage encodes binary: changing the voltage on the wire signals 1s and 0s (Chuck's
  oversimplified example: ~+2.5 V = a 1, ~-2.5 V = a 0). Real signaling uses many
  varying voltages and encoding schemes.
- Standards history: 10BASE-T (circa 1990) = 10 Mbps over Cat3, 4 wires / 2 pairs;
  the "T" means twisted pair (copper). 100BASE-TX (circa 1995) = "Fast Ethernet," 100
  Mbps, still 4 wires but upgraded from Cat3 to Cat5 wire. 1000BASE-T = gigabit
  Ethernet over Cat5e, all 8 wires.
- On an end device's NIC (e.g. a Raspberry Pi, PC), transmit is on pins 1 and 2,
  receive is on pins 3 and 6. A switch flips this — it receives on 1 and 2 and sends on
  3 and 6 — so an end-device-to-switch link lines up.
- Straight-through cable: same color order on both ends (pin 1→1, 2→2, 3→3, 6→6). Used
  to connect unlike devices (PC to switch). Most common cable; it's the one made in
  this video.
- Crossover cable: crosses the transmit/receive pairs (pin 1→3, 2→6). Historically
  required to connect like devices (PC-to-PC, switch-to-switch) because both sides
  transmit/receive on the same pins. Made by wiring one end T568A and the other T568B.
- T568B pin order (used in this video for the straight-through cable): white-orange,
  orange, white-green, blue, white-blue, green, white-brown, brown. T568A is the
  crossover-partner pin-out.
- On 1000BASE-T (gigabit), each pair can send AND receive simultaneously (all four
  pairs used bidirectionally), part of why it reaches gigabit speeds. Wire order still
  matters — both ends must match for a straight-through cable.
- Auto-MDI-X: modern switches/NICs auto-detect and swap which pins they send/receive
  on, so crossover cables are effectively not needed on gigabit gear (and switches had
  this even on 100BASE-TX). Exam answer for "connect two switches" is still "crossover
  cable," but in reality auto-MDI-X handles it.
- Copper cable distance limit is 100 meters (applies to 10BASE-T, 100BASE-TX,
  1000BASE-T). Exceeding it degrades the signal and causes late collisions; go longer
  with fiber.
- Future/faster wire: Cat6 is a more reliable Cat5e; Cat6a = 10GBASE-T (10 Gbps); Cat8
  = 40GBASE-T (40 Gbps). Still eight wires.
- Termination finish: insert wires with the RJ45 clip pointing down, white-orange on
  the left and brown on the far right; spot-check colors through the head; ensure part
  of the sheath sits inside the head so the crimper clamps it; crimp; then test.
- A cable tester lights pins 1–8 on each end; for a straight-through cable the same
  numbers must light up in order on both ends (1-1, 2-2, …) to pass.
