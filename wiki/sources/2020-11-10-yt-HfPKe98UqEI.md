# i hacked my grandma (social engineering and pretexting) // FREE Security+ // EP 3

## Summary

Episode 3 of Chuck's FREE Security+ series, sponsored by CompTIA. Chuck demonstrates
**social engineering** — specifically **pretexting** (inventing a fictitious story about
who you are and why you're calling in order to elicit information) — by phoning two
consenting family members, his wife and his grandmother, while acting as "Anita" from
a store/insurer they actually do business with. He frames the whole exercise as an
awareness demonstration: he repeatedly warns "never hack anyone for any reason, this is
for educational purposes only," and the payoff is a defensive lesson, not an attack manual.

Both women refused to hand over credit card details, which Chuck praises, though they did
give up their address — the teachable failure point. He uses this to pivot into a broader
segment on **identity theft/fraud**: how attackers first gather information (often from
what people themselves post on social media), how vishing (voice phishing) and phishing
fit in, and how **data breaches** leak personal data outside the victim's control. He tours
2020 breaches (Microsoft, MGM Resorts, T-Mobile), demonstrates **Have I Been Pwned** as a
self-check tool, and stresses regular password changes. He closes with physical social
engineering — **tailgating** through a badge-controlled door by exploiting social pressure —
and defensive advice: be vigilant, assume your accounts can be hacked and your data can leak,
and use monitoring services as an extra set of eyes. Techniques used (caller-ID spoofing via
a Raspberry Pi 3CX phone system, voice modification) are named but presented as narrative
color for the awareness point, not as an operational tutorial.

## Key claims (paraphrase — dated 2020-11-10)

- Social engineering is one of the core attack categories; a main goal is to **elicit
  (gather) information** from a target through various means. [concept]
- **Pretexting** = creating a fictitious story about who you are and why you're contacting
  someone, to extract information from them. [concept]
- A **sense of urgency** (e.g. "your payment didn't go through, you're not covered") is a
  common pretexting tactic to pressure the target into acting. [concept]
- **Vishing** = voice/telephone-based phishing used to elicit information; it builds on the
  phishing concepts covered in the prior episode. [concept]
- Attackers begin a hack by **gathering information**, much of which victims publish
  themselves on social media — the more attackers learn about you, the easier it is to
  impersonate you. [concept]
- People **over the age of 55 are more susceptible** to these attacks and are the largest
  pool of victims because they tend to be more trusting. [claim, subject-stated]
- **Identity theft** at its core is simply someone pretending to be you; Chuck cites ~14
  million US identity-theft victims in 2019 (about 1 in 15 people). [claim, subject-stated]
- **Data breaches** are largely outside the victim's control: trusted companies leak
  usernames, passwords, and payment/personal data in bulk. He cites 2020 breaches at
  Microsoft, MGM Resorts, and T-Mobile as examples. [claim, subject-stated]
- **Have I Been Pwned** lets you check whether your email appears in known breaches; leaked
  hashed passwords from such breaches are what feed password-cracking. [defensive tool]
- **Tailgating** = following an authorized person through a secured door by exploiting the
  social pressure to hold the door open; an attacker who looks the part and acts with
  confidence is rarely questioned. [concept]
- **Defensive lesson:** be vigilant; assume any account can be hacked and any data can be
  leaked. Change passwords regularly, share awareness with vulnerable family, use
  monitoring services (dark-web/account monitoring) as another set of eyes, and resist the
  urge to hold secured doors for strangers. [defensive]
- **Ethics framing:** run a "family pen test" with consent for awareness — but "don't really
  hack them"; the demonstration is education/awareness only. [ethics]
