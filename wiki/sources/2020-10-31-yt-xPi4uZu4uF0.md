# WAN....it's not the internet!! (sometimes) // FREE CCNA // EP 8

## Summary

Episode 8 of Chuck's FREE CCNA series covers WAN (Wide Area Network) technologies:
how a company's geographically separate sites — corporate office, data center, and
branch offices (his running "NetworkChuck Coffee" analogy, with coffee shops in Dallas,
Phoenix, and Seattle) — connect to each other. He first frames *why* sites must
connect: services are centralized (phone systems/CUCM, email, databases, websites,
payroll, POS) in the data center or corporate office, so branches need reliable
connections back.

He walks the technologies chronologically. **Legacy:** leased lines (T1 ≈ 1.54 Mbps,
T3 ≈ 43 Mbps; E1/E3 in Europe) — dedicated to your traffic, but expensive and messy to
mesh across many sites; plus Frame Relay and ATM, older leased-line alternatives no
longer configured on the CCNA. **Modern-ish:** MPLS (Multi-Protocol Label Switching),
the long-standing de-facto standard for connecting branches — a private, non-internet
carrier WAN using label switching at "layer 2.5," creating virtual circuits per
customer (hence "MPLS VPN," though not encrypted), Layer 3 at the customer connection,
with CE (customer edge) and PE (provider edge) routers. **Metro Ethernet (Metro E):** a
fast (1G/10G) fiber cable run under a city between main sites, usually Layer 2
point-to-point; variants E-Line (point-to-point / EVC), E-LAN (full-mesh "switch in the
sky"), and E-Tree (hub-and-spoke), demonstrated against Spectrum's enterprise WAN
product page. **Internet VPN:** the cheapest branch option — public internet plus a
site-to-site VPN (encrypted), but unreliable and lacking QoS traffic prioritization.
MPLS solved QoS; SD-WAN is now the rising competitor/replacement, especially as traffic
shifts to public cloud (AWS/Azure) rather than the data center. The episode closes with
two Boson ExSim CCNA practice questions (WAN cost reduction; VPN facts).

## Key claims (dated — the concepts)

All paraphrase unless quoted. Established/reaffirmed in this source, 2020-10-31.

- LAN vs WAN: anything inside a contained/private network (office, data center, house)
  is a LAN; anything outside those private networks is a WAN. WAN here focuses on
  connecting branches back to corporate/data center.
- Sites connect because services are centralized — phone systems (e.g., Cisco CUCM),
  email, databases, websites, payroll, and POS typically live in the data center or
  corporate office, so branches need connections back.
- Leased lines are dedicated cables leased for your traffic only, guaranteeing
  performance/speed/latency (unlike shared consumer internet), but are expensive and
  hard to mesh across many locations. Reference speeds: T1 ≈ 1.54 Mbps, T3 ≈ 43 Mbps;
  Europe uses E1/E3 at comparable speeds.
- Frame Relay and ATM were older alternatives to leased lines; Frame Relay
  configuration used to be on the CCNA but is no longer tested.
- MPLS (Multi-Protocol Label Switching) has been the de-facto standard for connecting
  branch offices to corporate/data centers. You order one MPLS circuit per site; the
  carrier's MPLS network routes traffic between all your sites.
- MPLS is private via per-customer virtual circuits, keeping your traffic separate from
  other customers — this is why it's called an MPLS VPN. It is virtual and private but
  not necessarily encrypted; separation comes from label switching, not encryption.
- MPLS operates at "layer 2.5" — not purely a Layer 2 data-link protocol nor a Layer 3
  network protocol. It applies a label at that layer so the carrier network knows which
  customer/virtual circuit the traffic belongs to.
- The customer connection into the MPLS cloud is Layer 3 (routing packets). The router
  at the customer's LAN edge is the CE (customer edge) router; the carrier's router is
  the PE (provider edge) router.
- Metro Ethernet (Metro E) is essentially a provider cable (usually fiber) run under a
  metropolitan area to connect main sites; often Layer 2 (exchanging Ethernet frames).
  Typical speeds 1 Gbps or 10 Gbps, usually with redundant (two) connections.
- Metro E point-to-point between two sites is called a point-to-point (P2P) connection;
  it resembles a leased line but is much faster. It can span beyond one metro area but
  is too expensive/uneconomical for typical branch/coffee-shop connections — used mainly
  for data-center-to-corporate and data-center-to-data-center (including DR secondary
  data centers).
- Metro E service types: E-Line (point-to-point, the circuit called an EVC / Ethernet
  virtual circuit), E-LAN (full-mesh, like one massive Layer 2 switch across distance),
  and E-Tree (hub-and-spoke). Spectrum's equivalents: Ethernet Private Line (E-Line),
  Ethernet Virtual Private Line (E-Tree/hub-and-spoke), Ethernet Private LAN (E-LAN).
  Metro E is backed by SLAs (service level agreements).
- For branch offices, a public internet connection plus a site-to-site VPN is the
  cheapest option. Unlike an MPLS "VPN," this VPN adds headers and encrypts packets so
  only you can read the traffic across the public internet.
- The public internet is unreliable for this — VPN connections drop, calls degrade, and
  you cannot prioritize traffic. MPLS's advantage was QoS (Quality of Service):
  marking/labeling important traffic (e.g., voice calls) for VIP treatment. QoS is just
  marking certain traffic as important and giving it priority.
- MPLS is dying (not yet dead), being displaced by SD-WAN (software-defined WAN), which
  uses standard internet connections and improves on site-to-site VPN problems.
- SD-WAN is rising also because traffic increasingly goes to public cloud (AWS, Azure)
  rather than back to the data center, so the site-to-cloud connection now matters more.
- Site-to-site VPN (site-to-site because it connects two sites, e.g. coffee shop to data
  center) is not new, is significantly cheaper than alternatives, and with the site's
  routers/firewall handling the VPN, end-user workstations need no VPN client.
- Boson practice-question takeaways: to reduce WAN/telephone cost, favor per-office
  internet access (DIA — dedicated internet access), internet VPN for interoffice
  connectivity, and a converged network with QoS; avoid old/expensive options (ATM,
  leased lines). VPNs typically cost less than a traditional WAN.
