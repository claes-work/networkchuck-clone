# your favorite websites can be HACKED! (watering holes, typo squatting) // FREE Security+ // EP 4

## Summary

Episode 4 of Chuck Keith's free CompTIA Security+ course covers a family of social
engineering / web attacks that exploit the trust users place in familiar, "safe"
destinations. The through-line: attackers strike when you feel safe and have let your
guard down, not when you are alert. Chuck walks through **watering hole attacks**
(compromising a website a target audience commonly visits so the whole population gets
poisoned at once), **typosquatting** (registering misspelled look-alike domains to
catch fat-finger typos), and **email prepending** (how the "internal"/"external"
subject-line tags meant as a defense can be turned into an offensive trick). He uses a
running joke of "hacking" his own `networkchuck.com` audience to illustrate the concepts,
cites the real-world Forbes "Thought of the Day" watering-hole compromise, and closes on
the defensive lesson: on the internet there is no truly safe place, so stay vigilant.
The episode is co-produced with David Bombal and Jeremy Cioara, with day-by-day content
released at thisisit.io.

## Key claims (dated — concepts + defense)

All dated 2020-11-17 (paraphrase unless quoted):

- Some social engineering attacks are ones almost no one is safe from, including
  security-savvy people, because they exploit the moment you feel safe on a site you
  know and trust.
- If a website you trust gets hacked, you would not know — there is no flashing "we're
  hacked" warning; the compromised site looks and feels normal.
- A **watering hole attack** compromises a website that a target group commonly visits,
  so that instead of attacking each person individually, the attacker poisons one
  central spot everyone comes to (analogy: animals gathering at a single desert
  watering hole).
- Watering hole attacks are typically **more targeted**, which is what makes them more
  dangerous; attackers reason that a given audience (e.g., a channel's subscribers)
  will visit a specific related site.
- The attack method: scan the target website for vulnerabilities, exploit them, and set
  up the malicious payload — often a page that tries to get the visitor to download
  something malicious.
- In the worst cases, no user action is required — simply visiting the compromised page
  infects you (drive-by), with no download, sign-up, or credential entry needed.
- **Forbes** (its "Thought of the Day" feature, which drew millions of daily users) was
  compromised in a watering hole attack that chained **two zero-day vulnerabilities** —
  one in Microsoft Internet Explorer and one in Adobe Flash Player.
- A **zero-day vulnerability** is the worst kind: a vulnerability no one knows about
  yet, so there is no patch.
- Defensive lesson: on the internet there is no truly safe place; you must stay vigilant
  because eventually there will always be a vulnerability you don't know about.
- High-profile sites (Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn) are harder to compromise because they
  have strong security teams, so attackers don't always need to hack the site to fool you.
- **Typosquatting** (typo squatting): attackers register domains similar to popular ones
  (e.g., a misspelling of facebook.com) to catch users who fat-finger the address bar;
  these look-alike sites redirect users to random/phishing destinations.
- Typosquatting works because social engineering targets the human mind and fingers,
  which make mistakes; attackers snatch up many domain names resembling existing ones.
- Large companies mitigate typosquatting by buying up the look-alike domains, or by
  suing the people who own them, to shut down the phishing sites (example given:
  the notorious "goggle.com" mimicking Google).
- **Email prepending** as defense: spam filters mark internal mail (from your own
  company domain) as trusted and often leave it untagged, while flagging mail from
  outside organizations as "external" in the subject line — helping employees spot
  spoofed "from" addresses that claim to be a colleague.
- **Prepending turned offensive:** attackers can prepend their own "internal" tag in the
  subject line so that users, conditioned to trust internal-tagged mail, click malicious
  links — a defense mechanism weaponized as offense.
- Overall defensive framing: assume attackers target you when you feel safe and have let
  your guard down, so never fully let your guard down; be vigilant and trust nothing
  completely online.
- A good spam filter (an appliance or cloud service that all email passes through) helps
  combat phishing but cannot catch everything, since attackers keep getting smarter.
