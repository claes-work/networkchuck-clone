# let's hack your home network // FREE CCNA // EP 9

## Summary
Episode 9 of Chuck Keith's FREE CCNA series teaches home-network fundamentals through a "let's hack your home network" hook. Chuck argues that most home networks can be hacked and walks the viewer through auditing their own network, then hardening it. He introduces the SOHO (small office / home office) network concept — every device connecting to one all-in-one box that is simultaneously router, wireless access point, and switch — and contrasts it with larger enterprise networks. He explains the public IP address as a layer-3 address the ISP assigns to the router, demonstrates how it leaks a rough physical location and becomes an attack target, and shows two ways to scan a network for open ports: the easy web tool (pentest-tools.com) and the "geeky" method of spinning up a cheap Linux cloud server (Linode) and running nmap (`nmap -sT`, `nmap --script vuln`, port-specific `-sT -p`, and OS detection `-O`). He then gives a hands-on hardening checklist on a TP-Link router (firewall on, port forwarding off, remote management off, change default credentials, update firmware, secure WPA2/WPA3 wireless with non-default SSID and strong password, disable ping responses on WAN). The back half covers segmenting untrusted IoT devices onto isolated VLANs with client isolation (his own UniFi setup: "Portkey", "Seeker of Truth", "Horcruxes" networks), IDS/IPS monitoring on a UniFi Dream Machine Pro, securing the work-from-home connection to a company via remote-access VPN (software) vs. site-to-site VPN (appliance), and running your own home VPN server for remote access. Sponsored by Boson software. Delivered solo, coffee-fueled and high-energy.

## Key claims
- (2020-12-06) A typical home network is a SOHO — small office / home office — network: all devices (phones, computers, tablets, Alexas, smart bulbs, TVs) connect to one all-in-one device that is router, WAP, and switch at once, and that device connects to the internet over one cable (coax or ethernet).
- (2020-12-06) Enterprise networks have massive routers, switches, firewalls, and many security features, yet still get hacked — so a small home network is even less safe; in most cases it is not safe.
- (2020-12-06) Chuck names four weak areas of a home network: (1) attacks straight from the internet, (2) the devices connected inside your network (called the scariest weak point), (3) wireless — anyone passing by can see and try to connect, and (4) the connection to your company/job when working from home.
- (2020-12-06) When you connect to the internet, your ISP gives your router a public IP address; this is a layer-3 network address used to surf the internet, likened to your name / home address so websites know how to reach you.
- (2020-12-06) You should not reveal your public IP address: it reveals a rough physical location (Chuck demos whatismyipaddress.com pinpointing near a home, e.g. Kansas) and lets a hacker begin probing your network for weaknesses.
- (2020-12-06) Ports are "holes" in your network; a router firewall protects the inside from the outside, but opening a port (e.g. port 80 / HTTP for a website you ran) leaves a hole that can persist even after you stop using the service. Ideally you want no open ports at all.
- (2020-12-06) pentest-tools.com's "scan your network" feature scans your public IP for open ports; in the demo it flagged port 3389 (remote desktop) open, which you never want exposed.
- (2020-12-06) That web tool uses nmap, a free open-source scanning program, under the hood — so you can run nmap yourself.
- (2020-12-06) To scan the outside of your network you need a Linux server outside it; you can create one cheaply in the cloud on Linode (~$5/month, Debian 10, default settings), which is easy even for beginners.
- (2020-12-06) Install nmap with one command (`apt install nmap -y`), then scan with `nmap -sT <ip>` (a TCP scan of 1000 ports). In the demo it found ports 80, 22, 443, and 1720 open.
- (2020-12-06) `nmap --script vuln <public-ip>` runs a script that checks a network against known vulnerabilities; the demo surfaced a Slowloris DDoS vulnerability and a TLS vulnerability — exactly what real hackers do after learning a public IP.
- (2020-12-06) A VPN (Chuck uses NordVPN) hides your public IP from the internet while surfing, but it is not foolproof: only the device running the VPN is protected; every other device in the network still uses the real public IP, and hackers scan whole ranges of IPs, not just one.
- (2020-12-06) Router hardening step 1: make sure the firewall is enabled (Advanced > Security > Firewall on the TP-Link demo).
- (2020-12-06) Router hardening step 2: turn off port forwarding — each entry under NAT forwarding > port forwarding is one open hole; the firewall being on does not close ports you have forwarded. (Example: Plex forwards port 32400.)
- (2020-12-06) Router hardening step 3: disable remote management (System > Administration) — it lets an outside attacker reach your router's login portal (e.g. on port 443); router firmware is often vulnerable and can be exploited from that page.
- (2020-12-06) Router hardening step 4: change the default router username and password immediately — defaults like admin/admin or admin/password are easily found.
- (2020-12-06) Router hardening step 5: update your router firmware — vendors release updates either to add a feature or to patch a discovered vulnerability; un-updated hardware likely has an exploitable hole.
- (2020-12-06) Router hardening step 6 (wireless): use WPA2 always (and WPA3 when available — always the most secure standard), use a strong password, and do not leave the SSID as a default (e.g. "TP-Link"). A default SSID tells a war-driver what router you have so they can look up its vulnerabilities.
- (2020-12-06) Do not put guests on your main network; give them a separate guest network that cannot touch your stuff. A router lacking a guest-network feature is a reason to upgrade.
- (2020-12-06) Bonus hardening step 7: disable "respond to pings" from WAN so your router does not answer a ping from outside — you want to fly under the radar when attackers scan IP ranges.
- (2020-12-06) The scariest weak point is internal devices: everything on your internal network can reach out to the internet and receive replies through the firewall, and so can IoT devices (smart bulbs, TVs) — but you do not know who those devices are talking to; a compromised bulb reaching a malicious server can put a hacker on your network, and a basic firewall sees it as legitimate traffic.
- (2020-12-06) Chuck's mitigation is network segmentation: put untrusted IoT devices on their own VLANs, separate from trusted devices, so they cannot reach his important systems (like his NAS / file storage). His trusted wireless is "Portkey" (main), and IoT sits on separate VLANs — "Seeker of Truth" (VLAN 6/7) and "Horcruxes" (VLAN 8) for the least-trusted cheap devices.
- (2020-12-06) He adds client isolation (device isolation) so IoT devices (e.g. Alexa, Philips Hue) cannot even talk to each other on their own network — recommended for IoT.
- (2020-12-06) Consumer routers (TP-Link and similar) cannot do VLAN/client isolation; options to get these features: DD-WRT custom firmware on a supported Linksys/Asus/Netgear router; buying used Cisco gear off eBay (great for CCNA/CCNP study); or UniFi/Ubiquiti "prosumer" gear.
- (2020-12-06) UniFi is controller-based (a controller device/server manages routers, switches, access points). The UniFi Dream Machine (UDM, ~$300) combines controller, wireless AP, switch, and firewall in one device; Chuck runs the faster Dream Machine Pro (UDM Pro) as his firewall/router plus two switches and three access points, making his home network feel enterprise-grade.
- (2020-12-06) The UDM Pro's IDS/IPS (intrusion detection / intrusion protection) is important with IoT: IDS tells you a threat happened; IPS detects and actively blocks it. It also runs continuous endpoint scans (like nmap on a schedule) identifying devices, their OS, open ports, and vulnerabilities. In the demo it detected a network trojan.
- (2020-12-06) You can replicate endpoint scanning inside your own network by running nmap on a Linux box against your subnet, e.g. `nmap -sT 192.168.1.0/24`, adding `-O` to detect the operating systems of devices.
- (2020-12-06) Working from home, you are the biggest security weakness to your company; the fix is a VPN. Two company models: remote-access VPN (software on your computer, e.g. OpenVPN or Cisco AnyConnect) vs. site-to-site VPN (the company gives you a firewall appliance — e.g. a Sophos firewall or a Cisco ASA 5506 — that your work computer connects to and that keeps a constant secure tunnel to the corporate network, giving the company tighter control).
- (2020-12-06) If you lock your network down but still want to reach internal services (file servers, a Plex server) while away, run your own VPN server on your home network — many home routers already include one you can enable, or use custom firmware (DD-WRT, Tomato), pfSense, or UniFi. Chuck uses UniFi to reach his home network remotely.
- (2020-12-06) Recurring call to action: harden and secure your own router, and also your friends' and family's routers, because you use their Wi-Fi when you visit.
