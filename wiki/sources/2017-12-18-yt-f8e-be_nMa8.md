# Is it still WORTH IT to become a Network Engineer? | CCNA CCNP

## Summary
Chuck answers a question he says he gets often — whether becoming a network engineer is still worth it — with an emphatic yes (as of December 2017). His core argument: network engineering lacks the marketing/PR that programming enjoys, which is actually a competitive advantage because fewer people enter the field. He directly rebuts the fear that network automation and SDN will replace network engineers, framing automation as just the latest chapter in a career that has "always been changing." He describes the field as a low-competition "shortcut to success" with strong demand, tempered by a tough barrier to entry (experience requirements).

## Key claims
- (2017-12-18) It is still absolutely worth it to become a network engineer / break into the field.
- (2017-12-18) Network engineering lacks the marketing/PR campaign that programming has — and Chuck argues that is a *good* thing, because it keeps competition low for those who do enter.
- (2017-12-18) Network/system engineering and admin roles are under-advertised; when he was in high school he only knew about programming and had never heard of network engineering — he discovered it on the helpdesk watching an engineer at a "black screen" who looked "like he's from the matrix." It was "a secret path you could take."
- (2017-12-18) On the automation fear — people are scared that network automation is "coming" or "here" and that engineers will be replaced by machines/programmers. Chuck flatly calls this "false." Network engineering is changing, but it has always been changing; engineers will "have to learn a bit of programming," maybe a lot depending on the role, but they won't be replaced. (Explicitly his 2017 view — automation is a threat to guard against, not an eliminator of the role.)
- (2017-12-18) Network engineers "are going to be needed very much so in the future" and are needed now.
- (2017-12-18) The career is still "rare" and in high demand — Chuck says he lives in a good market (Dallas) and during hiring season gets three or four recruiter calls a day; companies "can't fill the positions."
- (2017-12-18) The barrier to entry can be tough because roles demand 2–3, 5, or 10 years of experience, creating a chicken-and-egg problem for newcomers; he promises later tips/tricks to market yourself around it.
- (2017-12-18) Because competition is low, Chuck says he advanced far and fast in his career on "a little bit of Drive" and "a little bit of hustle" — he calls the field "like cheating" and "a shortcut to success."
- (2017-12-18) Encouragement to CCENT/CCNA students — "don't lose heart"; with hustle and continuous learning you'll have an incredible career, make good money, and be able to provide for your family, and do it fast.
