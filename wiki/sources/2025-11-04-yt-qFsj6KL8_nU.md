# DEFCON is Not What I Expected...

## Summary

A vlog of Chuck's first-ever DEF CON (Las Vegas), the annual hacker convention he
says draws ~30,000 hackers. Having avoided it for years because of its scary
reputation, he came in expecting danger and left having been "welcomed with open
arms." He frames the video as a rare filmed tour through DEF CON's villages —
normally a no-film environment — and repeatedly pivots from "this is sketchy/scary"
to "this is education, and the community is the real point." He walks through the
Packet Hacking Village (Wall of Sheep), Lock Picking Village, Physical Security
Village, Car Hacking Village, and the (unfilmable) Social Engineering Village, then
closes on his core thesis: DEF CON's value is community and networking — including
as a literal hiring/career shortcut for anyone wanting to break into cybersecurity.
He also notes DEF CON is distinct from Black Hat, which he attended the week before.

## Key claims (dated — what he saw + value)

- (2025-11-04) DEF CON is an annual hacker event that (per Chuck) gathers ~30,000
  hackers; this was his first time attending. He'd avoided it for years because of
  its scary reputation.
- (2025-11-04) The standard security advice on arrival: turn off Bluetooth and Wi-Fi
  and leave them off, and carry an RFID-blocking wallet to prevent card skimming;
  his team prepped this way (camera guy Brandon bought corded headphones).
- (2025-11-04) Reality vs. reputation: most hackers there were "pretty cool" and
  nice; beginners who are humble and want to learn are welcomed. He saw kids there
  and says he'll bring his own kids next year.
- (2025-11-04) Filming is normally banned and enforced by red-shirted staff called
  "goons," but many goons watched his channel and let his crew film — footage most
  people don't get.
- (2025-11-04) Packet Hacking Village hosts the "Wall of Sheep": usernames,
  passwords, and credentials captured from anyone using DEF CON Wi-Fi without good
  security. Despite the reputation, he found the people there the nicest he's met.
- (2025-11-04) Lock Picking Village: he picked his first lock (badly), was zip-tied
  and handcuffed, and was taught how to escape both. He got "addicted" and bought
  two lock-picking kits.
- (2025-11-04) Physical Security Village teaches multiple methods to defeat doors
  (coat hanger under-the-door tool, under-door deadbolt tools, Flipper Zero cloning
  of key cards/fobs), plus breaking into cars and skimming credit cards. He tested
  Flipper Zero card-cloning on his own pool gate and it worked.
- (2025-11-04) The justification he was given — and endorses — for teaching offensive
  physical skills: these vulnerabilities already exist and criminals already know
  them; seeing how attacks work lets you recognize and fix the weaknesses. It's
  education ("Fix it. And I can get behind that").
- (2025-11-04) Car Hacking Village: cars are networks of hundreds of computers linked
  by the CAN bus, a broadcast network where any connected node can read and inject
  messages to every system. Adding 4G/Wi-Fi/Bluetooth gave remote attackers a way in;
  compromise of driver-assist systems (steering/braking/throttle) is a safety hazard.
  They had a Ford F-150 "in a box" for hands-on fuzzing; he bought a "CAN bus flipper"
  from vendors and floated making a dedicated car-hacking video.
- (2025-11-04) Social Engineering Village was the most popular (line out the door),
  couldn't be filmed, and ran live on-stage competitions to social-engineer companies
  over the phone — which he calls the most dangerous skill at DEF CON.
- (2025-11-04) He compares DEF CON to Disneyland/Disney World for anyone into tech,
  and admits he spent way too much money on gadgets.
- (2025-11-04) A planned talk with John Hammond fell through (no speaker system), but
  fans came to meet them anyway; he frames the meetups with his audience as the best
  part.
- (2025-11-04) Career thesis: riding the Tesla loop, he met a CISO who said he attends
  DEF CON specifically to hire. Chuck's takeaway — networking/community at cons is the
  "secret path"/"shortcut" into a cybersecurity career.
- (2025-11-04) He only scratched the surface; villages he didn't reach include Bug
  Bounty, Scam Baiting, AI, and Red Teaming (he attended the Red Teaming after-party).
  He notes the con runs around the clock with parties and after-hours activity.
- (2025-11-04) He mentions BSides events alongside DEF CON as accessible hacking
  events worth attending (in his closing prayer).
