# you need to learn Google Cloud RIGHT NOW!!

## Summary

A "you need to learn X RIGHT NOW" tutorial in which Chuck Keith pitches Google Cloud
Platform (GCP) as one of the highest-paying skills for 2022 and pushes viewers to start
learning it immediately — and for free. The video is the third cloud entry in the
learn-X series (after AWS and hybrid cloud). Its central practical hook is **Google
Cloud skill badges**: individually earnable, verifiable credentials Chuck himself just
earned and displays on his LinkedIn profile under licenses and certifications.

Chuck first situates GCP in the cloud market — a cloud provider ("someone else's
computer") that is one of the big three (AWS #1, Azure #2, GCP #3), noting GCP is the
smallest but growing fast. He argues you should not learn just one cloud; companies run
multi-cloud, and once you learn one, the foundational concepts (VMs, networking,
storage, Kubernetes) transfer across all three. He gives three reasons to make GCP your
first: a less popular/rarer skill is more valuable, multi-cloud demand, and it is free
to learn.

The demo walks through Google's Cloud Engineer learning path leading to the Associate
Cloud Engineer certification, and shows how the path is broken into "quests" and skill
badges as milestones. Signing up is free for 30 days (no credit card), giving a
subscription that lets you earn as many badges as you can. He demonstrates the "Create
and Manage Cloud Resources" quest — launching a hands-on lab in real GCP (Cloud Shell,
configuring an Nginx web server, a "Check my progress" grader, then a challenge lab that
makes you configure Kubernetes and a load balancer within an hour, unassisted). Sponsor:
Manscaped (promo code "networkchuck").

## Key claims (dated — GCP / cloud-learning concepts, 2021-11-04)

- Google Cloud is projected to be one of the highest-paying skills in 2022, occupying the
  top two spots (ahead of AWS) on a skills/salary ranking Chuck shows on screen.
  (paraphrase)
- The cloud is "someone else's computer" — someone else's servers elsewhere that you rent
  (in GCP's case, from Google) instead of buying your own server, likened to renting a
  car. (paraphrase)
- Google Cloud Platform (commonly abbreviated GCP) is one of the three biggest cloud
  providers — four if you count China. (paraphrase)
- Market ranking: AWS (Amazon Web Services) is #1, the "heavyweight champion" go-to
  provider; Microsoft Azure is a distant #2; GCP is #3, the smallest of the three.
  (paraphrase)
- GCP is growing fast — Chuck cites its IaaS revenue increasing by 66% in 2020 — and
  appears on the Gartner Magic Quadrant. (paraphrase)
- You should not learn only one cloud provider; many companies now run **multi-cloud**,
  splitting workloads across AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud. (paraphrase)
- Once you learn one cloud, picking up the others is comparatively easy because
  foundational concepts — virtual machines, networking, storage, even Kubernetes — are
  remarkably similar across the three clouds. (paraphrase)
- Chuck's own path: Azure was his first cloud (he got certified and taught a course on
  it), which made AWS and then Google Cloud much easier to pick up. (paraphrase)
- Three reasons to make GCP your first cloud: (1) a less popular skill makes you more
  valuable because rarity equals scarcity equals more money; (2) multi-cloud demand —
  even an AWS shop may expand into GCP and need people with GCP skills; (3) it is free to
  learn. (paraphrase)
- Google has its own certification program like AWS and Azure. Associate-level examples:
  AWS Certified Solutions Architect – Associate, Azure Administrator Associate, and
  Google's Associate Cloud Engineer. (paraphrase)
- Certifications are not free — you can find free training, but you must pay to sit the
  exam. (paraphrase)
- What is unique to Google (per Chuck): along the certification path they let you earn
  **skill badges** for individual skills, as milestones you can show off, and those skill
  badges can be earned for free. (paraphrase)
- Why skill badges are valuable: (1) they give motivating milestones on the long, hard
  road to certification; (2) you may only need to verify one specific skill an employer
  wants rather than an entire certification. (paraphrase)
- The offer is free for 30 days via Google's "skills challenge" — free to sign up, no
  credit card required (unlike a normal cloud signup), just an email; you then have 30
  days to earn as many badges as you can. After 30 days it is no longer free but you are
  never charged automatically. (paraphrase)
- Signup flow: fill out the form, receive an email with an access code, click "claim
  offer," and use the code to unlock a 30-day free subscription. (paraphrase)
- Learning is structured into "quests," each ending in a challenge lab that earns a
  badge. A recommended starting badge is **Create and Manage Cloud Resources** (the one
  Chuck earned). (paraphrase)
- The labs use real Google Cloud, not a simulation: normally labs cost tokens/money, but
  the subscription covers them; you log into a provided GCP account (username/password
  supplied) and work in the real console. (paraphrase)
- The Create-and-Manage-Cloud-Resources quest has you open Cloud Shell, use gcloud,
  create a virtual machine, configure an Nginx web server, and set up a Kubernetes
  cluster with networking and load balancers — then "Check my progress" actually inspects
  your project to verify you did it. (paraphrase)
- The final challenge lab does not show you how — it just tells you to configure
  Kubernetes and a load balancer and gives you one hour to complete it, then verifies.
  Chuck says it "kicked my butt" and took a few tries. (paraphrase)
- Chuck knows of no other vendor that gives free access to hands-on labs that verify your
  skills with a badge this way. (paraphrase)
- After 30 days you can buy individual credits or a subscription; the learning process
  and the badges earned during the trial were free. (paraphrase)
