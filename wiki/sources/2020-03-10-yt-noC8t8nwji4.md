# the UniFi Dream Machine Pro....the nerdiest home router

## Summary

Chuck reviews the Ubiquiti UniFi Dream Machine Pro (UDM-Pro), a rack-mount all-in-one gateway/security appliance/UniFi controller, by installing it in his own home network. The video is the "big brother" companion to his earlier review of the (consumer-style) UniFi Dream Machine — see [[2020-02-16-yt-BezoNUflqXo]] — and repeatedly compares the two.

Setup was rocky at first (a boot failure on first power-up) but recovered after a factory reset; from there he found configuration nearly identical to the Dream Machine and praised the phone-based setup. Because he was migrating from an existing UniFi network running a USG Pro, he simply backed up that configuration and restored it onto the UDM-Pro, effectively plug-and-play. He highlights three things that make the UDM-Pro appealing to nerds/businesses: (1) threat management (firewall + IDS/IPS with high inspected-traffic throughput), (2) built-in UniFi Protect surveillance (requires an optional/user-supplied hard drive and PoE cameras), and (3) the little front touchscreen display. He notes what it lacks versus the Dream Machine (no built-in access point). Verdict: aimed at small-to-medium businesses and nerdy home users, with a $379 price tag he considers a strong value. The video is sponsored by NordLocker (file-encryption segment).

## Key claims (dated — review + audience)

All dated 2020-03-10, paraphrased from Chuck unless quoted below.

- The UDM-Pro is positioned as the best router for a small-to-medium-size business, and appealing to home users who are nerds.
- First power-up gave a "boot failed" error; a quick Google + factory reset fixed it and it booted fine afterward.
- Setup is almost identical to the (smaller) Dream Machine and was fast via the phone app; he rates ease of setup very highly.
- He migrated from an existing UniFi network on a USG Pro by backing up its config and uploading it to the UDM-Pro — near plug-and-play.
- Standout feature #1 is threat management: firewall plus intrusion prevention/detection (IPS/IDS) that inspects all traffic for attacks.
- IPS/IDS normally slows networks because inspection is CPU/RAM-intensive; on the UDM-Pro it's largely a non-issue.
- Ubiquiti advertises ~3.5 Gbps of inspected throughput, but at time of filming the (beta) threat-management UI reported his max throughput as 1 Gbps — he flags this as possibly a software bug and asks Ubiquiti about it.
- Practically it doesn't matter to him because he has gigabit internet (1 Gbps is his cap anyway); he enabled every feature and tested it against "bad stuff," which it caught and blocked.
- By contrast the plain Dream Machine does only ~850 Mbps of inspected traffic — enough to throttle good home internet.
- Standout feature #2 is built-in UniFi Protect surveillance software (a router with surveillance built in); it needs an optional hard drive (not included, which annoyed him) plus purchased PoE cameras.
- It shares all the Dream Machine's features (built-in UniFi controller, security features).
- Built-in switch: 8 ports (vs. 4 on the Dream Machine) plus two 10-gig SFP+ ports, and WAN failover — features the Dream Machine lacks — marking it as business-oriented.
- Standout feature #3 (nerd appeal) is the small front touchscreen display; he admits he'll never actually use it but appreciates it.
- Biggest potential dealbreaker: no built-in access point, so you need existing/separate Wi-Fi APs (not an issue for him since he already had them).
- Form factor is rack-mounted — more powerful and feature-rich but not as "sexy/pretty" on a desk as the Dream Machine.
- Price is $379, which he considers a good value given the feature set, versus "crazy gamer routers" that promise fast Wi-Fi but do nothing close to what the UDM-Pro does.
- Caveats: he hadn't tried advanced use like dynamic routing and hadn't accessed a command line; UniFi OS differs from the USG Pro / EdgeRouter OS, so how limited advanced users are is unclear to him.
- Audience/verdict: small branch office → the Dream Machine (for its built-in AP) is probably better; want Pro features (WAN failover, 10-gig SFP+, built-in surveillance) → get the UDM-Pro; also great for nerdy home users like him. It earns his "seal of approval."
