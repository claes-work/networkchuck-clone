# Fighting IMPOSTER SYNDROME in Information Technology - Network Engineer | System Engineer | CCNA

## Summary

Chuck opens with a definition of imposter syndrome — the persistent internalized fear
of being exposed as a fraud, of feeling you only got where you are by tricking people —
and notes it hits IT especially hard. He frames the video as reassurance ("you're not
alone") and brings on two family members from his "IT dynasty": his brother Cameron, a
newly-started (roughly three months) network admin, age 21; and his dad ("the virtual
Chuck"), an IT manager of 25+ years who runs a system-admin team, is a VMware expert,
and holds his CCP. The three sit at different career stages and each still deals with
imposter syndrome — the point being that it never fully goes away.

The conversation surfaces the specific triggers at each level: Cameron feels it as the
young new guy surrounded by 20-year veterans; the dad feels it in reverse, having to
keep up with the young talent coming in and staying current in a field where automation
threatens jobs; Chuck feels the fear of being "caught" fiddling at the command line or
searching Google. They share coping tactics: constantly refreshing knowledge
(re-listening to CBT Nuggets courses, reviewing certification notes), leaning on the
ability to research and figure things out as the real core of IT work, getting certified
to build confidence, joining user groups (VMware VMUG, the DFW Cisco Energy Group) to
discover that even people you think are smarter feel the same way, and reframing
competitiveness into being a team. A recurring reassurance: nobody can know everything —
the dad relays his own boss saying they don't hire people assuming they know everything.
Chuck cites that even experts fail exams (Kevin Wallace failing the CCNP ROUTE test he
wrote the book on; Jeremy Cioara admitting he might fail the CCNA), and closes: if
you're not feeling stupid at least some of the time, you're not surrounding yourself
with enough smart people. (The video is also Day 5 of a certification-training
giveaway.)

## Key claims (dated)

All dated 2018-12-16 unless noted; all paraphrase.

- Imposter syndrome is a psychological pattern of doubting your accomplishments and
  fearing being exposed as a fraud, and it affects many people, especially in IT.
- All three family members — at early-career, mid-career, and 25+-year-manager stages —
  still experience imposter syndrome; it does not simply go away with seniority.
- Cameron (21, ~3 months as a network admin) experiences it as the new, young person
  among long-tenured colleagues; the dad experiences it in reverse, needing to keep up
  with incoming younger talent.
- Staying current is hard because technology constantly changes and automation
  (network/system automation) creates a background fear of being automated out of a job.
- Chuck's dad copes by repeatedly re-listening to CBT Nuggets courses (often in the car)
  and periodically re-reviewing the notes he took while certifying, because forgetting
  what he learned is a real fear.
- A key reframe: most of IT is the ability to find things out; relying on your research
  and problem-solving ability matters more than memorizing everything.
- The dad's boss reassured the team that it is impossible to know everything and they do
  not hire people assuming they do — which is itself reassuring.
- Certifications are, to Chuck, a source of the confidence needed to overcome imposter
  syndrome, even when you already know how to do the work.
- Feeling stagnant — doing the same thing repeatedly without learning — feeds imposter
  syndrome; the antidote is to keep learning and keep going (pursue a cert, keep
  refreshing knowledge).
- Joining user groups (VMware VMUG; the DFW Cisco Energy Group, first Wednesday monthly
  at the Cisco Richardson campus) helps you sharpen skills with peers and realize others
  feel the same way.
- Even experts fail hard exams: Kevin Wallace reportedly failed the CCNP ROUTE test
  despite writing the Cisco Press book on it, and Jeremy Cioara said he might fail the
  CCNA — showing these exams are hard and no one knows everything.
- Test-taking is a distinct mindset (knowing how questions are phrased) separate from
  real-world application, which is why passing an exam and doing the job draw on
  different skills.
- Chuck's own Facebook study group had just passed 10,000 members as of this video.
