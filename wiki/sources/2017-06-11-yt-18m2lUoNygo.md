# Am I Smart Enough to Be a Network Engineer? - CCNA | CCNP Study

## Summary
Chuck Keith gives a candid, encouragement-focused talk about the fear that many aspiring IT professionals share: the worry that they aren't smart enough to become (or to be) a network engineer. Drawing on his own experience of moving from the help desk toward the CCNA/CCNP path, he names the misconceptions and self-doubts he battled and then works through a series of "reality checks" to dismantle them. The core message is that perseverance and drive — not raw brainpower or math genius — are what separate successful engineers from everyone else. The video is sponsored by INE (Internetwork Experts) IT training.

## Key claims
- (2017-06-11) Chuck says he personally struggled — and still sometimes struggles — with the fear that he isn't smart enough to be a network engineer; he had this fear badly when studying for his CCNA.
- (2017-06-11) He splits the fear into two: (1) the fear of *becoming* a network engineer, and (2) the fear of *being* one and feeling like you've just been "faking it." This video focuses mainly on the first.
- (2017-06-11) While on the help desk taking repetitive calls (printer spool resets, reboot requests), he grew bored and wanted more money for his family, and looked over at the network engineers doing "cool stuff."
- (2017-06-11) He bought into the misconception that any IT specialization beyond simple help desk required being a math/science "whiz" — something that never came naturally to him in high school.
- (2017-06-11) Reality check 1: Comparing yourself (an inexperienced help desk tech) to an established network engineer is unfair — every talented engineer started from scratch and had to learn subnetting from chapter one.
- (2017-06-11) Reality check 2: He wrongly believed network engineers were "beyond Google." In fact, knowing how to search Google skillfully is a large part of being a really good network engineer; a good engineer is marked not by how much they know but by knowing how to find the information they need.
- (2017-06-11) Not knowing something is a normal, everyday part of the job; you learn something new every day, and to learn you first have to not know.
- (2017-06-11) Reality check 3 (logic): You have nothing to lose by trying. Best case you become a successful engineer; worst case you fail but still pick up skills you can apply elsewhere. So just try.
- (2017-06-11) Second key fear: even after passing the CCNA, worrying you're only "book smart," can memorize/regurgitate facts, but can't actually troubleshoot or design real networks under pressure.
- (2017-06-11) Reality check (rebuttal): If you're smart enough to be on the help desk, you're smart enough to be a network engineer — the same technical aptitude transitions; help desk troubleshooting is "just the same thing on a larger scale."
- (2017-06-11) He acknowledges true "wizard" engineers / network architects exist who ooze talent, but stresses not every job needs a wizard, and you don't have to be the best, smartest, or fastest to do a solid job and make good money.
- (2017-06-11) Required traits: perseverance, drive, and commitment to constant learning; the prerequisite to doing well in IT is always learning (he plugs subscriptions like INE, CBT, Pluralsight).
- (2017-06-11) He shares his own failure record: failed his switch test once and his route test twice, but kept going — "if I can do it, you can do it."
- (2017-06-11) Key takeaways: (1) Don't compare yourself to others — compete only against who you were yesterday; (2) everyone's different (some love design, others troubleshooting, voice, wireless, routing/switching, security); (3) if you're watching this video seeking encouragement, you already have the drive, which is the first step.
- (2017-06-11) He found his old A+ certification and cites it as the credential that "fueled the fire" and opened the door for him.
