# the hacker's roadmap (how to get started in IT in 2023)

## Summary

Chuck Keith lays out his **canonical 2023 step-by-step roadmap** for breaking into IT
in general and cybersecurity/hacking specifically. The path has **eight parts**: the
first five are foundational and apply to *everyone* in IT regardless of specialization;
the last three are hacking/cybersecurity-specific. For each part he recommends a
certification (mainly for market value on a resume), notes the knowledge it delivers,
gives exam prices, and points to training resources (his sponsor IT Pro TV, his own
free YouTube courses, and free options like Jeremy's IT Lab).

The recurring thesis: **build a solid foundation first** — don't jump straight to
hacking — but also **just start somewhere right now** and get a job as fast as possible,
because real-world on-the-job experience beats every certification. Certifications are
about (1) knowledge and (2) marketability to hiring managers, but you can gain the
knowledge without sitting the (expensive) exams. Coding and CTF hacking practice should
actually start *early* in the journey even though he lists them at parts 5 and 6.
Video is sponsored by IT Pro TV and closes with a five-question A+ practice quiz using
Boson ExSim.

## Key claims (dated — this is Chuck's *2023-01-05* version of the roadmap)

The eight-part path (as of 2023-01-05):

- **Part 0 — Coffee.** Half-joking framing device: everything in IT requires coffee
  because you'll be studying a lot. (Today's cup: Never Chuck Coffee "default route.")
- **Part 1 — Foundations: CompTIA A+.** For anyone starting from zero or transitioning
  from another job. It's a "sample platter" of IT (two exams covering virtualization,
  cloud, networking, troubleshooting, OS, security, software troubleshooting), starts
  from no prior knowledge, and helps land that likely-first job (help desk / technical
  support). His informal polls favored A+ as the entry cert (YouTube 48,000 votes / 58%;
  Twitter 5,000+ votes / 40%; LinkedIn 4,000 votes / 50%). Two exams at **$249 each**.
  You can skip the exam and just gain the knowledge. The Google IT Support cert has good
  knowledge but is less marketable. A+ was Chuck's own first IT certification.
- **Part 2 — Security: CompTIA Security+.** "Probably one of the more valuable ones you
  get" — needed no matter what area of IT you go into. Foundational security knowledge
  plus resume gold; carries DoD 8570 compliance so it's required/checkbox for many jobs
  (especially government). One exam, **$392**. Should be your first security cert.
- **Part 3 — Networking: CompTIA Network+ OR Cisco CCNA.** Networking is a required
  skill, "kind of a superpower," especially for cybersecurity ("how are you gonna hack
  something you don't understand?"). Network+ is vendor-neutral (**$358–359**); CCNA is
  the **gold standard**, Cisco-focused, teaches you to actually configure networks, and
  holds more resume weight (**$300** but likely a harder exam). Chuck's personal pick is
  the CCNA. Note: CCNA is cheaper than Network+.
- **Part 4 — Linux.** Everyone needs to learn Linux; literally everything in IT
  (networking, coding, cloud, hacking) requires some Linux. Certification route =
  Linux+ (fun, deep, but skippable — it's more about knowledge than resume weight,
  unlike A+/Net+/CCNA/Security+). For most starting out, a "getting started" course /
  his *Linux for Hackers* playlist is enough. Goal: "learn enough to be dangerous,"
  not to become a Linux expert.
- **Part 5 — Coding.** Pick up a programming language and start playing — he recommends
  **Python** (easier to start) or **Golang** (newer, faster up-and-comer). He says he
  dislikes putting coding at #5 because it should really start *way earlier* — treat it
  like the "bathroom break" on the road trip: spend ~1–2 days a week on a little Python.
  No prerequisites needed; tons of free resources (his YouTube Python course, Code
  Academy, IT Pro TV). He regrets not learning Python until later in his own career.
- **Part 6 — Just start hacking (CTFs).** Also should start early. CTF = Capture The
  Flag: practice hacking real vulnerable targets. Free platforms named: **PicoCTF,
  TryHackMe, Hack The Box**. Do a few CTFs a week; they (1) show you what hacking is
  actually like / whether you even enjoy it, and (2) expose the gaps in your foundational
  knowledge, pushing you back to the basics.
- **Part 7 — Hacking certifications: CompTIA PenTest+ OR eJPT (from INE).** Pick one,
  not both; roughly the same knowledge — goal is to make you a **junior** penetration
  tester (hacking is advanced; it's a combination of all prior foundational skills).
  PenTest+ = easier to find training (IT Pro TV, TryHackMe lab pack), **~$392** exam
  voucher. eJPT = cheaper at **$249**, but official training is only through INE (which
  acquired eLearnSecurity, the eJPT's creator).
- **Part 8 — OSCP (Offensive Security Certified Professional) — "stop here."** Hard,
  "it'll kick your butt"; don't attempt it until after PenTest+ or eJPT. It tells the
  world you know a thing or two about hacking (not that you're an expert — many call it
  just the beginning). After OSCP, **stop collecting certs** — from here it's all about
  on-the-job experience. (Chuck notes he hasn't done the OSCP himself yet.)

Cross-cutting principles (2023-01-05):
- Parts 1–5 apply to everyone in IT; parts 6–8 are cybersecurity/hacking-specific.
- The point of the path is to **get a job** — it doesn't matter where on the path you
  land it (many land a help desk job midway through the A+). Start applying now even
  with no certs; most help desk jobs mainly want people skills.
- Certs deliver two things: **knowledge** and **marketability** to hiring managers. You
  can gain all the knowledge for free online; paid/structured training is just a better,
  higher-quality experience.
- **Real-world experience beats every certification.** Chuck says he often didn't need
  the certs because he learned skills on the job and put those on his resume (e.g., he
  doesn't hold Linux+ but configured real Linux servers — which is more valuable).
- He includes a quiz gimmick: five A+ practice questions (via Boson ExSim) so viewers
  can gauge whether they can skip a part; promises a bonus video quizzing Security+,
  Network+, and CCNA.
