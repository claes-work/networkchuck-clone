# I Tried running in 3D printed shoes

## Summary

A personal maker/hobby video, largely outside the core IT taxonomy, in which Chuck
Keith explores the Bamboo Labs H2D 3D printer (a sponsored unit) and its full feature
set, then attempts to run five miles in shoes he 3D printed with flexible TPU filament.
The video is framed as a hands-on "what can this thing do" tour rather than a technical
review — Chuck explicitly disclaims being a 3D-printing YouTuber. He walks through the
printer's size, dual-nozzle printing, laser engraving/cutting modules, a vinyl/pen
cutting module, and the trial-and-error of printing flexible shoes. The five-mile run
is played for comedy: the shoes physically survive but the experience is painful. Chuck
frames the whole thing as a proof of concept for what 3D printing could eventually do
(e.g., prosthetics). The video closes with his signature end-of-video prayer for his
audience.

## Key claims

- (2026-02-06) The video is a maker/hobby project: printing shoes and running five miles
  in them, plus a tour of the Bamboo Labs H2D printer's capabilities. Not a technical/IT
  topic and not a comprehensive review — Chuck says so directly. (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-06, self-reported) Chuck started 3D printing on a Creality Ender 3, which he
  "kind of hated" — it took forever to build and to print, but it hooked him on the idea.
  (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-06, self-reported) Looking for "the Apple of 3D printers," he found Bamboo
  Labs and bought a Bamboo Lab X1 Carbon himself, which he calls a game changer. Bamboo
  Labs later sent him additional printers including the H2D featured here. (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-06) The video is sponsored by Bamboo Labs, who provided the H2D printer,
  filament, laser/cutting modules, ventilator, and materials. Chuck discloses this
  plainly and jokes he "tricked them into thinking I'm a 3D printing influencer."
  (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-06) The H2D specs he highlights: ~325x320x325 build volume (his biggest
  printer), single- and dual-nozzle printing, ~1000 mm/s nozzle speed (double his X1
  Carbon), 350 °C max hot end, and active chamber heating to 65 °C for drying filament.
  (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-06) The printer's non-printing features: 10W and 40W laser modules for
  engraving/cutting (with a smoke ventilator and a physical kill switch), and a cutting
  module for vinyl and leather that also holds a pen to draw/autograph. (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-06) TPU (flexible filament) required extensive troubleshooting: lowering
  nozzle temp to ~215 °C to stop bubbling, feeding it manually via the bypass port,
  placing the printer on the floor so filament fed straight in, and drying the TPU in
  the heated chamber after a failed print. Root cause of one failure: skipping the
  documentation — "don't skip reading the instructions." (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-06, self-reported) Chuck delegates hands-on build/troubleshooting work to his
  team — "I made my team do it. I have a team" — while he sips coffee. Recurring theme
  of his running a production team. (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-06, self-reported) Personal life detail: he recently got a new pug named Mochi
  and laser-engraved a dog tag for him. (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-06, self-reported) He is an amateur runner: he ran the full five miles in the
  ill-fitting cork-soled shoes in the middle of Texas summer heat, got a rock stuck in
  the shoe, finished in significant pain, and expected blisters and jacked-up knees for
  ~two weeks. He jokes "Take that Mr. Beast." (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-06) Verdict: the shoes survived intact but did not protect his feet; the run
  "sucked." He concludes 3D-printed running shoes aren't ready, but sees the exercise as
  a proof of concept, speculating about medical applications like prosthetics.
  (paraphrase)
- (2026-02-06, self-reported) Filament types he name-checks: PLA (rigid, incl. glow-in-
  the-dark), PETG (incl. translucent), PAHT-CF (very strong, used for Sony camera
  covers), and TPU (flexible, used for the shoes). (paraphrase)
