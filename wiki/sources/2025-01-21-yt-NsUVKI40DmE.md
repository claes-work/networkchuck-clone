# A history of time in computers

## Summary

A rapid 60-second history of timekeeping, ending on how computers synchronize time. Chuck
walks from sundials and star tracking, to water clocks (400 BC), mechanical weight-driven
clocks (13th century), pendulum clocks (1656), Greenwich Mean Time for British Railways
(1847), global time zones (1884), quartz clocks (1927), and atomic clocks (1967) accurate to
the nanosecond. He explains that computers' quartz crystals (RTC) still drifted relative to
each other, which is why NTP (Network Time Protocol) was invented to synchronize time across
the internet — and teases PTP as the next step from millisecond to nanosecond accuracy.

## Key points

- Sundials, star tracking, water clocks (400 BC), mechanical clocks (13th c.), and pendulum clocks (1656) each improved timekeeping.
- Greenwich Mean Time was introduced by the British Railways in 1847; global time zones came from the 1884 international conference.
- 1927 quartz clocks: an electrocuted quartz crystal vibrates 32,768 times consistently — still used in computer RTCs today.
- 1967 atomic clocks (cesium/CCM, also rubidium) reach nanosecond accuracy — so accurate that leap seconds are added to correct Earth's rotation.
- 1970s: atomic clocks on GPS satellites provide highly accurate time signals globally.
- Computers' quartz crystals drift, so NTP (Network Time Protocol, invented by David Mills) synchronizes time across the internet to within 1–10 milliseconds.
- PTP is the next step, from millisecond to nanosecond accuracy.
