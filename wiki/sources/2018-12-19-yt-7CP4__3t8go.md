# CompTIA or Cisco? - Revisiting CCENT vs Network+ in 2019 | CCNA | MTA | MCSA

## Summary
Chuck explicitly revisits his earlier "CompTIA or Cisco?" video (see [[2017-08-30-yt-EywIAz8fPnY]]), narrowing the comparison to the two entry-level networking certs: the Cisco CCENT (ICND1) versus the CompTIA Network+. He flags up front that his position has softened since last time — previously he "emphatically" told people to skip Network+ and get the CCENT, calling Network+ a no-go, but two things changed: he built the Network+ training on CBT Nuggets (so he went through the full material) and he did fresh research, including a job-board search that surprised him. His 2019 verdict: if you know you want to be a network engineer, CCENT still wins (it's half of the CCNA, cheaper, and ranks better on a resume); but Network+ is now a legitimate, even recommended, choice for people who don't yet know their path or who are heading toward cloud (AWS/Azure/GCP), because it's broad — a "sampler platter" covering SDN, IoT, wireless, cloud, virtualization, SANs, next-gen firewalls, disaster recovery, and security. He also weighs cost (CCENT $165 vs Network+ $319), Indeed job counts, HR keyword-scanning, and how CompTIA/Cisco/Microsoft (A+, Security+, MTA→MCSA) fit into a career path.

> ⚠️ POSITION EVOLVED (2017 → 2019): In the 2017 video Chuck told viewers to skip Network+ entirely and go straight for the vendor cert. In this 2018/2019 revisit he softens: he still prefers CCENT for aspiring network engineers, but now openly recommends Network+ as a good option for people who don't know their path or are heading toward cloud, crediting his change of heart to (a) having built and gone through the Network+ course on CBT Nuggets and (b) fresh job-market research showing Network+ actually returns slightly more Indeed listings than CCENT. The core CCENT-first recommendation is retained; the blanket "Network+ is a no-go" stance is retracted.

## Key claims (dated)
- (2018-12-19) He explicitly revisits his earlier CCENT-vs-Network+ take: last year / the year before he "emphatically said Network Plus, don't get that one, it's a no-go" and called CCENT by far the superior cert. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) His view changed for two reasons: he created the Network+ training on CBT Nuggets (so he went through all the material), and he did more research and was "shocked" by what he found. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) CCENT = Cisco Certified Entry-Level Network Technician; it covers many industry-standard networking topics that carry over to any vendor, but with a heavy focus on Cisco products and the Cisco CLI. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) Cisco essentially invented networking and most other vendors (Dell, Arista, Juniper) copied it, so Cisco CLI skills and concepts transfer to other vendors' gear. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) CCENT is half of the CCNA (CCENT/ICND1 + ICND2 → CCNA), one of the most popular IT certs; being CCENT-certified means you're one step from the CCNA. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) Network+ is a CompTIA vendor-neutral cert from a nonprofit with no loyalty to any vendor; it's much broader, with more exam topics and more technologies covered than CCENT. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) Topics Network+ covers that CCENT does not: Software Defined Networking, IoT, wireless, cloud, virtual devices/VMs, SANs, next-generation firewalls, gateways, disaster recovery / business continuity, and security — but it doesn't go deep ("like walking through the puddle"). [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) Analogy: Network+ is a "sampler platter" for someone unsure what they want in IT; CCENT is for someone who already knows they want the Cisco networking / network-engineer path. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) On knowledge/topics the two are fairly similar; the deciding factor is the job outlook — how many jobs the cert helps you land. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) On Indeed (US-wide, no location filter, as of that day): CompTIA Network+ returned ~670 jobs vs ~477 for CCENT — Network+ wins on raw numbers, but it's close. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) Searching "CCNA" returned over 10,000 jobs — so CCENT puts you one step from a cert with far more openings. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) He searches by bare cert keyword because HR systems ("HR machines / programs") sift resumes by keywords like CCNA, CCENT, Network+, or Net+. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) The numbers are fuzzy: a bare "network plus" search returned ~319,000 hits, but many listings only matched the word "network" and never mentioned Network+/CompTIA; many jobs also list Network+ OR CCENT interchangeably as an acceptable entry-level networking credential. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) As a hiring manager choosing between a resume with Network+ and one with CCENT, he'd pick CCENT every time, because CCENT signals the candidate is in progress toward the CCNA, whereas the CompTIA brand reads as entry-level and isn't associated with higher-level/admin positions. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) Cost (2018 → 2019): CCENT exam $165, Network+ exam $319 — roughly double. Network+ teaches more but not deeply enough to justify double the price (his opinion). [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) Cost stacks further: CCENT ($165) + ICND2 ($165) = ~$330 for a full CCNA, versus $319 for just the Network+ — so CCNA is far more valuable for about the same cost; purely on cost, CCENT wins every time. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) Cost doesn't matter if you get vouchers (military/school); the cost argument is for people paying out of pocket. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) CompTIA A+ is "killer" for landing a first help-desk job when you know nothing about computers (over 2,000 Indeed jobs for "CompTIA A+"), but if you already have a help-desk job, don't get the A+ — get a higher-level, vendor-specific cert. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) CompTIA Security+ is an excellent cert to have purely for jobs because it's a standard requirement for many DoD/government/contractor roles; example: his father-in-law, an experienced IT pro, didn't need it but had to get it when it became a job requirement. Like all CompTIA certs, it doesn't go deep. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) Among vendor-neutral certs, the only CompTIA certs he'd personally consider getting are A+ and Security+ — plus maybe Network+ if he didn't know which networking path to take. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) Network+ would be a great prerequisite for an AWS or Google Cloud cert, but maybe not the best for a Microsoft path, because Microsoft has its own entry cert: MTA (Microsoft Technology Associate) → multiple MTA exams lead to MCSA (Microsoft Certified Solutions/Systems Associate), which he puts on par with the CCNA. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) The MTA networking specialization doesn't go as in-depth as CCENT and isn't as broad as Network+, but may be enough if you're headed to Microsoft while wanting some networking; if you'll have to touch Cisco, Arista, Dell, Juniper gear, get your CCNA. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) The Google IT Support Professional cert is a neat idea but returned only ~11 Indeed jobs — half the battle of getting an IT job is just having enough resume keywords to get scanned and surfaced. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) Career-planning method: search LinkedIn/Indeed for the job you aspire to (not one you already qualify for), note the certs listed (e.g., CCNP, VCP, CISSP), and reverse-engineer your cert plan to match those job descriptions as closely as possible — that's how the resume-matching machines work. [paraphrase]
- (2018-12-19) Net summary: if you know you want to be a network engineer, get CCENT → CCNA; if you just need a taste of everything and don't know your path, Network+ is good — and if you get Network+ first, a later CCNA will be a bit easier because you'll already have the networking knowledge. [paraphrase]
