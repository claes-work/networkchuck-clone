# everyone is putting AI in schools......

## Summary

Chuck reacts to Anthropic's announcement of **Claude for Education** and uses it as a
springboard for his broader stance on AI in schools. He praises the product's
"learning mode," which uses **Socratic questioning** — asking students *why* and probing
their evidence — instead of just handing over answers, because he believes critical
thinking is exactly the skill that matters in the age of AI. He explains how Claude is
being distributed via full-campus access agreements (Northeastern, LSE, Champlain
College) and integrations with platforms like Canvas (Instructure) and Internet2, and
notes it's essentially only accessible through an institution buying the plan.

His core argument: it does not matter whether adults approve of AI in education —
students are already using it universally, so the real question is how to shape that
use. He leans heavily on Wharton professor **Ethan Mollick**, citing the concept of
"illusory knowledge" (students *feel* like they're learning while AI actually undermines
it), a Turkish high-school study where GPT-4 raised homework scores but lowered final-exam
performance, and the failure of AI detection. Chuck's conclusion is that fighting AI is
futile; the goal must be AI that *encourages* thinking rather than *replaces* it. He then
shows his own DIY alternative: because he homeschools his kids and can't get Claude for
Education, he self-hosts Open WebUI and loads Mollick's tutoring prompt into a locked-down
"AI Tutor" model his kids use. He closes cautiously optimistic — pro-AI-for-learning, but
insisting users guard against illusory knowledge.
