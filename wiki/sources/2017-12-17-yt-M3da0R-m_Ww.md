# Should I Get the CCNP After the CCNA?

## Summary
Chuck answers a common viewer question: after finishing the CCNA Routing & Switching, should you go for the CCNP Routing & Switching, or divert straight into a specialty track (security, collaboration, data center, wireless)? His core stance is that Routing & Switching is the backbone of every Cisco track, so the CCNP R&S is the default choice for anyone who wants to stay well-rounded and doesn't yet have a role pulling them toward a specialty. He notes that Cisco recently changed the path so you can now branch from CCENT/ICND1 directly into other CCNA tracks, which he initially thought was crazy given how much foundational R&S knowledge every role needs.

## Key claims
- (2017-12-17) After the CCNA Routing & Switching, the CCNP Routing & Switching is Chuck's default recommendation for anyone without a specialty pulling them elsewhere, because it leaves you well-rounded and ready for any specialization.
- (2017-12-17) Chuck endorses advice he got from Jeremy Cioara (CBT Nuggets) that CCNP R&S gives the routing/switching foundation you need before going down any other track.
- (2017-12-17) Chuck says you should always complete the full CCNA Routing & Switching first — not jump from CCENT straight into CCNA security or data center.
- (2017-12-17) Cisco recently changed the certification path so you can go from CCENT/ICND1 directly into CCNA Security, Collaboration, or Wireless; previously you had to complete CCNA R&S first before diverting.
- (2017-12-17) The right path is situational: if your current role is pushing you toward voice/collaboration or security, it can make more sense to go that specialty route after CCNA R&S, especially at a value-added reseller (Cisco partner) that rewards specialization.
- (2017-12-17) At smaller companies (under ~500 people / ~100 users) you are a jack-of-all-trades; at larger companies and VARs roles are siloed (the voice guy, the security guy, the data center/Nexus guy, the wireless guy).
- (2017-12-17) Taking a break after CCNA R&S to learn Python is a smart alternative, especially if your company is moving heavily toward automation.
- (2017-12-17) Collaboration is Chuck's own specialty; he jokes most network engineers dislike collaboration.
