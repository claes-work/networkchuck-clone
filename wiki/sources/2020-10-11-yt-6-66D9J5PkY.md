# Data Center NETWORKS (what do they look like??) // FREE CCNA // EP 7

## Summary

Episode 7 of NetworkChuck's FREE CCNA course explains how data center networks are
designed and why they differ from campus/office networks. Chuck opens by conveying his
awe of data centers ("felt like I was at Disneyland") and defines a data center as the
place where essentially any resource accessed over a network lives — rows of racks
holding servers, routers, and switches.

He walks through the options a company has for hosting: (1) running its own data center,
which can be as small as one rack in a room or as large as a warehouse; (2) renting rack
space in a colocation data center that provides redundant power, redundant cooling, and
internet; or (3) using the cloud (AWS, Google Cloud, Azure), which is still a data center
but someone else's. Many companies run a combination (hybrid cloud).

The technical core contrasts two designs. The **traditional three-tier (campus) design**
uses top-of-rack (ToR) / access switches, a distribution/aggregation layer, and a core
layer of beefy multi-chassis switches, with redundancy at each layer. This design
prioritizes **north-south traffic** (server ↔ internet/user). The problem: after
virtualization, servers increasingly need to talk to other servers across racks —
**east-west traffic** — which grew to roughly 70–80% of data center traffic. In three-tier,
server-to-server communication can take three or more hops (up to the core and back down),
which is unpredictable and too slow. Spanning tree also blocks redundant links to prevent
loops, wasting bandwidth.

The solution is the **spine-leaf design** (also called a Clos design). ToR switches are
renamed **leaf switches** (still the access layer for their rack's servers); the middle
layer becomes **spine switches** (think "backbone"). Every leaf connects to every spine in
a full mesh, but spines do NOT connect to each other and leaves do NOT connect to each
other. This guarantees any server-to-server path is exactly **two hops** — predictable and
reliable. Spines don't need to be massive multi-chassis beasts because they share/load-
balance the traffic among themselves. Chuck notes leaf-to-spine links are typically
**Layer 3** (routed), not Layer 2, which avoids spanning-tree blocking and lets all links
load-balance at full bandwidth. He shows Cisco **Nexus** switches (the data center line, vs.
Catalyst for campus) as example hardware, and mentions the spine-leaf fabric is just the
**underlay**, on top of which an **overlay** network (e.g., Cisco ACI automation) is built —
to be covered later. The episode closes with two Boson ExSim practice-exam questions on
Cisco ACI / leaf-node connectivity.

## Key claims (dated — the concepts)

All dated 2020-10-11 (source publication date).

- A data center is where essentially any resource accessed over the internet or a network
  lives; it is rows of racks holding servers, routers, and switches.
- Companies obtain data center capacity three ways: own data center (from one rack to a
  full warehouse), rented rack space in a colocation facility, or the cloud (AWS, Google
  Cloud, Azure). A colo provides redundant cooling, redundant power, and internet. Many
  companies run a combination = hybrid cloud.
- If a company is fully cloud (rare), it doesn't design the data center network — the
  provider handles it. Network engineers design the network when the company owns a data
  center or rents rack space.
- **Top-of-rack (ToR) switches** sit at the top of each rack; servers in the rack connect to
  them. "ToR" stands for top of rack (nothing to do with the dark web). They are also the
  **access layer**.
- Traditional data center design used the **three-tier / campus model**: access (ToR) →
  distribution/aggregation → core, with redundant connections and (for redundancy) dual
  core switches — big multi-chassis "beast" switches that cost a lot.
- **North-south traffic** = traffic to/from the internet/user (up and down through core →
  distribution → access → server). The old design prioritized this.
- **East-west traffic** = server-to-server (horizontal) traffic within the data center.
  Virtualization made data centers distributed, so servers across racks now need to talk to
  each other. East-west grew to ~70–80% of data center traffic and has the biggest impact on
  performance.
- In three-tier, east-west server-to-server communication can take three or more hops (ToR
  → distribution → core → distribution → ToR), which is unpredictable and too slow for
  modern needs.
- Spanning tree, a Layer 2 loop-prevention mechanism, blocks one of each pair of redundant
  links (keeping only one active) to prevent switching loops — which wastes bandwidth in the
  three-tier design.
- **Spine-leaf design** (a.k.a. **Clos design**) is today's standard for data center networks
  (used by Facebook, Google, etc., with modifications). ToR switches become **leaf switches**
  (still the access layer); the middle layer becomes **spine switches**.
- "Spine" = backbone; like the body's spine it supports the network and handles the most
  bandwidth/packets. But individual spine switches need only be moderately powerful, not
  backbone-caliber multi-chassis beasts, because they share the load among themselves.
- Every leaf connects to every spine in a **full mesh**; spines do NOT connect to spines and
  leaves do NOT connect to leaves. Traffic is load-balanced equally across the spines.
- Result: any server-to-server communication is a **maximum of two hops** — always, every
  time — making it predictable and reliable (vs. three-plus unpredictable hops before).
  Downside: it's a cabling-heavy design (use fiber in a data center).
- Leaf-to-spine links are most often **Layer 3** (routed, IP), not Layer 2 (MAC). This means
  leaf and spine switches are Layer 3 / multi-layer switches. Two benefits: (1) no links are
  blocked by spanning tree, and (2) routing lets you load-balance across all links and use
  full bandwidth.
- Cisco **Nexus** switches are purpose-built for the data center (Catalyst switches are for
  campus/access-distribution-core). Example throughput cited: a 2RU Nexus at 12.84 Tbps and
  a 1RU 9332C at 6.4 Tbps; the 9300 series can act as either leaf or spine depending on
  network size (e.g., 30,000–50,000 servers needs bigger gear).
- Spine-leaf is just the **underlay** (foundation network). On top of it, big players build an
  **overlay** network doing network automation (e.g., Cisco ACI) — covered later in the course.
- Chuck notes spine-leaf is starting to be used for campus networks too, not just data
  centers.
