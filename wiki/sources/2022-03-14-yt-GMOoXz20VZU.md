# my kids built a HACKING computer!! (i almost died)

## Summary

A family/tech video in which Chuck guides two of his young daughters (unnamed here; family
detail is **self-reported**) through building their own gaming/"hacking" PCs from scratch,
framed as a race — the fastest builder wins 10,000 Robux. The bulk of the video is an
accessible, beginner-level PC-build walkthrough (motherboard, CPU, RAM, storage, GPU, PSU,
staging the build, posting to BIOS) taught to children, intercut with comedic chaos. Despite
the "hacking computer" premise, the machines run Windows at the end; Chuck states the girls
will use them to learn Linux, Python, and hacking, and to do school on. The framing bit about
"stealing" parts is a joke — they actually **bought** two entire prebuilt gaming PCs from
Walmart solely to harvest the GPUs inside, because a standalone graphics card was impossible
to source during the 2021 GPU shortage. The build was actually done in January 2021; the video
released March 2022. The closing message is Chuck's core ethos: anyone who wants to get into
IT should build a computer, and if you have kids, build one *with* them — frustrating but
rewarding, and it teaches you a huge amount.

## Key claims (paraphrase unless quoted)

- **[2022-03-14]** The best way to get started in IT is to build a computer yourself — you
  learn an enormous amount from the process (and end up with a gaming machine). Chuck says he
  would do the whole ordeal again.
- **[2022-03-14]** Chuck's "build it with your kids" pitch: if you have kids, build a PC with
  them — it will be frustrating and hard, but it's rewarding and gets them into tech early.
- **[2022-03-14]** The daughters' machines are positioned as learning tools, not just gaming
  rigs: Chuck says the girls will learn Linux, Python, and how to hack on them, and they now do
  schoolwork on them.
- **[2022-03-14]** PC-build teaching points delivered at a beginner level:
  - **Stage the build** — assemble the PC outside its case first to confirm all parts work
    before final assembly.
  - Use a properly grounded **anti-static / ESD wrist strap**, because static can kill
    electronics (the kids kept touching clothes and getting staticky).
  - **Motherboard** — the board everything plugs into (holds RAM, CPU, etc.).
  - **CPU** ("central processing unit") — the brain of the computer; more powerful CPU means a
    more powerful computer. Install carefully: don't touch the pins/circuits on the bottom,
    match the arrow on the CPU corner to the arrow on the socket, don't force it, then lock the
    retention arm.
  - **RAM** ("random access memory") — the computer's fast short-term memory for what you're
    working on right now; more RAM lets you do more at once. Install sticks (DIMMs) in the order
    the motherboard manual specifies, lining up the notch. Chuck bought the girls 32 GB; he runs
    64 GB himself and was using ~20 GB while recording the voiceover.
  - **Storage** — an NVMe SSD (in the M.2 slot) is tiny, very fast, and pricier, used for the OS
    and important apps; paired with a larger HDD for bulk files.
  - **GPU** — hard to source in 2021; installs into a PCIe slot.
  - **PSU** (power supply unit) — delivers power to all the parts.
- **[2022-03-14]** GPU-shortage workaround: with no standalone GPU available at Micro Center,
  Amazon, or Newegg, Chuck bought two entire prebuilt gaming PCs from Walmart just to pull the
  GPUs out — a pair of NVIDIA GeForce GTX 1660 Supers ("okay, but nothing to get excited about").
- **[2022-03-14]** Parts list used: Gigabyte Aorus X570 Elite / MSI MPG X570 Gaming Edge WiFi
  motherboards, AMD Ryzen 5 3600 CPU, 32 GB G.Skill Trident 3200 MHz RAM, 1 TB NVMe SSD + 2 TB
  HDD, 750 W PSU, custom spray-painted case.
- **[2022-03-14]** When the built PC wouldn't power on, there was no case power button attached;
  consulting the motherboard manual, Chuck shorted the two power-switch jumper pins with a
  screwdriver to boot it, and it POSTed on the first try.
- **[2022-03-14]** Competition result: both daughters assembled a PC (placing CPU, applying
  thermal paste, installing the CPU fan, RAM, storage, GPU); the winner finished in about
  5 min 15 s vs. ~7 min 12 s.
