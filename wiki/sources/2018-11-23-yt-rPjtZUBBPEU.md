# What if you forgot EVERYTHING? - Re-Learning IT after MEMORY LOSS w/ Shawn Powers | Linux | CCNA

## Summary

This is a **guest interview**. Chuck (NetworkChuck) sits down with **Shawn Powers**, a
Linux educator (CBT Nuggets trainer, contributor to Linux Journal), who tells the story
of a car accident in **March 1999** that left him with amnesia and destroyed his memory
of everything before that date. The bulk of the narrative — the accident, the amnesia,
losing his IT tech-support job, his family's hardship, and re-learning IT from scratch —
is **Shawn's life story**, not Chuck's.

Shawn recounts dropping out of Michigan Tech in the early-to-mid 1990s after abandoning
electrical engineering to teach himself networking in the computer lab, then working
tech support at a community college. After the accident he could no longer do his job
(he describes agoraphobia and a hard recovery), his pregnant wife had to bus tables, and
they slept on a mattress at his mother-in-law's. He rebuilt an IT career by teaching
himself **Linux** — chosen originally not on philosophy but because it was **free** and
that was all he could afford. His method for learning was simply "just do it": install
an early Red Hat release from floppy disks on his only computer (no VMs existed then) and
learn by doing.

Chuck frames the whole interview as motivation for his audience: leave excuses at the
door, live with gratitude, and start learning Linux today because free resources now make
the barrier far lower than what Shawn faced. Chuck plugs Shawn's CBT Nuggets Linux course
and Linux Journal.

## Key claims

**Attributed to Shawn Powers:**
- (Story set ~1993–1999) Shawn attended Michigan Tech intending to be an electrical
  engineer for the money, but abandoned it during a third-semester Calculus 3 class and
  spent his time in the computer lab teaching himself how networking worked; he eventually
  dropped out. *(paraphrase)*
- (Story ~1993+) He worked tech support at a local community college that had just started
  offering internet service to students, helping people install Trumpet Winsock. *(paraphrase)*
- **In March 1999** a car accident sent his head through the passenger window; he retains
  **no memory of anything before March 1999**, and still had not recovered those memories
  ~20 years later at the time of the interview. *(paraphrase)*
- After the accident he could not do his tech-support job, experienced agoraphobia, his
  pregnant wife went back to bussing tables to pay for food, and they slept on a mattress
  on the floor at his mother-in-law's house. *(paraphrase)*
- His fingers "knew how to type" even though he did not know what to type — some motor
  skills survived while explicit knowledge did not. *(paraphrase)*
- He chose **Linux because it was free** — the only OS he could afford — not initially out
  of an open-source philosophy (though he says he grew to love that). *(paraphrase)*
- He learned Linux by "just doing it": installing an early "Halloween" Red Hat release
  from floppy disks on his only computer, with no VMs available to sandbox it. *(paraphrase)*
- He rebuilt an IT career from near-zero knowledge and became a CBT Nuggets Linux trainer
  and a Linux Journal contributor. *(paraphrase)*

**Attributed to Chuck (NetworkChuck):**
- Key takeaway he draws for viewers: live your life full of gratitude, be thankful for
  what you have, and "leave all your excuses at the door." *(paraphrase, voice data)*
- Learning Linux today is far easier than what Shawn faced — free YouTube videos, courses
  (he plugs Shawn's CBT Nuggets course), and VMs let you sandbox installs so "no one's the
  wiser." *(paraphrase, voice data)*
- Linux admin/engineer roles (per LinkedIn, Dice) are lucrative, and Linux is a rarer,
  harder-to-break-into skill than Windows admin because most people already use Windows
  daily but few run Linux as their main OS. *(paraphrase, voice data)*
- Chuck's own framing: he says he keeps Linux as a VM rather than his main OS. *(paraphrase, voice data)*
- Encouragement to viewers stuck on the help desk / in tech support: you may have fears,
  but you have no excuses — make this video your "wake-up call" and start learning now.
  *(paraphrase, voice data)*
