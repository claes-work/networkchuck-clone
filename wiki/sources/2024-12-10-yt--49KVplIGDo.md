# who is this even for?? (Raspberry Pi 500)

## Summary

Chuck reviews the Raspberry Pi 500 — a keyboard with a Raspberry Pi built inside — plus
the companion Raspberry Pi Monitor, both sent to him (unsponsored) by Raspberry Pi. He
frames the whole video around one question: who is this device actually for? He unboxes
both, compares the Pi 500 against its predecessor the Pi 400, walks through the specs,
and stress-tests the "easy setup" promise by handing the whole kit to his five-year-old
daughter to assemble and set up. His verdict: the Pi 500 is not for tinkerers like him,
but it's great for kids and for newcomers who don't want to flash SD cards or pick an OS
— and the monitor is a genuinely good, cheap, portable display he'd buy for himself. The
video is a review; the NordVPN segment is a mid-roll sponsor read (unrelated to the
hardware).

## Key claims (dated — his take)

All dated 2024-12-10, paraphrased from Chuck.

- The Pi 500 is a keyboard with a Raspberry Pi built in, which he thinks makes it cooler
  than a regular keyboard; the new keyboard feels a lot better to type on than the Pi 400.
- The Pi 500 is essentially a Raspberry Pi 5 in keyboard form: quad-core 64-bit Arm
  Cortex-A76 at 2.4 GHz, versus the Pi 400's Cortex-A72 at 1.8 GHz.
- RAM is the other big upgrade over the 400: 8 GB LPDDR4X (vs the 400's 4 GB LPDDR4),
  double the size and double the speed; LPDDR4X gives improved energy efficiency, better
  heat management, and longer lifespan — things you want in compact computing.
- The Pi 500 ships preloaded with an SD card running Bookworm OS, whereas the 400 "left
  you out in the cold" — the intent is a hand-it-to-a-kid, set-it-up-yourself experience.
- Pricing: Pi 500 base is $90 (vs $70 for the 400 base); the desktop kit is $120 (vs $100
  for the 400 kit) and adds a mouse, a 27 W USB-C power supply, a 2 m micro-HDMI-to-HDMI
  cable, and a beginner's guide. He thinks the extra $30 for the kit is probably not worth
  it, but it makes a great gift — and note the base Pi 500 does not come with a power
  adapter.
- Shared specs with the 400: two micro-HDMI ports (up to 4K/60), two USB 3.0 + one USB
  2.0, gigabit ethernet, micro-SD slot, a 40-pin GPIO header, Wi-Fi 802.11ac, Bluetooth 5.0.
  You still need a separate mouse.
- The Raspberry Pi Monitor is a 15.6-inch 1080p (1920x1080) IPS LCD with anti-glare
  coating, 16.2 million colors, two built-in 1.2 W speakers, comes in red/white or black,
  and costs $100 — which he calls competitive with similar Amazon monitors, with the bonus
  of matching Raspberry Pi branding.
- The monitor can be powered over USB from the Pi 500 (or later Pi models), but USB power
  caps it at 60% brightness and 50% volume; full brightness/volume needs a separate 27 W
  power supply. He confirmed this on camera — even his external supply only hit 50% volume.
- The monitor's stand folds flat/back for many viewing angles and feels light but not
  cheap; the buttons feel "very Fisher-Price" in a good way — durable enough a kid can't
  break them.
- The "easy setup" promise mostly holds: his five-year-old plugged in the monitor,
  mouse, and power and got through first-boot with only light help. Wi-Fi and account
  setup needed him, but he says most people wouldn't struggle with that.
- Who it's for: kids; people who won't flash an SD card, choose an OS, or troubleshoot;
  and anyone who just wants a cheap little computer to tinker with Linux (a great Linux
  starter).
- Who it's NOT for: tinkerers like him and his audience — people who break things, know
  networking, already own monitors/keyboards, and run their Pis headless with SD-card
  config done up front. Still, he's giving this one to his kids so they grow up familiar
  with Linux.
- Disappointment: he promised his daughter Roblox and only then realized Roblox can't run
  on a Raspberry Pi (Linux); he publicly asks Roblox to fix that.
- Off the Pi 500, the monitor showed some flickering; he blames the underpowered supply
  and expects the desktop kit's 27 W supply would be plenty.
- He'll probably buy a Pi Monitor for himself — for setting up other Pis that need a
  display, for filming videos, for server work, and as a portable travel monitor (small,
  USB-powered, fits an airplane tray). He also verified it works as a solid second monitor
  over HDMI off his MacBook.
- He likes the problem Raspberry Pi is solving: people who buy a Pi then scramble for a
  compatible monitor/keyboard now get a known-good, matching set from one brand.
