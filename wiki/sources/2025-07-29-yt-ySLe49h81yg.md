# Network Engineer lets AI run his Network

## Summary

An on-location interview filmed at Juniper Networks in which Chuck ([[../entities/networkchuck|NetworkChuck]]) sits down with a guest network engineer named **Allan** to cut through vendor "AI this, AI that" marketing and find out what it is actually like to let AI run a real network day-to-day. Allan, a longtime network engineer who works for an integrator (and does infrastructure for a security company), walks through his hands-on experience with Juniper's **Mist** platform and its AI assistant **Marvis** — what "AIOps" really means, how much he trusts AI to make changes, and where he thinks network engineering is headed.

The conversation frames a maturity spectrum: from AI as back-end data-distillation and "fancy alerting," to correlation and prediction, to today's early "self-driving" changes (e.g. "just reboot the switch for me"), toward a future where engineers *talk to the network* instead of typing CLI commands. Both agree AI won't take network engineers' jobs — it moves them up the stack from typing CLI to designing and delivering solutions. A strong secondary thread: even in an AI-run future, newcomers must still learn the traditional stack and fundamentals (OSI model, routing) first, because AI is still frequently wrong and you can't catch its errors without the foundation.

Chuck is the interviewer/host here; the deep technical claims are Allan's. Only Chuck-attributed material trains the persona.

## Key claims (dated — concept + his take, attributed)

- **[2025-07-29 | Allan]** "AIOps" today mostly means back-end systems that interpret data and distill up results a human engineer probably would not have caught — AI doing the investigation across operations. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-29 | Allan]** The field is only now moving from reactive AIOps into early "self-driving" territory — letting AI make actual changes (reboot a switch, fix an issue) rather than just reporting. He trusts Marvis enough now to let it make those changes. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-29 | Allan]** Trust in AI network automation took *time* and depends on the data pool being big enough to cover the corner cases — networking is easy the ~90% of the time it just works; the trouble is the other 10% of corner cases. Earlier/immature AI kept blaming "always DNS/ARP/DHCP" or "always an LLDP problem"; maturity means it now says *it's DNS, but DNS because of this specific thing*. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-29 | Allan]** Mist/Marvis keeps hundreds of pieces of client state per minute, so you can rewind to an exact past moment (e.g. "last Thursday at 3pm my wireless dropped") and explain what happened — and correlate beyond the network into things like a bad Teams call caused by CPU overload. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-29 | Allan]** It has moved beyond fancy alerting and beyond plain machine-learning correlation into making *predictions* ("will this be a problem?"), and — with the agentic movement — toward proactive change; today it is still largely reactive, with the main blocker being human resistance (old-school engineers reluctant to let the network change itself). (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-29 | Allan]** For an integrator, good AIOps removes the need to babysit clients post-deployment (it cut his pro-services hours), freeing him to add value via Python scripting/programming and higher-level solutions. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-29 | Allan]** "If a network engineer is afraid of AI because it's going to take my job, then they're doing their job wrong." The value shift is from typing on a CLI to driving, offering, and delivering solutions. (verbatim / paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-29 | Allan]** Five-year outlook: engineers will *talk to the network* — asking it what's going on, what to investigate, what to change/upgrade — instead of typing CLI commands to figure out state. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-29 | Allan]** Newcomers must still learn the traditional stack and fundamentals first (OSI model, how to design a routed network / OSPF). AI is still frequently wrong; without the foundation you'll do it wrong and not know why. Learn basics and AI as building blocks, back and forth. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-29 | Allan]** On avoiding cognitive offloading: start with Google (AI answers turned *off*), learn to read and parse results and release notes / data sheets; ChatGPT gives a confident-sounding answer you can't verify without comprehension skills. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-29 | Chuck]** Chuck's own framing/take: he agrees the future role sounds like "network prompt engineers" — knowing which questions to ask the network to get the right answers. (paraphrase — Chuck)
- **[2025-07-29 | Chuck]** Chuck voices concern that the coming generation (and people like us) will offload cognitive abilities to AI and rely on it for everything — a danger he wants to see balanced against genuinely knowing the material. (paraphrase — Chuck)
- **[2025-07-29 | Chuck]** Chuck praises the NetworkChuck Discord: experienced folks like Allan jump in to steer newcomers toward the real stack and fundamentals, not the flashy AI stuff. (paraphrase — Chuck)
