# 5G: what you DON'T know!! (3 things)

## Summary

A Verizon-sponsored networking explainer, framed around the iPhone 5G launch, in which
Chuck walks through three (plus a bonus) lesser-known aspects of 5G. He interviews Heidi
Hemmer, VP of Network Engineering at Verizon, whose statements are attributed to her
throughout — only Chuck's own explanations train the persona. Chuck's angle is that 5G
is "all about the network," not the phone: the air interface between phone and tower is
only the visible tip, and what happens behind the tower (the core network) matters just
as much. He explains the three "flavors" of 5G spectrum (low/mid/high band), the shift
from a shared 4G core to a dedicated 5G standalone core built on cloud-style
containerized, web-scale, software-defined architecture, and his prediction that 5G will
replace Wi-Fi (public, home, and private enterprise) before it replaces anything else.
The companion piece to his earlier general 5G video.

## Key claims (dated — the 3 things)

All dated 2020-12-10 (video publication). All paraphrase unless quoted below.

1. **(#1) A 5G phone can't do much new yet, and speed depends on which of three
   spectrum "flavors" you're on.** Chuck frames 5G spectrum as three bands: low band
   (nationwide, long range, but 4G-like speeds because it reuses 4G frequencies), mid
   band (roughly 100 Mbps up to ~900 Mbps, but you must be closer to the tower), and
   high band (~1 Gbps up to possibly 10 Gbps, but you must be right next to the tower;
   deployed in dense areas — cities, stadiums, arenas, airports — via small cells on
   light posts and traffic lights). Verizon's high-band product is 5G Ultra Wideband
   (millimeter wave). The phone and network auto-select the best available layer (Ultra
   Wideband → nationwide 5G → 4G). His verdict: for now, you can do everything you did
   on 4G, just faster — nobody knows the killer use cases yet.

2. **(#2) Not all 5G is the same — what matters is what happens *after* the tower.**
   Chuck's core point: 5G hype focuses on the phone-to-tower air interface, but traffic
   then hits a whole back-end network (fiber, routers, switches, firewalls, the core).
   A carrier's network built for 4G may not deliver on 5G's promise. As of November 2020,
   Verizon's 5G traffic still routes to a 4G core (via a 4G "anchor site"), which works
   but caps the low-latency benefit. To truly deliver, Verizon is building a dedicated
   **5G standalone core** carrying only 5G traffic, built on cloud-style principles:
   **software-defined networking (SDN)** to automate/manage thousands of devices without
   large manual engineering teams; **containerized, web-scale architecture** (the same
   way AWS/Google Cloud build data centers) to spin capacity up/down per customer and
   event; and **AI/machine learning** baked in so the network auto-scales to demand
   (e.g. a crowd event) without engineers manually building it. Bonus: **MEC (mobile edge
   compute)** brings compute close to the tower to cut the round-trip latency that would
   otherwise undo the fast air interface.

3. **(#3) 5G will replace Wi-Fi first (Chuck's prediction).** Scenario: you walk into a
   coffee shop and normally connect to their Wi-Fi — Chuck predicts 5G Ultra Wideband
   will remove that step (you're already connected, likely faster). Verizon has announced
   in-building units (partnering with Corning and Samsung) for small venues, plus home
   5G internet, plus a "network in a box" — a **private 5G core** enterprises can deploy
   on their own campuses (even internationally, where Verizon lacks spectrum). Chuck adds
   a security angle: cellular is materially safer than public Wi-Fi (he references his
   own earlier video showing how easy it is to hack someone on public Wi-Fi). Heidi's
   reason 5G beats Wi-Fi for business: the carrier owns and controls licensed spectrum,
   so it can optimize and grow capacity in ways Wi-Fi access points can't.

4. **(Bonus #4) 5G isn't really about your phone or speed test — it's the "fourth
   industrial revolution."** Verizon spent 2–3 years pre-launch running 5G labs and
   technology forums with network OEMs, device OEMs, and application developers, because a
   network is useless without use cases (examples: 360° virtual clothes try-on, AR/VR
   holograms at a Chainsmokers concert visible only on 5G devices). Chuck highlights
   **network slicing** (a 5G standalone-core feature) via a factory example: slicing
   guarantees latency/throughput/QoS for safety-critical robotic forklifts so a traffic
   spike in back-office apps can't cause a forklift accident.
