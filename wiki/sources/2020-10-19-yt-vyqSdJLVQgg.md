# 3 Hacking Skills EVERYONE has // FREE Security+ // EP 1

## Summary

Episode 1 (the kickoff) of NetworkChuck's FREE CompTIA Security+ course — a second
flagship free-education series, produced in collaboration with David Bombal and Jeremy
Cioara. Chuck frames the whole series and this episode around social engineering:
instead of hacking computers, servers, and networks, hackers hack the "human OS." He
argues everyone already possesses the three most dangerous hacking skills and walks
through each one, both as an attack technique and how to defend against it.

The three skills are: (1) **Recon / reconnaissance** — gathering information on a target,
which for most people looks exactly like snooping on an ex or a new coworker via
Facebook, LinkedIn, and Twitter. In hacker terms this is OSINT (open source
intelligence): using only publicly available info, which is not illegal. He demos tools
including the OSINT Framework, a username lookup that finds "networkchuck" across
platforms, Twint (advanced Twitter search with no account needed, including tweets near
a location), Hunter.io (pulls legit corporate email addresses and phone numbers), and
Google hacking / Google dorking. (2) **Dumpster diving** — digging through trash (largely
legal as public domain in much of the US) to recover pre-approved credit offers,
documents, passwords, email lists, and purchase orders. (3) **Shoulder surfing** —
watching over someone's shoulder, peeking at laptops/phones/ATM PINs in public, plus
eavesdropping on conversations (e.g. sitting in a Starbucks near a Tesla HQ with earbuds
in, recording, listening for badge-wearing engineers talking shop).

Chuck stresses these attacks are dangerous because anyone can do them and they are the
hardest to defend against, since they rely on flawed humans. Defenses: don't put info
out (assume everything online is public even when "private"), shred documents / hire
third-party disposal companies, control line of sight and use biometrics, and don't talk
about work outside work. He closes by pointing to a weekly YouTube cadence for the free
series and earlier access via thisisit.io.

## Key claims (paraphrase)

- (2020-10-19) This video is Episode 1 of a FREE CompTIA Security+ course NetworkChuck is
  producing in collaboration with David Bombal and Jeremy Cioara, with a new free video
  posted each week on YouTube.
- (2020-10-19) The three most dangerous and effective hacking skills are social
  engineering techniques that everyone already has: recon, dumpster diving, and shoulder
  surfing — they hack "the human OS," not computers.
- (2020-10-19) Recon (reconnaissance) is the first thing a hacker does — gathering as much
  information as possible about a target (a person or a company) to prep for later attacks.
- (2020-10-19) Casual snooping on someone's social media (Facebook, LinkedIn, Twitter) is
  functionally the same skill as hacker reconnaissance, just without malicious intent.
- (2020-10-19) In the hacking community this is called OSINT — open source intelligence —
  gathering only publicly available information, which is not illegal.
- (2020-10-19) Small facts (interests, places visited, religion, friends, family) let an
  attacker craft targeted phishing emails, impersonate the target's friends, or
  impersonate the target to attack someone else.
- (2020-10-19) LinkedIn is especially valuable for targeting a company: an employee's work
  history and listed skills advertise what technologies (e.g. Cisco ASAs) a company uses
  to defend its network.
- (2020-10-19) There is a tension between the career advice to market yourself online and
  the fact that the same information can be used against you and your employer.
- (2020-10-19) OSINT tooling includes the OSINT Framework (a graph of tools), username
  search tools that locate a handle across social platforms, Twint (advanced Twitter
  search with no account, including tweets near a location), Hunter.io (returns real
  corporate email addresses and phone numbers for a company), and Google hacking / Google
  dorking to surface public info that probably should not be public.
- (2020-10-19) Recon defense: assume anything you put online — even "private" or
  friends-only posts — will be seen; companies should have policies and train employees not
  to post about the company.
- (2020-10-19) Dumpster diving is a legit and effective technique; in most of the US
  digging through trash is legal (public domain), though Chuck advises consulting a lawyer.
- (2020-10-19) Discarded mail and documents (pre-approved credit offers, passwords, email
  lists, purchase orders, classified company documents) can be recovered from the trash
  and weaponized.
- (2020-10-19) Dumpster diving defense: don't throw away anything you wouldn't want seen;
  shred documents into tiny pieces; enterprises hire third-party shredding/disposal and
  recycling companies to wipe hard drives and destroy hardware — never throw computers away.
- (2020-10-19) Shoulder surfing is watching what someone is doing — seeing a password or
  document over their shoulder, peeking at laptops or phones in public, or observing an ATM
  PIN entry.
- (2020-10-19) Shoulder surfing extends to eavesdropping: an attacker can sit in a public
  place with earbuds in (playing nothing) and listen for or record valuable technical/work
  conversations — e.g. camping in a coffee shop near Tesla HQ to catch engineers talking.
- (2020-10-19) Shoulder surfing defense: control line of sight to your screen, prefer
  biometrics over typed passcodes, be aware of long-range camera/telescope observation,
  and don't talk about work outside of work.
- (2020-10-19) These social-engineering attacks are the most dangerous because almost
  anyone can perform them and they are the hardest to defend against, since they exploit
  flawed, unpatched humans rather than an OS like Windows, Linux, or macOS.
- (2020-10-19) These techniques set the stage for later, more advanced social-engineering
  attacks covered later in the series; understanding the hacker's mindset is how you
  protect yourself and your company.
