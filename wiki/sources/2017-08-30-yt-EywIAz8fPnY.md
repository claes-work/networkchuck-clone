# CompTIA or Cisco? - Should I get the CompTIA A+/Network+ OR the Cisco CCNA/CCENT?

## Summary
Chuck answers a recurring viewer question — CompTIA (A+/Network+/Security+/Server+) versus a vendor-specific cert (Cisco CCENT/CCNA or Microsoft MCSA) — by framing it around two questions: where are you now, and where do you want to be. He opens with his own regret: he ignored a network engineer's advice to skip the A+ and dive into Cisco, finished the A+ anyway while already on a helpdesk, and got nothing career-wise from it. His core recommendation is that CompTIA (especially A+) is only worth getting to land a first IT job when you lack computer confidence; anyone aiming above helpdesk should skip CompTIA and go straight for a vendor-specific cert, because Cisco/Microsoft entry certs teach the same fundamentals plus vendor skills and rank far higher on a resume.

## Key claims
- (2017-08-30) The decision comes down to two questions: where you are in your career right now, and where you want to be. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) He started studying A+ in 2011 with zero IT experience, but landed his first two IT jobs (an "off-brand Geek Squad" tech role, then a junior helpdesk tech role) without holding the A+. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) A network engineer at his company advised him to skip the A+ and CompTIA entirely and dive straight into Cisco (CCENT / ICND1, half of the CCNA); Chuck ignored the advice, finished his A+, and nothing changed for his career because he was already on the helpdesk — the cert is designed to get you a helpdesk job he already had. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) If you are already on a helpdesk or in any IT support / computer-repair role, do NOT go for the A+; go for a vendor-specific cert (Cisco CCENT/CCNA) instead — it benefits you far more. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) The only time he can justify recommending the A+ is to land your very first IT job when you have zero experience and don't yet feel confident troubleshooting basic computer issues, because A+ is a common keyword in desktop-support job descriptions and resume searches. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) If you're already computer-savvy and confident supporting desktops, skip CompTIA and jump straight into Cisco or Microsoft certs; a CCNA or MCSA puts you above candidates who only have an A+. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) The natural progression to network engineer or system admin runs through a helpdesk role; getting a network-engineer/sysadmin job with no prior IT experience is very difficult (not impossible). [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) His advice for breaking in: find a company with room for growth that runs the technology you want (a Cisco shop or Microsoft shop), get on their helpdesk no matter how low you start, then hustle to move up — companies prefer to promote from within. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) Studying visibly (e.g., coming in early to study before a shift) gets management and senior IT staff to notice you as a hustler and think of you first for promotions. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) Skip Network+ and Server+ too — Cisco and Microsoft certs already cover networking and server basics while also teaching vendor equipment. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) Cisco and Microsoft entry-level certs (e.g., CCENT — Cisco Certified Entry Networking Technician) have no prerequisites and are designed for new techs; you do not need an A+ first to tackle them. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) Cisco is a network leader used by nearly every company and most companies run Microsoft, so vendor certs teach the equipment you'll actually use on the job. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) He was promoted to junior network admin simply because his employer knew he was working on his CCNA. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) If you're three-quarters through the A+ or already have it, finish it / keep it — it's a great asset and won't hurt you; the video is aimed at people still choosing between CompTIA and a vendor cert. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) Renewing the A+ every three years is a good idea for people who genuinely want to stay on the helpdesk and love front-line user interaction. [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) General principle: always get the certification that benefits you most for where you are right now — which is why he personally switched from pursuing CCNP to going for CCNA Collaboration (voice). [paraphrase]
- (2017-08-30) He held A+ (now expired) and currently holds Security+, but would not recommend CompTIA to someone trying to advance their career and get the most value for their money. [paraphrase]

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "if you want to make a lot of money, if you want to advance as fast as you can and be as valuable as you can be to a company, get those vendor specific certs. You will thank me for it, I promise you."

> "I always advise people should always get the certification that's gonna benefit you the most where you're at right now."
