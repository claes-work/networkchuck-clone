# The Future of Networking Certifications

## Summary

An on-site interview at Cisco Live, filmed by [[../../SUBJECT|Chuck Keith / NetworkChuck]]
with two Cisco guests: **Kareem Escander** (technical advocacy for learning at Cisco) and
**Joe Clark** (27-year Cisco veteran, distinguished engineer, oversees the continuing
education program). The conversation is a follow-up to a 2019 San Diego interview Chuck did
with Joe about the (then major) CCNA update; this time the frame is AI. The discussion
covers how Cisco is folding AI into certification blueprints, whether AI will replace
network engineers, how AI could reshape certification exams themselves, and how to keep
skills sharp in the AI era.

Because this is a multi-voice interview, only Chuck's statements are used for persona
training. The Cisco guests describe Cisco's product/program direction; those claims are
attributed to them and are treated as context, not as the subject's beliefs.

## Key claims (dated)

_Chuck Keith (subject — persona-eligible):_

- **[2025-07-03]** The CCNA is Chuck's favorite certification of all time and the one he
  recommends as the "gold standard" entry point — with a CCNA you can get an entry-level
  job "anywhere in IT," even if you plan to go into hacking or something else. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-03]** AI feels like a bigger, faster, more "exponential" shift than network
  automation was; automation once made him fear "programmers are going to take my job" (he
  called himself a "CLI jockey"), and AI feels like an even more massive change. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-03]** He raises the "am I automating myself out of a job?" fear directly — the
  worry that if AI opens up all the APIs/MCP and does everything, the engineer's role
  shrinks. He voices the concern but the guests (and his framing) land on "no." (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-03]** On degrees vs. certifications in the fast-moving AI era: "Colleges can't
  keep up" is his opinion. (paraphrase — see verbatim below)
- **[2025-07-03]** He agrees AI used "the right way" makes an engineer better rather than
  obsolete; his recurring Cisco Live theme was asking people what they're using AI for and
  whether they're excited about it. (paraphrase)

_Cisco guests (context — NOT persona-eligible):_

- **[2025-07-03] (Joe Clark)** Cisco has revised the CCNA blueprint to add AI: awareness of
  what AI can do, AI use cases, and — most importantly — how to use it responsibly (what
  you feed the AI, how you interpret and verify what comes back). The junior network
  engineer still needs fundamental network skills but should use AI as a tool. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-03] (Kareem Escander)** Cisco's "RevUp" program (on Cisco U) offers free
  tutorials from entry to expert; the CCNA-level content covers predictive vs. agentic AI,
  spotting wrong LLM output, and using RAG. There's also an AI RevUp (DCAIA) about
  automating AI infrastructure. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-03] (Joe Clark)** Recertification is being reframed away from repeatedly
  passing exams toward earning continuing-education credits in currently-relevant skills.
  CCNA is on a yearly cadence for now; they may have to speed that up. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-03] (Kareem Escander)** The DevNet track is being renamed to "automation";
  professional-level certs (automation core, enterprise networking automation) are evolving
  to include AI topics like automating the network with MCP. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-03] (Joe Clark)** Future exams may drop multiple-choice memorization in favor
  of hands-on tasks (dropped into a real CLI or web portal to actually configure/implement)
  plus critical-thinking scenarios — e.g., given a topology, configs, a prompt, and an LLM's
  answer, judge whether the AI's response is correct. Chuck characterizes this as teaching
  "a little bit of prompt engineering with networking." (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-03] (Joe Clark)** His advice: don't fear AI taking your job — "run towards
  learning AI"; the human's enduring value is to **innovate** and apply **critical thinking**,
  which AI doesn't have (yet). Certification "isn't an end, it is the beginning of a
  journey." He recommends Cisco U over a degree. (paraphrase)
- **[2025-07-03] (Kareem Escander)** He uses AI almost daily (email feedback via an internal/
  responsible AI instance, code writing). Cites Cisco leadership's aggressive prediction that
  70% of code will be AI-written within ~18 months. (paraphrase)
