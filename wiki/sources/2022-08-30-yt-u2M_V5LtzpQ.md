# How YouTubers Get Hacked

## Summary

A security-awareness PSA aimed at content creators, breaking down a real-world
phishing/malware campaign that has been taking over YouTube channels — attackers gain
full control to add/delete videos, start live streams, and ultimately delete the
channel. Chuck frames it as personally worrying ("has me worried about my channel")
because the attack is clever. The bulk of the video is an interview/walkthrough with
guest **John Hammond**, a security researcher and YouTuber who was himself targeted and
nearly fell for the hack before reverse-engineering it.

The attack chain: a victim receives what looks like a **legitimate Google Drive
notification** ("YouTube has shared a PDF file with you") plus a real Google Drive email
("YouTube copyright report"). Because a copyright strike is terrifying to a creator, the
lure creates urgency to "read the full report" via a linked download. The credibility
tell was the shared-from email address, which "raises an eyebrow." The download link
(hosted on a `next[.]get report`-style site, later a Dropbox link, later a Discord-hosted
file) leads to a ~400 MB Windows executable disguised as a screenshot/`.docx`. The site
**checks the user agent** and refuses to serve the payload to Tor, curl, or non-standard
browsers — dodging security researchers — only serving the malware to real Chrome/Firefox
victims. The final payload is the **RedLine information stealer**, which harvests saved
browser sessions, logins, and username/password bundles and exfiltrates them to the
attacker. Stolen browser session tokens/cookies let attackers bypass the login and 2FA to
seize the channel. Chuck closes with the blunt takeaway: "don't click any links they're
all bad."

## Key claims (dated — attack + defense)

- **[2022-08-30] (attack)** Attackers are taking over YouTube channels and gaining full
  control — adding/deleting videos, starting live streams, and deleting the channel
  outright. (paraphrase)
- **[2022-08-30] (attack)** The lure impersonates official Google/YouTube channels: a
  real-looking Google Drive push notification plus a genuine Google Drive email claiming
  "YouTube copyright report" — more convincing than a plain phishing email because it
  arrives through legitimate Google delivery. (paraphrase)
- **[2022-08-30] (attack)** Social engineering leans on creator fear: a copyright strike
  is terrifying, and you supposedly can't appeal unless you read the linked report — that
  urgency pushes victims to open the malicious download. (paraphrase)
- **[2022-08-30] (attack)** Links included the victim's own email as an HTTP GET
  parameter, likely for tracking who is a "legitimate victim" for future attacks.
  (paraphrase)
- **[2022-08-30] (attack)** The malicious payload was a ~400 MB Windows executable
  disguised as a screenshot / `.docx`; delivery moved across hosts (a `get report` site,
  Dropbox, then a Discord-hosted file) and changed over the recording session. (paraphrase)
- **[2022-08-30] (attack)** The delivery site checks the user agent and only serves the
  payload to modern victim browsers (Chrome/Firefox/mobile), ignoring Tor and curl to
  evade security researchers. (paraphrase)
- **[2022-08-30] (attack)** The end payload is the **RedLine information stealer** malware
  family, which harvests saved browser sessions, logins, and credential bundles and sends
  them to the attackers' HQ. Stolen sessions/cookies are what let attackers hijack the
  account. (paraphrase)
- **[2022-08-30] (defense)** Do extra due diligence: don't trust the Google Drive pop-up
  alone — go check the actual email and inspect the sender address, which is where the
  fake reveals itself. (paraphrase)
- **[2022-08-30] (defense)** Don't click the link directly — right-click and copy the link
  address to inspect where it actually goes before opening anything. (paraphrase)
- **[2022-08-30] (defense)** Investigate suspicious files in a safer sandbox — an
  amnesiac/ephemeral OS like Tails or a Whonix/Tor VM, and a separate VM — rather than on
  your real machine. (paraphrase)
- **[2022-08-30] (defense)** Blunt final rule for creators: don't click any links; assume
  they're all bad. (paraphrase)
