# will my dad LOSE his job? (a TRADITIONAL IT infrastructure engineer)

## Summary

Chuck uses his own father — also named Chuck Keith, an infrastructure manager
at Supreme Lending focused on the system/storage side — as the lens for a
career question the whole traditional-IT audience is asking in 2021: as
workloads shift to the cloud and to cloud-native technologies (containers,
Kubernetes, microservices, DevOps automation), will the "traditional
infrastructure engineer" role become obsolete? His dad appears on camera
throughout as an interviewee describing what he does (storage,
virtualization, VMware/Hyper-V, Active Directory, on-prem servers, break-fix)
and where his own company is heading (starting to move to Azure, expects to
stay a hybrid shop).

Chuck's answer is reassuring but conditional: traditional on-prem skills stay
"insanely valuable" and on-prem is not going anywhere, but the industry is
genuinely shifting, so engineers still must keep learning. He frames the cloud
decision around "three laws" (economics, physics/latency, and laws of the
land / compliance), explains cloud-native and DevOps as their own disciplines,
and pitches a middle path where traditional engineers use tools they already
know (e.g. VMware vSphere + Tanzu, delivered via the video's sponsor Dell
Technologies APEX) to run cloud-native workloads on-prem and multi-cloud
without a full skill-set overhaul. The video is sponsored by Dell Technologies
(APEX cloud, Customer Solution Centers lab).

## Key claims (paraphrase; attributed)

- **[Chuck, 2021]** IT is evolving in how infrastructure is deployed and
  managed, requiring new skills the traditional infrastructure engineer may
  not have. He admits he shares this fear: "I'm traditional in a lot of ways."
- **[Chuck's dad, 2021]** A traditional infrastructure engineer's job is
  storage, virtualization, and "anything on-prem" — virtual machines,
  VMware/Hyper-V, Active Directory (the backbone for many companies), plus
  day-to-day server management, building/preparing servers, and break-fix
  troubleshooting.
- **[Chuck, 2021]** The skills of a traditional infra engineer (virtualization,
  storage, server management) are crazy valuable and will remain valuable for a
  long time — but a big shift is underway as workloads move to the public cloud
  (AWS, Microsoft/Azure).
- **[Chuck, 2021]** The "three laws" of whether to move to the public cloud:
  (1) **economics** — cloud avoids costly server-refresh cycles, cheaper
  upfront, but weigh long-run cost; (2) **physics** — latency of accessing data
  in the cloud vs. on-prem; (3) **laws of the land** — security/compliance
  requirements the cloud may not meet, forcing some data to stay in an owned
  data center. Moving one workload is often called a "lift and shift" upgrade.
- **[Chuck, 2021]** Cloud-native technologies (microservices, containers,
  Kubernetes) are their own discipline/career path with their own
  certifications; to truly wield them you also need automation tools like
  Ansible, Puppet, Chef — which crosses into DevOps territory.
- **[Chuck, 2021]** A DevOps engineer combines infrastructure skills (cloud,
  Kubernetes, microservices) with enough software-development skill to
  coordinate with the dev team and build pipelines — the "person in the middle."
- **[Chuck, 2021]** Cloud adoption is gradual, not a switch-flip; many companies
  are slow/hesitant ("if it ain't broke, don't fix it") and stay on-prem while
  moving select workloads. Widely used apps (Microsoft SQL Server, Oracle) are
  now offered as containers.
- **[Chuck's dad, 2021]** His company has started moving to the cloud —
  Active Directory domain controllers in Azure, building machines and doing
  development in Azure; he's concentrating on the Azure side. He expects the
  company will "always be somewhat of a hybrid shop" but focus more on cloud in
  the future. To his knowledge they are not yet using microservices, but want
  to move there later this year or next.
- **[Chuck, 2021]** When traditional skills start to become less relevant, one
  of two things happens: (1) the company adopts a new dedicated (often public
  cloud) environment and must hire DevOps talent or retrain existing staff —
  both fine but expensive/time-consuming; or (2) the newer trend — extend the
  existing on-prem environment to run cloud-native workloads on-prem.
- **[Chuck, 2021]** With VMware vSphere + Tanzu (delivered via Dell
  Technologies APEX), traditional engineers can deploy cloud-native workloads
  (e.g. Kubernetes) alongside VMs using the same familiar tool (like vCenter),
  on-prem and across multi-cloud (Azure, AWS, GCP), without a complete skill-set
  overhaul.
- **[Chuck's dad, 2021]** Going forward the role will need more programming —
  PowerShell, Python — plus Kubernetes and the whole cloud journey; "we're
  gonna have to adapt and change." He knows on-prem "the way it is today is
  going to be totally different in a couple years," and is studying to be more
  cloud-centric.
- **[Chuck, 2021]** Verdict: the traditional skills stay valuable, on-prem isn't
  going anywhere, and companies don't have to go to the cloud to use
  cloud-native tech. But traditional engineers should still change and learn —
  containers and Kubernetes are here to stay. "The learning never stops" is
  nothing new in IT, and it doesn't have to be as drastic or scary as news
  articles make it seem. He's not worried; IT jobs will change and vendors will
  help.
