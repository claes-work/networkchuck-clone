# I PASSED THE CCENT EXAM!! - ICND1 Exam Tips

## Summary

An interview-format video in which Chuck hosts his brother **Cameron** (a guest) to
debrief Cameron's recent pass of the **CCENT** certification via the **ICND1** exam
(the first of the two exams that make up the CCNA). The transcript's auto-captions
repeatedly garble "CCENT" as "CSUN" / "C sent" and "GNS3" as "gns3"; the topic
throughout is the Cisco CCENT / ICND1.

The pass being celebrated is **Cameron's**, not Chuck's. Chuck states he took his own
CCENT years earlier ("back in I think it was 2012 13 or something"), and hosts this as
a "recent test taker's perspective." The conversation walks through Cameron's study
timeline, resources (video courses, books, labbing), the exam experience, and closing
advice for prospective test takers.

## Key claims (dated — milestone + tips)

Milestone:
- [2017-10-01] Cameron (Chuck's brother) **passed the CCENT** exam (ICND1), taken "a
  couple weeks ago." He already held the CompTIA A+ prior to this. *(guest milestone,
  self-reported)*
- [2017-10-01] Chuck states he passed his own CCENT earlier, around **2012–2013**, and
  frames this video as bringing in a recent test-taker because "a lot's changed since
  then." *(Chuck, self-reported — not the milestone of this video)*

Study timeline & scheduling tips:
- Cameron's studying took ~6 months, partly because he had originally studied for the
  previous exam version (100-101 / ICND1 v2) but missed the deadline and got revised to
  CCNA v3; he estimates 3–4 months with full focus.
- Scheduling the exam ~1 month in advance is recommended to create a deadline and
  pressure, since "you could study forever and never schedule the exam."
- Chuck notes Cisco exams can be rescheduled on 24-hour notice; he rescheduled his own
  ICND2 "like 4 times" when not ready — revise the plan, don't get discouraged.

Exam-format tips:
- On the CCENT/ICND1 you **cannot go back** and review or flag questions — every answer
  is final, unlike the CompTIA A+ where review is allowed. This makes you think harder.
- The A+ is more factual/book-reading; the CCENT tests how things *work* (switching,
  layer 2 / layer 3, how routers forward packets). Cameron only had to run **show**
  commands, never had to configure anything on the exam.
- ~55–60 questions total (Cameron had 56), and that count includes **simlets** (a sim
  scenario with 3–4 questions attached). Do well on simlets — they weigh heavily.
- Subnetting on the exam was simpler than expected; nothing past a **class C**. Keep
  subnetting questions to about one minute.
- Hardest topics are the ones you can't just memorize: network/routing fundamentals,
  how routers forward packets, data-link layer behavior, the **OSI model** and
  **TCP/IP model** — these are also the largest percentages of the exam. **NTP** gets
  mixed in and is easy to under-prepare.

Resources & method tips:
- Recommended order: start with a **video course** to build the foundation and spark
  interest, then read the **books** to fill gaps, then **practice exams** and **labbing**.
- Video courses discussed: **CBT Nuggets (Jeremy Cioara)** and **INE (Keith Bogart)**.
  Cameron preferred Keith Bogart/INE for its stricter, fundamental teaching for this
  exam; Chuck's ICND2 studies leaned on CBT Nuggets/Jeremy Cioara for motivation.
- Use **Cisco Press** books (official cert guide + the **Exam Cram**-style practice
  books); the chapter pre-tests let you skip or focus reading. Even Keith Bogart advises
  pairing his video series with a good book rather than relying on video alone.
- Use **multiple resources** — no single training partner can cover every exam topic in
  depth.
- For labbing, Cisco **Packet Tracer** is recommended: free (via a free Cisco Academy
  account), works out of the box, includes switching sufficient for CCNA. GNS3 is great
  but has a learning curve and setup overhead; Packet Tracer is the easiest way to start.
  Cameron preferred building/breaking his own labs over the purchased Cisco-provided labs.
- Readiness check: read through every single point in the exam topics and honestly say
  "I know that" for each; if you're scared to look, you're not ready.
- Night before: relax (video games, a movie) rather than cram if you studied hard — over-
  stressing leads to overthinking on the test. Cameron played video games the night
  before; Chuck has a ritual of watching *The Hobbit* the night before an exam.
- After passing, take a short break (a few days to a week) before starting ICND2 — you'll
  be mentally spent even if riding the post-pass "high."
- Chuck points people to the **NetworkChuck Facebook study group** (where Cameron answers
  questions) and to networkchuck.com for his recommended resources.
