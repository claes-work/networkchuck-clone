# Is the NEW CompTIA A+ Exam Worth It? (220-1201 and 220-1202)

## Summary

On his second channel (`@networkchuck_v2`), Chuck reacts to the then-unannounced new CompTIA A+ exam launching spring 2025, which he found on CompTIA's website in draft form before any official announcement. The new exam replaces the 2022 edition (Core 1 = 220-1101, Core 2 = 220-1102) with the 220-1201 (Core 1) and 220-1202 (Core 2). He walks through the draft exam objectives, compares them domain-by-domain against the 2022 version, and delivers his verdict on whether the cert is worth it and who should get it.

Key content changes he flags: Core 1 (220-1201) keeps its five domains (mobile devices, networking, hardware, virtualization/cloud, hardware & network troubleshooting) but shifts emphasis — more MDM/BYOD, NFC/biometrics security, IPv6 and APIPA, USB-C, cloud multi-tenancy, less focus on legacy VGA/older USB; networking got a ~3% weighting increase. Core 2 (220-1202) covers operating systems, security, software troubleshooting, and IT operations, with Windows 11 now the primary focus (Windows 10 still present), new file systems (ReFS, XFS), deeper macOS/Linux commands, and a much bigger security focus — zero trust, privileged access management (PAM), TOTP, QR-code phishing, business email compromise (BEC), supply chain attacks. Notably, a new AI-literacy objective was added to operational procedures (public vs. private AI, data privacy, bias, hallucinations, plagiarism). Security weighting is up ~3 points; operating systems down ~3; operational procedures down.

This is his 2025 update to his long-running A+/CompTIA stance. It reaffirms the position from his earlier career-roadmap content: the A+ is the canonical first IT certification for landing an entry-level help desk role, and the real value is the foundational knowledge, not the paper itself.

## Key claims (dated — his A+ recommendation)

- **[2025-02-07] Chuck still recommends the CompTIA A+, including the new 2025 version (220-1201/1202).** He calls it "the biggest name in the game for entry-level IT" and says employers still look for it when hiring for help desk / entry-level roles. (Paraphrase)
- **[2025-02-07] The A+ is his recommended FIRST IT certification** — it was his own first cert. Its purpose is to land that first entry-level help desk job, which he frames as the best job in the world for learning. (Paraphrase)
- **[2025-02-07] If you're already studying for the old 2022 edition, keep going and finish it** — don't let the new version stop or hamper your progress. Resumes don't show exam versions; a 2022 A+ looks just as good as a 2025 A+, and the cert is valid for 3 years. (Paraphrase)
- **[2025-02-07] The old 2022 exams are probably safe to keep studying for over roughly the next 6 months** — CompTIA hadn't announced a retirement date; he guesses a grace period of maybe 6–7 months, but explicitly says he's guessing. (Paraphrase)
- **[2025-02-07] The knowledge matters more than the certificate.** Core IT foundations (networking, cloud, CPU/RAM/drives, operating systems) haven't changed much, so someone already studying via any A+ book or course should keep going. (Paraphrase)
- **[2025-02-07] You may be able to skip the A+ if you're already advanced** — e.g., you've built your own PC or run a Raspberry Pi — and jump straight to a higher cert like Network+, CCNA, or Security+. Only you can judge where you're at. (Paraphrase)
- **[2025-02-07] You may not strictly need the A+ to get a help desk job, but get it if you're struggling** — it looks good on a resume and helps you land that first role. (Paraphrase)
- **[2025-02-07] Cert-sequencing / "buffet" framing:** the A+ is a sampler that exposes newcomers to the different IT roles/paths so they can find what they like; after his own A+ he discovered he loved networking and went down the Cisco path next. (Paraphrase)
- **[2025-02-07] The new version brings needed modernization but likely isn't a dramatic change** — updated tech, Windows 11 focus, more security/networking, added AI concepts; he expects "more of the same, just more modern content," and hopes (but doesn't know) the exam might become more practical/hands-on. (Paraphrase)
- **[2025-02-07] Price is a real drawback:** he cites a base price of $250 per exam (two exams required), calls it steep, and recalls failing one early in his career, which hurt financially. Pricing for the new version wasn't announced. (Paraphrase)
