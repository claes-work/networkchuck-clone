# The Hacker's Roadmap (How to Get Started in IT in 2025)

## Summary

Chuck's updated (2025) roadmap for breaking into IT and reaching a cybersecurity /
ethical-hacking career, his first refresh of this video in two years (prior version 2023).
The core thesis is unchanged: this is a fast path to an IT job and eventually a "dream job,"
six figures is reachable, and **no college is required**. Phases zero through three are
foundational for everyone in IT; after that the learner picks a specialty, and Chuck deep-dives
the one he's personally pursuing right now — offensive security / red teaming (ethical hacking,
pen testing).

The biggest structural change from 2023 is the addition of **Phase Zero: apply for jobs
immediately, before learning anything**, because experience is the single most valuable thing
on a resume. Throughout every phase he layers in three recurring habits: keep applying for jobs,
build a home lab (each project is a "resume building moment"), and network socially. He also
threads Linux and Python learning through every phase as optional-but-recommended "superpowers."
The video ends with hacker John Hammond reviewing and largely endorsing the roadmap, plus a
section on shortcuts, adapting the roadmap to jobs you land, and when a college degree makes
sense (WGU-style programs that bake certs into the curriculum).

## Key claims (dated — 2025-01-28; the full roadmap)

**Framing / what's unchanged**
- (2025) It's been two years since his last IT roadmap (i.e., the 2023 version); some things
  changed but the goal is the same: get a job in IT fast, on a clear path to a dream job.
- (2025) Six figures is "absolutely within reach" faster than you'd expect. No gimmicks, no
  shortcuts — hard work on a clear path, and **no college needed**.
- (2025) Phases one through three are foundational for everyone in IT no matter the eventual path;
  after phase three "the world is your oyster" and you pick a specialty.
- (2025) The roadmap is "hacker approved" — John Hammond reviewed and endorsed it.
- (2025) You don't have to follow the roadmap strictly in order; you may find shortcuts (covered later).

**Phase Zero — Apply for jobs right now (new for 2025)**
- (2025) Start applying for entry-level IT help desk / technical support jobs immediately, even
  with zero IT skills — "you don't need IT skills to get that first job in IT."
- (2025) Entry-level help desk listings often look demanding (e.g., a Houston help-desk spec asking
  for a year of experience + technical skills) but what they really want is excellent customer
  service skills.
- (2025) Chuck got his own first IT job with no certifications and no real IT skills — just fast-food
  job experience and hunger to learn and prove value.
- (2025) Help desk is the normal first IT job (entry level). You can try to skip it — he encourages
  applying for jobs above it too — but for a fresh beginner help desk is an amazing place to learn.
- (2025) Tailor your resume "for what you want to do, not what you have done." Reframe non-IT jobs in
  IT terms (his Starbucks example: he put "fixed their printer all the time" on his resume) and be
  upfront about what you're studying/working toward.
- (2025) Rationale: **experience is king / gold** — nothing looks better on a resume, and on-the-job
  learning beats anything you study. Get the job first.
- (2025) Deliverables: polish resume + make LinkedIn look great, start applying right after the video
  (takes a couple hours), maintain a regular applying habit until hired. Failing interviews is fine —
  it teaches you and builds contacts.
- (2025) Footnote habit: start **social networking** — follow IT people on Twitter, join Discords,
  talk to peers who are studying and to mentors above you. "It's not what, it's who." Most of his own
  jobs came through relationships.
- (2025) Don't move on to phase one until you've applied for at least two jobs.

**Phase One — Foundations / core IT skills (~2–3 months)**
- (2025) Recommended first cert: **CompTIA A+** — the "help desk certification" that lands the first job.
  It's an "IT buffet" that samples many paths (programming, networking, etc.) so you discover what you like.
- (2025) A NEW A+ version is launching **Spring 2025**.
- (2025) A+ is two exams, each **$253** (current exams; 2025 pricing TBD). It's a lot of info; Chuck
  himself failed one exam and had to pay to retake. It's hard for most beginners.
- (2025) The cert is technically optional — you can learn the knowledge free — but the cert's value is
  that it **proves** knowledge and gets you past the HR filter. CompTIA "has done the marketing" so HR
  recognizes the cert. TLDR: you need the knowledge; the cert is optional but helps get the first job.
- (2025) A+ study resources: free — **Professor Messer** (full A+ course on YouTube); paid — **ITProTV**
  (now "ITPro"), and Chuck's own in-progress **Academy** (~$12/month at time of video), which will include
  a full A+ course covering the 2025 exams.
- (2025) Goal of phase one: get A+ or at least equivalent knowledge — but there's more (new vs. 2023).
- (2025) OPTIONAL (so you're not overwhelmed): while studying A+, **start learning Linux and coding**.
  For coding, either Python or Go — **Python is the favorite, so go with Python.** A+ stays the main focus.
- (2025) Suggested cadence: e.g. one day a week (Saturdays), 30 min Linux + 30 min Python, or an hour/two
  hours each — "as long as you do something." Resources: his free "Linux for Hackers" and "Python for
  Everyone" courses on YouTube.
- (2025) Also in phase one: **start building a home lab** — do the things, don't just memorize theory.
  Examples: install VirtualBox + a VM, install Ubuntu (Linux practice), install a router / replace home
  router with **pfSense**, install a DNS server. Most can be done free.
- (2025) Home-lab projects are "resume building moments" — put real projects (e.g., "deployed a DNS server
  in my home") on your resume; a home lab signals passion and drive, which employers love and find hard to
  hire for. Keep applying for jobs and refining the resume throughout.
- (2025) You can do phase-one items in order or in parallel; if all you manage is the A+, that's fine.
  Estimated ~2–3 months with a consistent study schedule.

**Phase Two — "Dipping your toes in the security water" (DIBS) (~2–3 months)**
- (2025) Focal point: earn **CompTIA Security+**. Security is part of every IT role, so everyone benefits.
  Phase two isn't deep security — just enough knowledge + credentials to land a great job.
- (2025) Security+ does two things: (1) a "security buffet" of knowledge that surfaces cybersecurity roles/paths
  you didn't know existed; (2) it's a **checkbox cert** — many companies require it, and it satisfies the
  **DoD 8570 directive** (minimum requirements for IT/cyber roles), making it the easiest way to check that
  box, especially for government work.
- (2025) Security+ costs **$404**, one exam. Again, knowledge is learnable without the cert, but the cert
  looks great on a resume.
- (2025) Study resources: free — **Professor Messer** (full Security+ course on YouTube); paid — **ITPro**
  (not a sponsor; link is an affiliate link — no extra cost to you, small kickback to Chuck).
- (2025) Linux becomes "very, very important" here. Do **security-focused Linux**: the free site
  **OverTheWire**, specifically the **Bandit** path, teaches tons of Linux basics.
- (2025) More Python: keep learning basics, then build a project for your resume — e.g., a simple
  **network scanner** that scans your network for open ports using Python.
- (2025) Don't stress the Linux/Python side — focus is Security+ — but 1–2 hours/week (more if you can) on
  Linux/Python is better. Keep doing home-lab work (automate things with Linux/Python, find fun YouTube
  projects) and keep applying for jobs / updating the resume. ~2–3 months.
- (2025) He's intentionally vague on exactly how much Linux/Python/home-lab to do, to avoid overwhelming
  people; may make a more detailed roadmap video if requested.

**Phase Three — Networking (his favorite) (~2–4 months, leaning 4 for CCNA)**
- (2025) Networking is "the bread and butter of IT" — don't skip it.
- (2025) Focus cert, two options:
  - **Cisco CCNA** — his favorite; "one of the best certifications you can get," the gold standard employers
    love. Harder, more study, but more valuable *because* it's harder and demonstrates practical skills.
    Cisco-focused: you learn networking inside-out plus Cisco CLI, automation, and security products. The
    no-brainer if going down network engineer / network admin path.
  - **CompTIA Network+** — vendor-neutral alternative, very popular, still teaches core networking (router,
    switch, subnetting). Good if CCNA is too much for your intended path.
- (2025) Both look great on a resume; CCNA makes you look better, and knowing Cisco helps because Cisco is
  everywhere.
- (2025) Pricing: **Network+ = $369** (one exam); **CCNA = $300** — so CCNA is the better deal despite being harder.
- (2025) Study resources: Network+ — Professor Messer (free), ITPro (paid). CCNA — free: **Jeremy's IT Labs**
  on YouTube (full CCNA course, Jeremy is a friend, lives in Japan); paid: Chuck's Academy, where he's
  partnered with **Jeremy Cioara** (a CCIE) — the man who taught Chuck networking and how to teach — who built
  "the best CCNA course he's ever made." (Chuck notes this is the first public announcement of the partnership.)
- (2025) Keep layering Linux/Python, made **networking-focused** here — network automation is a big part of
  CCNA and largely means using Python to log into routers/switches and automate. ~2–3 hours/week.
- (2025) Home-lab challenge (homework): actually replace your home network — buy old Cisco gear off eBay, or
  install a **pfSense** router + **UniFi**, then monitor your network and internet quality, "be really nerdy
  about it," and add it to the resume. Keep applying and networking socially.
- (2025) Timeline depends on cert choice: CCNA takes longer — estimate **2–4 months**, leaning 4 for CCNA
  (aggressive; extend if needed, just be consistent).

**Phase 3.5 — "Get that job"**
- (2025) If you don't have a job by now, make full-time job-hunting your focus — after phases 1–3 you have
  more than enough to land one. Keep learning/labbing/applying/interviewing/networking.
- (2025) At 3.5 you can competitively apply for many entry-level, beginner, and even intermediate jobs — with
  CCNA + Security+ + A+ + Python + Linux + home-lab projects you look really good.

**After 3.5 — Pick a path; the hacking (red team) deep-dive**
- (2025) After phase 3.5 there are "a billion" paths — security, networking, sysadmin, cloud (learn AWS etc.).
  He can't cover them all; he covers the one he's on now: **offensive security / red teaming** (ethical hacking,
  pen testing) — the flashy, most popular cyber path but just one of many.
- (2025) Hacking uses phase 1–3 skills in reverse: instead of build/protect/defend, you learn to break — a
  different mindset. He'd target three certs in sequence:
  1. **Junior cert** — **eJPT** or **PenTest+**. eJPT = **$249 including 3 months training via INE** (who run
     the eJPT), ~two attempts. PenTest+ = **$381 not including training**, but more well-known / resume-friendly.
     Between the two he'd choose the **eJPT**.
  2. **CPTS (Certified Penetration Testing Specialist) from Hack The Box** — the one he's working on now.
     Requires going through Hack The Box Academy modules. HTB is *the* gamified place to practice hacking.
     Pricing ~**$490** (he believes voucher-only, likely not including training). Fairly new, so "not quite
     resume gold" yet.
  3. **OSCP** — "probably the best hacking certification you can put on your resume," long the gold standard.
     Official training is **PEN-200**; exam bundle ~**$1,600** including the course, 90 days lab access, and one
     exam attempt. Most practical, if not the most difficult. On his own to-get list, but he's doing CPTS first
     to ease up to it.
- (2025) He says only **one** of these hacking certs (implying the OSCP) truly makes people go "oh, they know
  what they're talking about."
- (2025) This phase is Linux-heavy and potentially Python-heavy; you'll likely need a lot of both for the exams.
- (2025) **Notable AI update: OSCP recently began allowing ChatGPT during the exam**, recognizing that modern
  engineers/hackers have AI right there.
- (2025) This is just the beginning of a red-team journey but he thinks it's enough to get a pen-tester job.
  Start applying immediately for what you want; keep studying while applying.
- (2025) For the hacking path, sprinkle in **CTFs (capture the flag)** throughout — free **picoCTF** is very
  beginner-friendly; CTFs are how hackers practice (exploit a vulnerability to capture a hidden flag).

**Shortcuts / adapting the roadmap**
- (2025) Beginners should follow the roadmap in order initially, but non-beginners can skip phases they've
  already mastered (e.g., already know Linux/Python, can build a PC, have a home lab → just apply and move on).
- (2025) Certs exist to *prove* knowledge, not gain it — you don't need them for the knowledge, but you do need
  them to prove it in most cases.
- (2025) Higher certs subsume lower ones on a resume: if you skip A+/Network+ and go straight to CCNA, no one
  cares about the missing lower certs — they assume passing knowledge.
- (2025) Landing a job can rewrite the roadmap for you — e.g., getting hired into a SOC analyst or NOC role can
  let you skip help desk, and the employer may push you toward a specialist cert like **CCNA CyberOps** (blue-team
  focused — not otherwise covered in this video). Adapt to opportunities; take shortcuts into jobs you're "not
  ready for" and learn on the job — that's what most people do. (Shortcut to a *job* yes; never a shortcut to
  *knowledge*.)

**College / degrees**
- (2025) Chuck didn't feature college on the roadmap because he's a fan of this cert path and has no degree /
  didn't need one — but degrees can be valuable and open doors (time-consuming, expensive doors).
- (2025) Caution: many IT degrees don't include the certs in the curriculum, so grads still need CCNA / Security+
  afterward. His advice: pick a degree program that **bakes certs into the curriculum** — e.g., **WGU (Western
  Governors University)**, where many IT degrees include CCNA, A+, and AWS cloud certs as part of the coursework/exams.
  More expensive than self-study, but you graduate with degree + certs.

**John Hammond's review (guest — see attribution)**
- (2025) Hammond "1000% agrees" that you should study Linux and Python early and keep doing it throughout —
  though it needn't be strictly Python; command line + scripting/automating in some language is the superpower.
- (2025) On networking, Hammond admits he doesn't know what CCNA "goes darn" is / may be out of the loop; from a
  hacking/cyber standpoint he says knowing what IP addresses are, CIDR, class C/B, networks and subnets is roughly
  enough — you don't need to architect VLANs / whole networks.
- (2025) On hacking certs, Hammond isn't sure about eJPT's current status and hasn't taken PenTest+, so won't speak
  with authority, but thinks either junior cert is a good choice (PenTest+ gives more "runway" as a well-known
  CompTIA "plus" cert). He still considers **OSCP the crown jewel** — drive toward it as your target, with CPTS or
  others as stepping stones.
- (2025) On college, Hammond "kind of agrees with Chuck": optional, not strictly needed, but can be extremely
  helpful — "crawl, walk, run" fine without a degree; a degree is an extra notch / speed boost.

**Closing**
- (2025) Times are changing rapidly with AI; no one knows where jobs go, but you need skills, and these
  foundational skills are still hired for and likely in demand a long time. This was his first video of 2025.
  He asks which other roadmaps to cover (cloud engineer, network engineer, more blue team).

watch when reconciling with older pages:

  roadmaps front-loaded certs/skills.

  parallel track (earlier positions treated them as later/optional).

  is updated (A+ $253/exam, Security+ $404, Network+ $369, CCNA $300).
