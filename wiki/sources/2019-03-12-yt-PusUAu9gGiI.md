# CCNA Cyber Ops vs CCNA Security

## Summary

A Monday-motivation episode in which Chuck Keith answers a viewer question (from a
commenter named Fernando) about the difference between two Cisco security tracks that
existed in 2019: the **CCNA Cyber Ops** and the **CCNA Security**. He walks through the
exam prerequisites, the exam topics/codes, and the job roles each certification targets,
then gives his recommendation.

Key structural points he draws out:
- **CCNA Security** requires a prerequisite (CCENT / CCNA Routing & Switching / any CCIE)
  and is **one exam** (210-260 IINS). It targets an entry-level network **security admin**
  role and feeds naturally into CCNP Security → CCIE Security.
- **CCNA Cyber Ops** has **no prerequisites** and is **two exams** (210-250 SECFND
  fundamentals + 210-255 SECOPS). It targets a **Security Operations Center (SOC) analyst**
  role — correlating security data, incident handling, forensics, compliance frameworks.

His verdict: go for **CCNA Security** first, because Cyber Ops is a dead-end track (no
CCNP Cyber Ops exists) and CCNA Security is more marketable/recognized by recruiters.

> ⚠️ Date note: Both certifications discussed here were **later retired by Cisco** in the
> February 2020 certification overhaul. CCNA Security, CCNA Cyber Ops, CCENT, and the
> separate CCNA tracks were consolidated into a single CCNA plus the CyberOps
> Associate/Professional certs. This page reflects Chuck's advice **as of 2019-03-12** and
> should not be read as current cert guidance.

The episode also includes a news segment (Google Chronicle "Backstory" for network
telemetry/log analysis; the NSA open-sourcing its **Ghidra** malware reverse-engineering
tool at RSA) and cross-promotion of his podcast "This is IT" (interviews with Stuart on
extreme ownership, Bart Castle on AWS, Jason Gooley "godfather of network
programmability").

## Key claims (dated)

All dated 2019-03-12 (paraphrase):

- CCNA Security requires the CCENT (or CCNA Routing & Switching, or any CCIE) as a
  prerequisite and is a single exam (210-260 IINS).
- CCNA Cyber Ops has no prerequisites and consists of two required exams: 210-250 (SECFND,
  Cyber Security Fundamentals) and 210-255 (SECOPS).
- The SECFND exam opens with network concepts but leans toward security, then covers
  security concepts, cryptography, host-based analysis (HIPS), monitoring, and attack
  methods.
- The SECOPS exam goes deeper into computer forensics, correlating/analyzing attacks,
  incident response, incident handling, and compliance frameworks (PCI, SOX, HIPAA).
- CCNA Security overlaps on some topics but skips network concepts (covered by CCENT),
  omits much of the cryptography and incident-response material, and focuses on firewalls
  (implementing Cisco firewalls), layer-2 attacks, and mitigation.
- CCNA Security targets an entry-level network security admin role; CCNA Cyber Ops targets
  a specific SOC-analyst role ("the guy in front of a lot of monitors" correlating data).
- Chuck's recommendation: choose CCNA Security over Cyber Ops "for now," because Cyber Ops
  is the end of its track — there is no CCNP Cyber Ops, and Cisco may never make one (he
  cites CCNA Industrial as a track that never got a CCNP).
- You get a certification for two reasons: (1) the knowledge, and (2) marketability on your
  resume/LinkedIn so recruiters and jobs can find you.
- A job search showed CCNA Cyber Ops does appear in listings, but usually as an
  interchangeable "CCNA Cyber Ops OR CCNA Security OR CCNP Security OR Security+" — so it is
  an either/or scenario, not a required cert.
- Security+ exam objectives overlap heavily with Cyber Ops and in fact cover more breadth;
  Cyber Ops may go deeper on specific cryptography/incident response but not deep enough to
  make a hardcore SOC analyst — most of that is learned on the job.
- Nothing stops you from getting both; but if it is either/or, go CCNA Security then jump
  straight to CCNP Security rather than detouring to Cyber Ops.
- Chuck's stance (stated as long-held): everyone should get CCNA Routing & Switching before
  any other track, the way Cisco used to require it (he had to get R&S before CCNA Voice).
- News: Google's parent Alphabet has a company, Chronicle, whose new tool "Backstory"
  stores unlimited network telemetry/logs (syslog, firewall data from Palo Alto/ASA) and
  uses AI/machine learning to surface security breaches — competing with Cisco Stealthwatch
  and Palo Alto tooling.
- News: The NSA released its malware reverse-engineering tool, Ghidra, as open source (both
  announced at the RSA Conference).
