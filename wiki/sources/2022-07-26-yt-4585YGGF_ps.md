# talking with HakLuke (Hacker and creator of Hakrawler and other tools)

## Summary

An interview conducted by Chuck (NetworkChuck) with guest **HakLuke** (Luke Stephens) —
an Australian bug-bounty hunter, penetration tester, and creator of the recon tool
**Hakrawler** (a fast Go-based web crawler) and roughly 20 other released hacking tools.
The video frames a walkthrough of installing and using Hakrawler around a conversation
about HakLuke's career path, why he builds his own tooling, the difference between bug
bounty and penetration testing, running your own cybersecurity business, and the value
of open-sourcing your work.

Nearly all technical and career content is **HakLuke's** (guest context). Chuck's own
contributions are his framing, questions, and reflections drawn from his own experience
running a business and creating content — those are the only parts that train the
persona.

## Key claims (dated — labeled guest vs Chuck)

- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** Hakrawler is a web crawler: you feed it one or many
  websites, and it recursively follows the links it finds down to a depth you specify,
  surfacing links, JavaScript files, and interesting parameters to start a hacking
  process from. (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** He built Hakrawler to be **fast** so it can be run
  across many targets at once (e.g. a target with ~100 web servers), giving broad
  coverage with minimal manual work. (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** Career arc: started in **web development**, moved
  to the "dark side" as a **penetration tester**, got into **bug bounties** to keep
  hacking at night, then worked at **Bugcrowd** as a manager hiring and training a team
  of triagers, and for the ~13 months before this recording has run his own
  cybersecurity consultancy called **Haksec** (pen testing plus, more recently, hacker
  content marketing for security organizations). (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** He noticed most cybersecurity organizations are bad
  at content marketing, so he added a service producing good security content for them.
  (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** He is his own boss, sets his own hours, and made the
  move mostly for flexibility and freedom (relevant with a second child on the way).
  (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** Working for yourself gives more meaning because you
  reap the benefits directly; the incentive is more tangible than a fixed salary.
  (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** He makes his own tools to "scratch his own itch,"
  usually spending no more than a day or two per tool, automating repetitive or
  un-fun tasks — he calls it "being lazy." (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** He uses **Go (golang)** rather than Python mainly
  for its **native concurrency** (important for running across many targets) and because
  compiled tools are a bit faster; he knows Python well but prefers compiled Go.
  (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** Bug bounty vs pen testing differ in the **incentive
  model**: pen testing pays for your time regardless of what you find; bug bounties pay
  per bug (e.g. a $5k–$10k payout for a good critical), which he finds far more
  addictive. Pen testing rewards **coverage**; bug bounty rewards **digging deep** into
  technical detail on already-tested applications. (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** Pen testing is fairly repetitive; most of his tests
  are web applications, and the core tooling is a good HTTP proxy — **Burp Suite**,
  **Caido**, or **ZAP** — where you can view and edit requests; his own tools are added
  convenience on top. (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** He has ~98 GitHub repos (many are forks); roughly 20
  are tools he actually released, almost all open source. GitHub handle is `hakluke`
  (spelled h-a-k-l-u-k-e). (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** Hakrawler install options: via `go`, via Docker, or
  via `apt` on Kali Linux; he recommends the `go` install for the most up-to-date
  version because the apt package is out of date. (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** Advice: **just start / just do it** — people are
  scared to release code, blogs, or videos fearing it gets picked apart, but "nobody
  cares if it sucks"; open-source everything so others learn from your learning.
  (paraphrase)
- **[GUEST — HakLuke, 2022-07-26]** His company is him plus a rotating group of ~10–15
  subcontractors (no full-time hires); most of the work is admin and email, not the
  "fun stuff." (paraphrase)
- **[CHUCK, 2022-07-26]** Chuck tested Hakrawler before the call and found it "insanely
  fast." (paraphrase)
- **[CHUCK, 2022-07-26]** Chuck relates that he quit his own job to work for himself and
  frames it as scary but the most fun thing in the world — you feel like a boss because
  you are the boss. (paraphrase — subject data)
- **[CHUCK, 2022-07-26]** Chuck's framing: you don't have to be a coder to be a hacker or
  do bug bounty, but being able to code your own tools makes you better and is an asset;
  he wishes he knew more coding. (paraphrase — subject data)
- **[CHUCK, 2022-07-26]** Chuck echoes the "put yourself out there" message: your work
  won't be perfect and someone will always pick it apart, but it's the **ideas** that
  stand and get improved by others; he says his own GitHub is "full of garbage" but he
  put himself out there anyway. (paraphrase — subject data)
- **[CHUCK, 2022-07-26]** Chuck notes that running a company is mainly admin — most of his
  own time is emailing and managing people, and only ~20 minutes is actually recording a
  video. (paraphrase — subject data)
- **[CHUCK, 2022-07-26]** Chuck found a bug: the only Hakrawler Docker Hub image was for
  ARM architecture, not AMD; he offered to file a pull request and worked around it by
  building in a Docker container. (paraphrase)
