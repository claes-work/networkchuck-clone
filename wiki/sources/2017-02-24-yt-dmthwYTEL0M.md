# CCNA or COLLEGE? - Become a Network Engineer

## Summary
Chuck weighs a four-year college degree against IT certifications (primarily the CCNA) for someone trying to break into or accelerate an IT career. He compares the two across three categories — cost, time, and worth — and concludes the CCNA wins all three for jumping into networking/sysadmin work fast. He stresses that experience is the true differentiator, recommends starting on a help desk at a small-to-medium company, and grounds the argument in his own path from help desk to network engineer without a completed degree.

## Key claims
- (2017-02-24) The core question when starting an IT career is "college or CCNA?", and for most people the underlying goal is simply more money via career acceleration.
- (2017-02-24) He evaluates the choice across three categories: cost, time, and worth.
- (2017-02-24) Cost: a four-year degree runs roughly $6,000/year (accelerated online like WGU) up to $9,000–$40,000/year (brick-and-mortar or online schools like University of Phoenix); a self-study CCNA totals about $1,790 including premium materials (CBT Nuggets ~$1,000/year or ~$84/month, official cert guide library ~$60, lab kit ~$400, exam ~$330). Roughly $2,000 for CCNA vs ~$12,000+ for a bachelor's — CCNA wins on cost.
- (2017-02-24) You can cut CCNA cost further using GNS3 (needs Cisco IOS) instead of a physical lab and skipping books; two-to-three months of month-by-month CBT Nuggets is enough for some.
- (2017-02-24) Time: a bachelor's takes 2–4 years (2 if accelerated with prior credit); a CCNA from scratch is achievable in ~6 months, or 2–3 months for someone with existing IT experience, with a year being a generous worst case when life gets in the way — CCNA wins on time.
- (2017-02-24) Worth: a four-year IT degree shows general knowledge but not job-ready task ability; universities have always struggled to match curricula to workforce needs, while vendor certifications update much faster and keep you current.
- (2017-02-24) A four-year degree will realistically only land an entry-level help desk position — the same role a high school graduate can get; it will not by itself land a network admin or sysadmin job.
- (2017-02-24) A CCNA can land a low-level networking job (he has seen it happen), though it is difficult without experience; job postings frequently say "CCNA preferred" and "four-year degree or equivalent experience."
- (2017-02-24) Experience is king — nothing guarantees an interview more than experience in the role you're applying for. To escape the "need experience to get experience" trap, start at the bottom on a help desk.
- (2017-02-24) He recommends targeting a small-to-medium company: big enough to run Cisco/Microsoft enterprise equipment, small enough to avoid red tape so you can get hands-on and move up.
- (2017-02-24) A degree may help you land a help desk role but does little to help you rise; a CCNA both lands the role and drives promotion because leadership and admins take notice once you start certifying.
- (2017-02-24) His overall verdict: college is genuinely valuable and a legitimate option (especially straight out of high school), but for breaking into IT fast without heavy debt, certifications are the best bet — you can out-earn many degree holders.
- (2017-02-24) He notes some universities (WGU, University of Phoenix) now bundle certifications into their degrees, which adds value to the degree.
- (2017-02-24) He points to his own prior video "Seven CCNA/CCNP study tips for the new year" as a starting point, and suggests classroom options (Cisco Networking Academy, community college CCNA courses) for those who don't do well with self-study.
