# we're out of IP Addresses….but this saved us (Private IP Addresses)

## Summary

An episode of NetworkChuck's "you suck at subnetting" series covering the two mechanisms
that rescued IPv4 from address exhaustion: **private IP addresses** (RFC 1918) and
**NAT** (Network Address Translation). Chuck frames it as a 1996 crisis — the internet is
"doomed, we're out of IP addresses" — and casts RFC 1918 as the "unlikely hero" and
"giant band-aid." He explains that IPv4 offers ~4.3 billion addresses, that the designers
organized them poorly, and that not every device can get a unique **public** IP (an IP
reachable on the internet, which must be globally unique like a phone number). The fix:
carve private ranges out of the Class A/B/C public space, keep them non-routable on the
internet, and let millions of home networks reuse the same private addresses. NAT
(performed by the home router, his running "Oprah" metaphor) then translates all a home's
private IPs to the single public IP the ISP loans out, tracking which internal device a
returning packet belongs to. He closes by noting that even with private IPs + NAT, IPv4
still ran out — leading to **IPv6** (2^128 addresses vs. IPv4's 2^32), which cellular
carriers are already adopting — while stressing IPv4 and subnetting remain essential to
learn. Sponsors: Dashlane (password manager) and Boson (practice exams/labs); the deeper
NAT mechanics are deferred to his CCNA series.

## Key claims (dated — concepts)

_All 2022-04-18, paraphrase unless quoted._

- IPv4 provides roughly 4.3 billion addresses, and the internet effectively ran out of
  them; the address space was organized poorly by the internet's designers.
- **RFC 1918** defined **private IP addresses** as the solution to IPv4 exhaustion — a
  "band-aid" that made the modern internet possible.
- A **public IP address** is one reachable on the internet and must be globally unique;
  two devices cannot share a public IP (analogy: two people can't share one phone number).
- The authorities took chunks out of the Class A, B, and C public ranges and reserved
  them as private — those addresses can no longer be used publicly.
- Private ranges keep the same default subnet masks as their class counterparts.
- The most common private range home routers use is **192.168.x.x** (a Class C range),
  which is why "everyone has it" — e.g. 192.168.1.0.
- Private IP addresses are, by nature, **not unique** — millions of people/homes can use
  the same private addresses simultaneously, unlike public IPs.
- Private IP addresses are **not publicly routable**; you cannot reach them from the
  internet, so a device with only a private IP can't directly access the internet.
- **NAT (Network Address Translation)** is the second half of the band-aid and is what
  actually lets private-addressed devices reach the internet.
- An ISP typically loans a home just **one public IP address**; NAT lets that single
  public IP serve all the home's devices (Chuck cites 200–300+ devices in his own home).
- The **router** performs NAT: it translates a device's private IP to the shared public
  IP on outbound traffic, and tracks the reverse so returning packets are forwarded to the
  correct internal device.
- To all external services (Netflix, YouTube, a web server), every device on a home
  network appears as the single public IP the ISP assigned.
- Practical demo: Google "what is my IP address" shows the one public IP; `ipconfig`
  shows the device's private IP (Chuck's is 192.168.1.204).
- Even with private IPs + NAT, IPv4 addresses still ran out — motivating **IPv6**.
- IPv6 provides **2^128** addresses versus IPv4's **2^32**; big enough to give every
  device a unique public address with plenty left over. ("We skipped five" — went 4 to 6.)
- Cellular carriers (AT&T, Bell, Orange, etc.) already assign phones **public** IPs, often
  IPv6; a phone on cellular (not Wi-Fi) has a public IP.
- The industry is shifting to IPv6, but most websites still use IPv4; **IPv4 is going
  nowhere** and remains worth learning (Chuck predicts useful for "at least the next 50
  years").
