# HACK your IT Study Habits - CCENT - CCNA - A+ | Atomic Habits

## Summary

Chuck applies James Clear's book *Atomic Habits* to studying for IT certifications
(CCENT, CCNA, A+, Security+, MCSA). His thesis: losing motivation a week or two into
studying is normal, and the fix is NOT "try harder / get more discipline / discipline
equals freedom" but building a **system** of small habits that carry you through the
months-long grind of certification study. He credits David Bombal (via David's review)
for turning him onto the book, and reframes the book's central idea for IT learners: a
cert isn't earned in a day or a week — it's tiny study sessions accumulated over ~six
months.

He walks through James Clear's **habit loop** — cue, craving, response, reward — and the
four corresponding "laws" (make it obvious, make it attractive, make it easy, make it
satisfying), giving each an IT-study-specific application. Throughout he frames the whole
approach as "hacking your brain" / "tricking yourself" into good habits rather than
relying on willpower. He also cites Keith Barker (interviewed on the "This Is IT" show
with David Bombal) for the motivation-ritual / anchoring technique. The video repeatedly
returns to Chuck's personal signature habit: coffee as his motivation ritual before
studying, labbing, or making videos.

## Key claims (dated — 2019-04-24)

Framework attribution note: the four-law habit-loop structure and all named techniques
below (habit loop, implementation intentions, habit stacking, environment priming,
temptation bundling, motivation ritual, law of least effort / reducing friction,
inversion for bad habits, decisive moment, two-minute rule, "what is immediately rewarded
is repeated") come from **James Clear's book *Atomic Habits***. The **IT-study
applications and personal examples are Chuck's**.

- Traditional advice ("become more disciplined, get more grit, discipline equals freedom,
  just try harder") is NOT the real answer to losing study motivation. [Chuck's framing]
- A goal without a system in place will fail; goals are essential but insufficient. [Book]
- A habit is a behavior that becomes automatic; an "atomic habit" is a tiny change that
  yields marginal gains which compound over the long run into big results. [Book]
- IT certification study is inherently atomic-habit-shaped: a CCNA is not done in a day or
  two weeks — it's tiny study sessions over ~six months. [Chuck's application]
- The book is less about grit/discipline and more about analyzing why we do what we do and
  hacking/tricking our routines into making us better people and engineers. [Chuck's read]

**Cue — "make it obvious" (three sub-techniques):**
- **Implementation intentions**: define the WHERE, WHEN, and WHAT of studying (e.g., "in
  the morning after coffee, in my office"). The biggest pitfall is leaving study time to
  chance — "I'll study today" without a fixed time/place usually means it won't happen.
- **Habit stacking**: pile a new habit onto an existing one (Chuck's example: make coffee
  → then watch a CBT Nuggets video or do a Packet Tracer lab). Can be chained into a
  sequence (dinner → plate in sink → brew coffee → chat with spouse → walk into office →
  study).
- **Environment priming**: design your environment so studying is easy; you are a creature
  of your environment (kitchen smells make you hungry; a TV on makes you binge-watch).
  Define a dedicated study place so your mind shifts into work mode — Chuck's studio is
  literally a closet; a coffee shop or library also works. Don't study where you eat,
  sleep, or relax (studying on the couch/bed fails ~9 times out of 10).

**Craving — "make it attractive" (three sub-techniques):**
- **Temptation bundling**: pair something you want to do with something you don't (e.g.,
  study 25 min → then play video games 15 min); the brain reframes studying as positive.
  Chuck ties this to the **Pomodoro method** (25 min focused work + 5 min break; after
  ~3 sessions, reward with ~30 min of Call of Duty). [Pomodoro is Chuck's own habitual tool]
- **Surround yourself with people who share your values** — study groups, events like
  Cisco Live, motivated coworkers (sit with engineers who study on lunch, not the ones
  goofing off). "You're the sum of the people you hang out with most." Online options:
  Chuck's Facebook "NetworkChuck CCNA study group" (~14,000 members at the time).
- **Motivation ritual**: do something you enjoy right before something you must do, to get
  in the right frame of mind. Chuck's ritual = brewing coffee (the smell alone makes him
  want to open a book, lab, or study Python). Attributed also to Keith Barker's anchoring
  technique (see below).

**Response — "make it easy":**
- Humans default to the easier path — the **law of least effort**.
- **Reduce friction** to start studying: pre-load your lab environment / Packet Tracer,
  bookmark the page, and have a **study plan** so you know exactly what to study that day
  (not knowing what to study next often kills the session). Example plan: "today = OSPF —
  read two chapters at lunch, watch two CBT Nuggets, do an OSPF lab at night."
- **Inversion — add friction to bad habits**: turn off Netflix autoplay; unplug the PS4
  and put it in the closet with your CCNA book on top.
- **The decisive moment**: the first few decisions of the day snowball; master the first
  moments (work out, eat right, Chuck adds devotion/Bible/prayer) and the day compounds
  in a good direction — and vice versa.
- **Two-minute rule**: start a new habit tiny (don't declare "I'll study six hours a day"
  — it never sticks). Commit to just one small thing (one 5-min CBT Nuggets video, five
  minutes of labbing); starting small usually leads to doing more. Even 5–10 spare minutes
  = flashcards, review OSPF LSA types, a few subnetting questions. Expand the habit only
  after mastering the small version. "A little bit of progress every day is a lot better
  than no progress."
- **Use technology/automation** to enforce habits: IFTTT to shut off the smart TV at 10pm;
  a timed access list on your router to block family Netflix.

**Reward — "make it satisfying":**
- Immediately reward yourself after completing the habit (Pomodoro → video games). The
  reward must be immediate because humans gravitate to immediate over delayed rewards
  (Chuck's weight-loss/brownie example: the long-term "ripped Captain America" goal loses
  to the cake in the moment).
- James Clear's principle: **"what is immediately rewarded is repeated."** [Book]
- Chuck's personal reward: at the end of a productive day he lets himself binge-watch TV
  with family only if he's completed a set number of things.

- Closing intent: Chuck says he'll apply these atomic habits to losing weight, producing
  more content, cert study, and learning Python. [Chuck, self-reported]
