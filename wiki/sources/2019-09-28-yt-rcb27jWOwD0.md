# How WiFi 6 (REALLY) works — Is it a SWITCH? Feat. the Clear to Send Podcast

## Summary

An interview/collab in which Chuck brings on Rowell Dionicio and François Vergès, the
hosts of the **Clear to Send** WiFi podcast, to settle whether WiFi 6 (802.11ax) "is a
switch." Chuck had made an earlier video comparing WiFi 6 to a network switch and drew
heavy backlash from the WiFi community (including a response video). His framing here is
half-joking provocateur — he keeps insisting WiFi 6 *is* a switch — while the two guests
(the actual subject-matter experts) methodically explain why it is not, and walk through
the real 802.11ax mechanics: OFDMA, multi-user MIMO, Target Wake Time, BSS Coloring,
1024-QAM, and 8 spatial streams. The back half covers how to break into wireless as a
career (CWNP certification, Ekahau, Cisco wireless tracks) and whether it is lucrative.

Almost all technical content is spoken by the guests and is **context, not persona
material**. Only Chuck's questions, framing, analogies-under-fire, and the video's intro
train the persona.

## Key claims (dated 2019-09-28)

- **[Chuck — framing]** Chuck stands by his earlier claim that "WiFi 6 is a switch," used
  as a running comedic bit throughout the interview; he treats the disagreement as a
  friendly "battle" to be settled by experts.
- **[Guest]** WiFi 6 is **not a switch**; if compared to anything wired it is more like a
  very fast **hub**, because wireless is **half-duplex** and everyone shares the same
  contention medium — clients and APs still have to contend for airtime.
- **[Guest]** The features that make people *think* it is switch-like are the multi-user
  features: **multi-user MIMO** and **multi-user OFDMA**.
- **[Guest]** **Multi-user MIMO** uses multiple antennas/streams to send different data to
  different clients simultaneously, but in practice needs physical separation between
  clients so the AP can beamform and distinguish them; it is hard when users are
  congregated, adds sounding-packet overhead when clients move, and support varies widely
  across client chipsets/drivers — so the experts de-emphasize it.
- **[Guest]** A switch trivially identifies each user by MAC address per port; wireless
  MU-MIMO cannot pinpoint who is who nearly as easily — a key reason it is not a switch.
- **[Guest]** **OFDMA** (borrowed from cellular) is the real "switch-like" feature: it
  splits a channel (e.g. a 20 MHz channel) into smaller sub-channels called **resource
  units (RUs)** and assigns them to different clients at once, instead of one client taking
  the full channel width per transmission. Analogy (guest): a road where a motorcycle no
  longer needs the full road width — you fit several vehicles side by side.
- **[Guest]** WiFi 6 is about **high efficiency**, not raw peak speed. Benefits show up in
  **high-density environments** (conferences, stadiums, apartments) and only when the
  **clients are also WiFi 6**; a lone WiFi 6 client on a WiFi 6 AP just gets the whole
  channel and sees little OFDMA benefit.
- **[Guest]** Downlink OFDMA is easy (the AP schedules RUs); **uplink OFDMA** is harder —
  clients must transmit synchronized in time on assigned RUs. Vendor implementations vary
  and 802.11ax was still in **draft** (expected ratified ~2020), leaving much to vendor
  interpretation.
- **[Guest]** **1024-QAM** is a new modulation technique in ax giving slightly higher
  speeds only when very close to the AP; this is why some people see higher speeds on AC
  than AX in real conditions (many variables, configuration-dependent).
- **[Guest]** Routers with many antennas exist to support **8 spatial streams** (defined
  but never implemented in AC); most client devices only do 1–2 streams for power reasons,
  so 8 streams are mainly useful via MU-MIMO serving multiple clients at once. AX targets
  both downlink and uplink MU-MIMO (AC was downlink only).
- **[Guest]** **Target Wake Time (TWT)** is a scheduled power-saving mechanism — a device
  (e.g. a battery IoT/temperature sensor) negotiates a schedule to wake once per interval,
  freeing the RF the rest of the time; mainly valuable for enterprise battery IoT.
- **[Guest]** **BSS Coloring** has nothing to do with colors — it is numbers 0–64 in a
  frame field (visible in Wireshark). It reduces the collision/contention domain by letting
  clients on the same channel from a different "color" transmit simultaneously at lowered
  transmit power, so signals don't travel far enough to collide. Guests credit Chuck's own
  "team" analogy from his earlier video as actually a good explanation.
- **[Guest]** WiFi remains a two-way acknowledged medium even in WiFi 6; collisions cause
  retransmissions, which is what makes wireless feel slow. AX shifts more decision-making
  to the AP (which RUs, when clients talk) but only within a transmit opportunity, and
  contention for the RF still happens; mixed AX/non-AX environments will be common.
- **[Guest]** **5G vs WiFi 6:** cellular uses **licensed** frequencies (operators pay,
  exclusive use, can force interferers off) while WiFi uses **unlicensed** frequency
  (anyone can use it, no way to evict). The two are seen as **complementary**, not
  competitive.
- **[Guest — career]** To get into wireless, start with **CWNP** (Certified Wireless
  Network Professional), specifically the vendor-neutral **CWNA** administrator cert; the
  Sybex CWNA book by David Coleman and David Westcott is recommended (~1000 pages).
  **Ekahau** is the standard software (and Sidekick hardware) for WiFi design, site
  surveys, spectrum analysis, and troubleshooting, with its own training tiers (ECSE
  Design / Advanced / Troubleshooting). Cisco still has wireless tracks (CCNP
  concentrations, enterprise core, CCIE-level wireless). Recommendation: learn
  vendor-neutral WiFi fundamentals (CWNP) first so you understand "what the knobs do,"
  then layer vendor certs on top.
- **[Guest — career]** Wireless is described as fun and lucrative — "networking plus
  physics"; troubleshooting (fixing existing WiFi) is more common and important than
  greenfield deployment.
