# Your Cable Management SUCKS!! (Fixing My Server Room)

## Summary

A homelab/server-room cleanup video in which Chuck Keith rebuilds and re-cables his
personal on-prem rack after years of accumulated bad cabling habits. Sponsored by
Patch Box (whose retractable ethernet "cassettes," DevMount cage-nut replacements,
and "third hand"/setup.exe rack-holder tools frame the whole project). Over "five plus
days" he rewires his wall drops onto a fresh patch panel, replaces switches, re-racks
heavy servers (including "Terry," a dual-GPU server he gets off the floor), installs an
80 Gig inter-rack link, cleans and lights the room, and closes by roasting viewer-
submitted server-room photos from Twitter/LinkedIn. The through-line: messy cabling
makes troubleshooting impossibly slow, and disciplined cable management (and planning
ahead / doing work during maintenance windows) is worth the pain.

## Key claims (dated — practices + infra)

All dated 2024-06-26 (publication):

Practices / lessons:
- Cable messes accumulate from "good intentions" plus incremental patch-cable fixes;
  the cost shows up as troubleshooting time — Chuck tells employees a problem will take
  him ~an hour just to dig through the cable mess.
- Don't move/re-rack live gear during the workday while people are working; that's what
  maintenance weekends / after-hours windows are for. He did it live to avoid disturbing
  video editors, which made everything harder and forced unplugging anyway.
- Plan the cabling layout beforehand; because he didn't, he had to re-pluck and rearrange
  patch-panel IDCs after the fact (which aren't meant to come out easily).
- Poor documentation cost him time — with trunks/VLANs undocumented he had to relearn his
  own network to redo it.
- Troubleshooting lesson: when a new switch's throughput was bad, "just reboot" — after a
  reboot of the new switch, performance came right.
- Wiring a patch panel is easier than making a regular ethernet cable: score/strip the
  sheath, separate wires into the labeled conduits, punch down with a punch-down tool, add
  a dust cap, load into the panel.
- Flat patch cable (as used by Patch Box) is still quality twisted-pair and is made flat to
  allow more airflow than round cable; retractable cassettes let a run extend/shorten and
  move with a port without re-tracing.
- Over-cable-tying (zip-tying too aggressively) makes future tracing/undoing painful; he
  admits he zip-tied "almost too much."

Infra details revealed:
- Two racks in his personal server room, connected across the room.
- New inter-rack backbone: two MikroTik switches linked via QSFP+ DAC cables — each QSFP+
  port is 40 Gig; he bonded two into a port-channel for an 80 Gig connection between racks.
- Replaced a UniFi 8-port (~6 usable) 10 Gig switch (bought to give video editors 10 Gig
  access to his NAS) with a 24-port MikroTik switch, all 10 Gig SFP+, plus the two QSFP+ 40
  Gig ports.
- Storage: a 45Drives NAS ("45 drives") is his main storage; retiring a Synology server.
- "Terry" — a large dual-GPU server — moved off the floor onto a newly built rack shelf.
- Other gear moved/re-racked: his main desktop PC (kept in the server room to stay cool), a
  Dell server, a heavy UPS (moved to the bottom with help), his 45Drives NAS rack.
- Replaced an unmanaged little Cisco switch (only carrying management ports, cable strung
  across the rack, not rackable) with an old managed Cisco Catalyst 3570.
- To configure the 3570 he lacked a console-to-USB cable; used a Bluetooth wireless console
  adapter ("enter tooth" per captions) that worked on PC but not initially on Mac
  (unsponsored, just sent to try).
- Identifies as "a Cisco guy" — still prefers Cisco's CLI/way of doing things but now
  understands and likes MikroTik.
- NAS transfer speed baseline on same switch ~410 MB/s; the new bonded link initially gave
  only ~10–20 MB/s until the reboot fixed it.
- Wall drops: ~20 drops re-punched onto the new patch panel.
- Room finishing: swept floor, RGB lighting via Govee (floor strip + strips inside the rack
  rails, can blink like link lights), mounted a monitor to see system status; also runs
  display/camera connections over fiber to the other rack.
- Cross-promo: was teaching patch panels in the "intro to networking" course on NetworkChuck
  Academy ("launching very soon"); Network Chuck Coffee has a new decaf roast "404 Not Found."
