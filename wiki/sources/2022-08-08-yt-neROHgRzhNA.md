# How to get a job in IT (according to the experts)

## Summary

A long multi-expert compilation in which Chuck Keith (NetworkChuck) hosts and
interviews four IT/cybersecurity hiring insiders about how to land an IT job,
especially in cybersecurity. Chuck asks the questions and occasionally shares his
own hiring perspective; the substantive job-hunting advice comes from the **guests**,
each interviewed in a separate segment. The four guests are a technical recruiter, an
offensive-security hiring manager, a security consultant/trainer, and a security-team
manager. Common threads across all four: **projects and a home lab beat a thin resume**,
**certifications can substitute for a degree but must be backed by real knowledge**,
**communication and a "hungry / tinkerer" attitude often win over raw credentials**, and
**job-hopping and sloppy/inflated resumes are red flags**. Because the video is a
compilation of independent interviews, attribution below is tracked per speaker.

> Attribution note: Most claims here are **guest/context material** and do NOT train the
> persona. Only statements Chuck himself asserts are marked as subject data.

## Key claims (dated — 2022-08-08; labeled guest vs Chuck)

**Chuck (subject) — asserts:**
- Chuck now hires people and understands why recruiters exist: finding good people is
  "almost an art. It's a skill." (paraphrase)
- Chuck's own path mirrors the guests': help desk → network engineer; he "dabbles" in
  cybersecurity and makes videos about it. He has no college degree and considers himself
  to be doing okay. (paraphrase)
- Chuck frames breaking into IT/cyber as "a cheat code for life." (near-verbatim)
- When Chuck hires, his top factors are communication skills, culture fit / getting along
  with the team, hunger to learn, and whether the person likes to tinker. (paraphrase)
- Chuck's stance on certs vs degrees: certifications can replace degrees and often tell an
  employer more about what you know than a degree does. (paraphrase)
- Chuck's resume caution: don't list a skill (e.g. Active Directory) you touched 10 years
  ago and no longer know — you must be able to back up everything on the resume. (paraphrase)
- Chuck's teaching philosophy referenced by a guest: his videos make you do hands-on, and
  he tells people not to do a project just to do it — "have a reason for it." (paraphrase)

**Guests (context) — aggregate advice across the four interviews:**
- **Projects / home lab are the great equalizer** for candidates without experience. All
  four guests independently prize demonstrated work (home labs, Hack The Box / TryHackMe,
  GitHub, documented projects). One guest (Chris) described a no-experience candidate who
  got hired by writing a "project history" section detailing design → implementation →
  result, out-competing candidates who had experience. (paraphrase)
- **Tell a story on the resume.** Non-traditional backgrounds (insurance salesman, plumber,
  fireman) are fine if the resume shows the trajectory into IT/security. Recruiters scan a
  resume in ~8–10 seconds, so lead with the most relevant/impressive material. (paraphrase — Joe, Gene)
- **Resume red flags:** frequent job-hopping (esp. multiple sub-1-year or 3-month roles),
  spelling/grammar errors, listing too many tools, skill/experience mismatch (claiming
  "expert" with one year), and unexplained employment gaps. (paraphrase — Gene, Chris, Amir)
- **Degrees:** ~60%+ of job descriptions still list a degree (Joe), but most hiring managers
  interviewed no longer require one and weight demonstrated skill higher; on government
  contracts a degree can substitute for years of experience via "labor categories"
  (LCATs). (paraphrase — Joe, Gene)
- **Certifications:** useful and can replace a degree, but knowledge behind the cert matters
  most; collecting many certs without being able to answer basic questions is a red flag.
  For pen testing, Gene treats OSCP/GPEN as the entry-level bar; he also praised eCPPT (INE)
  and CRTO (RastaMouse / Daniel Duggin, Cobalt Strike training) and recommends the A+/Net+/Sec+
  "trifecta" for foundations. Gene called certs "a necessary evil" and a "crutch" because the
  industry lacks a better way to validate talent. (paraphrase — Gene, Chris, Amir, Joe)
- **Personal website / blog / GitHub / video** help — a domain, a maintained GitHub, a blog,
  or a talking-head video showcase communication skills and passion, and can act as a modern
  cover letter. Configuring your own MX/SPF/DMARC records signals attention to detail (Gene).
  A stale GitHub (one commit six months ago) hurts more than helps (Joe). (paraphrase)
- **Interview / soft skills:** communicate what *you* specifically did (Amir's STAR method;
  saying "we did it" raises doubt), it's fine to say "I don't know," follow up with a thank-you
  email, and let attitude/hunger carry the interview. (paraphrase — Amir, Chris, Gene)
- **Getting in with no experience:** network relentlessly (Joe: recruiters, LinkedIn, Discord,
  Reddit), build foundational labs, get a couple of relevant certs, and mirror a target job's
  environment in your home lab. (paraphrase — all four)
