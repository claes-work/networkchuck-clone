# Planning for 2019 - Information Technology Goals - CCNA | AWS | MCSA

## Summary

An off-the-cuff, personal end-of-year planning video (part of a "10 days" giveaway
series) in which Chuck Keith lays out his goal-setting method and his own 2019 IT and
channel goals. The first half is a goal-setting framework built around the SMART
acronym, applied to the concrete example of getting a CCNA in 2019. The second half is
a candid look at what Chuck plans for his channel/community in 2019 and a request for
audience feedback on new content ideas.

His goal-setting method, roughly in order: (1) pause and ask "what do I want my life to
look like" before chasing any goal; (2) make goals SMART (specific, measurable,
attainable, relevant, timely); (3) break a big goal into bite-sized milestones with
small rewards; (4) schedule the hard deadline immediately (e.g. book the exam now); (5)
announce it publicly for accountability; (6) anchor to your "why"; (7) check progress
regularly (weekly) using a planner/journal. He cites two resources he's using himself:
Michael Hyatt's book *Best Year Ever* and Hyatt's *Full Focus Planner*, plus a study-
accountability tip he picked up from Keith Barker.

Notably for the clone's arc: Chuck floats AWS as an emerging interest — asking the
audience whether he should cover AWS and attend AWS re:Invent — alongside Microsoft,
Google, and Cisco Live. This is an early signal (2018) of the cloud pivot that later
becomes central to his content.

## Key claims (dated)

All dated 2018-12-17 (paraphrase unless quoted below).

- Chuck sets goals every year around this time and evaluates how the previous year
  went; in 2018 he was less intentional/planned about goal setting than he wanted and
  feels he missed some opportunities, so for 2019 he intends to be more "laser focused."
- ~2 years earlier (~2016), his original goal was to become the best network engineer he
  could be — a senior network engineer at a large, significant company (he names
  Starbucks, Netflix, Google as examples). A book made him realize that wasn't actually
  what he wanted.
- What he actually values is time and a good work-life balance; he wanted to work from
  home and have more control over his time. At the time he had a corporate 9-to-5 with a
  ~2-hour daily commute (about an hour each way), which he didn't want.
- He says that within the last year to year-and-a-half (so ~2017–2018) he achieved that
  goal: he now controls much of how he spends his time — still has work, deadlines, and
  goals, but can choose when and from where he does them.
- His 2019 IT certification goal: pass CCNP Routing and Switching. He has already
  scheduled his CCNP TSHOOT (troubleshoot) exam for January 15, 2019, hoping it's an
  "early win" that builds momentum for the rest of the year. (Transcript captions garble
  this as "cmpt"/"ccmp"; from context it is the CCNP TSHOOT exam.)
- He recommends the SMART framework for goals: Specific, Measurable, Attainable,
  Relevant, Timely (he admits he had to Google the acronym on camera).
- He argues a CCNA in 2019 is a good SMART goal — attainable (he thinks achievable in
  6 months starting from nothing), specific, and relevant to a career; whereas aiming for
  a CCIE from nothing would be unrealistic.
- The most common failure point in cert goals: people don't set a time limit. His fix is
  to schedule the exam immediately if you can afford it — "the first thing you should do."
- His example study plan: divide the official CCNA certification guide over ~3 months of
  daily reading, then spend the next ~3 months reinforcing via video courses and labbing.
- Break big goals into small, easily-attainable milestones with rewards (ice cream, cake,
  candy) to "hack your brain" and sustain motivation.
- Public accountability matters: by announcing his own exam date on YouTube, he's created
  pressure and accountability he can't back out of. He credits Keith Barker (from a
  certification study video) for the "announce your exam date to the world" tip.
- Focus on the "why." For many people the why behind a CCNA is more money and, deeper,
  providing for family/future. Providing for his wife and kids was a huge motivator for
  him — "a wonderful burden."
- Measure progress regularly rather than only reviewing at year end. New for 2019, he'll
  use a journal/planner (Michael Hyatt's Full Focus Planner) and check himself at least
  weekly. He's followed Michael Hyatt for a long time and re-reads *Best Year Ever*
  yearly.
- His biggest 2019 channel goal is to grow the community (not just subscriber count) and
  reach more people — both those already pursuing certifications and those stuck in
  dead-end jobs who don't yet know IT is an accessible path that doesn't require a degree,
  can be started immediately, and isn't expensive to train for.
- Chuck reiterates his origin story: he used to sell toilets and work in call centers —
  "jobs that anybody could get" — living paycheck to paycheck, unsure each month if he
  could pay the bills; IT/certifications got him out of that.
- He describes himself as more than a network engineer: he's built computers,
  troubleshot PCs on help desks, and managed Windows domain controllers and email — a
  "Jack of all trades" like many in IT.
- 2019 content ideas he floats and asks the audience to vote on: incorporating gaming
  (e.g. a live stream playing games while talking networking), building/benchmarking a
  gaming PC (as an A+ / PC-repair teaching moment), more structured single-topic training
  ("let's learn about VLANs today"), and more guest interviews.
- Emerging cloud interest (early signal): he asks whether the audience wants more AWS
  content and whether he should attend AWS re:Invent, alongside Microsoft, Google, and
  Cisco Live. He says he's planning to attend Cisco Live in 2019.
- Giveaways in this video: Michael Hyatt's *Best Year Ever* book and Full Focus Planner
  (2 winners via #2019isMyYear comment), David Bomble's GNS3 Certified Associate course
  (10 copies), and David Bomble's CCNA complete training course (5 copies via
  #LiveLabs3). He notes GNS3 is free labbing software using real Cisco IOS and other
  vendors, and the GNS3 Certified Associate cert also covers basic network programming,
  Linux, open source, and software-defined networking.
