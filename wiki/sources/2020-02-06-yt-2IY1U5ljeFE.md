# OVERWHELMED with technology (IT)....what can I do?

## Summary

A personal, mindset-focused video in which Chuck describes waking up "paralyzed"
and "frozen" by a mental list of everything he needed to study, make, and do. He
crowdsourced coping advice from his audience across Twitter, Facebook, YouTube,
Instagram, and LinkedIn, received hundreds of responses, and curated them into a
simple two-step framework for handling learning/career overwhelm: **STOP**, then
**RESET**.

- **Step 1 — STOP**: disengage from the work to get out of the bad mindset. Sub-tactics
  he endorses: pray or meditate; exercise (physical activity resets the body, which
  resets the mind); take an "emotional timeout" (a moving movie, time with your spouse);
  spend quality time with family and friends to remember your "why"; ask for help
  (ideally from someone outside your field, like a spouse, for outside perspective); and
  procrastinate deliberately as "medicine, not a habit" (for him, playing Call of Duty).
- **Step 2 — RESET**: with a clear head, rebuild priorities. Write down everything you
  feel you must do (the list makes overload obvious), then write a *new* prioritized
  list based on what's important for your career, life, and family. Cut things out, say
  no, and do less — "by doing less I actually end up accomplishing more." Don't compare
  yourself to others (their why is different from yours). Reestablish a healthy daily
  routine (routine is what actually gets things done, and overwhelm makes you abandon it).
  Finally: **go slow and be okay with it** — "go slow, it's the fastest thing you can do."

Concrete personal example: Chuck was simultaneously studying CCNP Route, CCNP Switch,
DevNet Associate, and Linux Plus — all due in February — while working full-time at CBT
Nuggets, making YouTube content, learning hacking, and raising four kids. Applying the
framework, he cut CCNP Route and CCNP Switch (his only "why" for them was to say he'd
done it) and focused on the DevNet Associate, partly to make the DevNet 500.

## Key claims (dated — his advice)

*All dated 2020-02-06; paraphrase unless quoted.*

- Overwhelm is fundamentally a mindset problem — the fix is to change the mindset, not to grind harder.
- The two-step response to feeling overwhelmed is: STOP, then RESET (the sub-steps can be done in any order).
- To STOP, disengage and do something else: pray/meditate, exercise, take an emotional timeout, spend time with family/friends, ask for help, or deliberately procrastinate.
- Exercise resets the body, which in turn resets the mind, and produces endorphins that improve mood.
- Ask for help from someone outside your field (e.g., a spouse not into IT) — the outside perspective gets you out of your bubble and helps you see clearly.
- Deliberate procrastination (e.g., video games) is legitimate only as short-term "medicine" to escape the overwhelmed mindset, not as a habit or a "license to be lazy."
- To RESET, first write down everything you feel you must do — seeing the full list makes it obvious you're trying to do too much.
- Then rewrite a new, prioritized list of only what's best for your time and energy right now, judged against your career, life, family, and friends.
- Cut things out and say no — do less; doing less lets you accomplish more, because overwhelm leads to doing nothing and burning out.
- Don't compare yourself to others in IT — their circumstances and their "why" differ from yours.
- Reestablish a solid, healthy daily routine; routine (daily habits and schedule) is what actually makes things happen, and overwhelm makes people abandon the routines that matter to them.
- Go slow and be okay with it — slowing down and focusing means you do things well and enjoy the process; if you never enjoy the process you'll hate it down the road.
- You have time; hustle culture pushes racking up certifications fast, but some things just take time, and for persistent hard workers there is always another opportunity.
