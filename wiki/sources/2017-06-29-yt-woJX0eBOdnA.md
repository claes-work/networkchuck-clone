# CCNA or Python? | Should I Become a Network Engineer or a Programmer?

## Summary
Chuck argues that the CCNA-vs-Python question is a false choice: rather than picking networking or programming, aspiring engineers should do both and become what the industry calls a "unicorn" — someone who knows networking but also a little programming. He rejects the claim (from an unnamed critic) that the CCNA is worthless, insisting it remains extremely valuable, while conceding programming is the future as networking shifts from manual CLI configuration toward controllers, scripting, and automation. His practical advice: keep pursuing certifications, but invest a small amount of regular time (an hour a week or a day) learning Python. The video also debuts two recurring segments — "The Broadcast Address" (news) and "certification wins" (viewer shout-outs) — plus a Cisco Live 2017 news roundup and a homelab tip.

## Key claims
- (2017-06-29) Not every network engineer will need to become a full-fledged developer or programmer; Chuck does not foresee needing in-depth API knowledge in the near future, though he allows it's possible.
- (2017-06-29) Networking is changing, and engineers who don't keep learning and growing will get left behind — but constant learning is nothing new for network engineers, which is why certifications are valuable.
- (2017-06-29) What you learned in college 10 years ago has very little to do with today's job; staying current on certifications is what makes you valuable.
- (2017-06-29) He credits Cisco's certification tracks and ecosystem with changing his life and enabling his career.
- (2017-06-29) He strongly disagrees with an unnamed critic who says the CCNA is worth only the toilet paper it's written on; the CCNA is extremely valuable and will make your career, and beginners shouldn't be discouraged by that view.
- (2017-06-29) Programming is the future, and networking will move away from individually configuring switches/routers in the CLI toward programmatic automation via controllers, programs, and scripting — but you still have to know how to do it manually.
- (2017-06-29) One way or another, programming will become part of your life if you work in networking, voice, security, or IT.
- (2017-06-29) Jumping straight into Python or Java instead of the CCNA is a viable path to a lucrative career; Python is very popular and is the typical entry-level language, and Cisco has released Python training via its network programmability tracks.
- (2017-06-29) At a Dallas Cisco users group event hosted by TekSystems, a recruiter reported the network engineering job market in Dallas had roughly doubled and they were struggling to find network engineers.
- (2017-06-29) Recruiters are specifically looking for "unicorns" — people who know networking plus a little programming — because they are rare.
- (2017-06-29) Chuck's own answer and personal practice: hold/pursue your certifications (he has CCNA, CCNA Voice, is studying CCNP Routing & Switching) AND start learning a little Python; he bought a Python course on Udemy for this reason.
- (2017-06-29) Actionable advice: invest a little time — even once a week or an hour a day — into learning programming; you don't have to become a programmer, just start playing with it.
- (2017-06-29) Udemy is recommended as a cheap resource for certification and Python training; Udemy courses geared toward network engineers teach writing Python scripts to automate routing/switching tasks and configure routers.
- (2017-06-29, news) Cisco is transitioning from a hardware-only company toward software; its Cisco DNA (Digital Network Architecture) uses machine learning and Encrypted Traffic Analytics to detect malware even in encrypted traffic, with ~75 companies (e.g., Royal Caribbean, NASA) using it.
- (2017-06-29, news) Cisco announced the Catalyst 9000 series, an IoT/"smart" programmable switch with an API for the ASIC, end-to-end security, container-based architecture for hosting third-party apps, and design help from Ferrari.
- (2017-06-29, tip) For lab/study, get hands-on experience; Packet Tracer and GNS3 are great, but physical hardware can't be beaten — labbing it up builds confidence. Chuck mentions buying a Cisco UCS chassis running Call Manager, Presence, Unity, and Expressway.
