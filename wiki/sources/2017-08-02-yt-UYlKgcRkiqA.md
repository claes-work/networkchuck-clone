# How Does Cisco Create Exams? + CCNA GiveAWAY - INE BUNDLE!!

## Summary
Chuck Keith recounts a conversation he had a few months earlier with someone from The Cisco Learning Network about how Cisco builds its certification exams. He explains that a small, isolated Cisco exam team designs the questions by surveying industry leaders about needed skills, while the content and study-material providers only ever receive the published exam topics — never the actual exam — which is why candidates often meet questions their study material never prepared them for. (The video's second half is an INE CCNA bundle giveaway, not covered here.)

## Key claims
- (2017-08-02) The Cisco exam team — the people who write the certification questions — is described as a small, isolated "silo" part of Cisco.
- (2017-08-02) The exam team's stated goal is to make certified engineers more valuable to their employers, so their exams verify real skill.
- (2017-08-02) To decide exam content, the exam team surveys industry leaders — CIOs of major companies and talent recruiters filling roles — asking what skills they need from network, security, and collaboration engineers.
- (2017-08-02) They aggregate that survey input and build the exam questions around what the industry actually needs, deliberately making the exams difficult.
- (2017-08-02) Chuck identifies a "disconnect": The Cisco Learning Network (and third-party training providers like CBT and INE) that create study material have little to no contact with the exam team.
- (2017-08-02) The only thing content providers receive from the exam team is the published exam topics — the same list available to the public — not the exam itself.
- (2017-08-02) Because exam topics are broad rather than a detailed bulleted list (e.g., "switching concepts, know about spanning tree"), providers are "flying blind" and can't perfectly predict question content.
- (2017-08-02) Chuck concludes this disconnect is likely why candidates hit exam questions their study material didn't lead them to expect, and says the process gave him more respect for content creators.
