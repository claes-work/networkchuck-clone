# you're about to get hacked!! (7 reasons why) // FREE Security+ // EP 6

## Summary

Episode 6 of Chuck Keith's FREE Security+ course (sponsored by CompTIA, tied to the
new SY0-601 exam). The framing device: humans run a "human OS" that has seven
un-patchable vulnerabilities, which is why social engineering attacks succeed. Chuck
walks through the seven psychological principles of social engineering by analyzing
famous movie/TV scenes where each is exploited — Sneakers (1992), a Jimmy Kimmel
street-password bit, Catch Me If You Can, Breaking Bad, and Hackers (1995) — plus a
real-world example from spam comments on his own channel. Interspersed are CompTIA
CertMaster practice quiz questions (pretexting, impersonation, scarcity/urgency).

The seven weaknesses named: **authority, intimidation, consensus, scarcity,
familiarity, trust, urgency**. The defensive takeaway is awareness: you can't patch
the human OS, so the "hotfix" is staying current on attacker patterns so you can
recognize them — Chuck compares this to how antivirus/firewalls work off signatures
and patterns. He closes by deliberately running all seven tactics on the viewer to
sell NetworkChuck Coffee, demonstrating them live and disclosing the manipulation.
Course is co-produced with David Bombal and Jeremy Cioara; releasing ~one video/week
free on YouTube.

## Key claims (dated — the 7 vectors + defense)

All dated 2020-11-20; all paraphrase unless quoted.

- **The premise:** humans run a "human OS" with seven vulnerabilities that essentially
  can't be patched, which is why we fall for social engineering attacks.
- **Vector 1 — Authority:** people comply with perceived authority figures. Examples:
  Frank Abagnale claiming to be Secret Service (Catch Me If You Can); the Jimmy Kimmel
  TV host; Walter White posing as a New York Times reporter (Breaking Bad); the Hackers
  caller invoking his boss "Mr. Kawasaki."
- **Vector 2 — Intimidation:** an attacker gets forceful/aggressive to pressure the
  target into compliance (the Sneakers delivery man becoming forceful at the door).
- **Vector 3 — Consensus (social proof / social norms):** people follow the crowd.
  Examples: spam "this tool works" comments manufacturing agreement; paid Amazon
  reviews; and tailgating, where holding a door open for someone who acts like they
  belong is a social norm people feel pressured to obey.
- **Vector 4 — Scarcity:** limited availability ("last bag," "only three left") makes
  people act fast; used in both hacking and ordinary sales.
- **Vector 5 — Familiarity:** people comply with what/who feels familiar (the fake
  delivery guy in Sneakers dropping the name "Marge" and a birthday party).
- **Vector 6 — Trust:** people extend trust once a small measure of rapport is built
  (the Sneakers door guard buzzing the man in; Abagnale building trust with the FBI
  agent on the fly).
- **Vector 7 — Urgency:** manufactured time pressure ("I'm late for the party," a debt
  offer that "expires") makes people decide quickly without thinking.
- **Supporting techniques named:** *pretexting* (inventing a cover story — the cake
  delivery, the NYT reporter, the "power surge wiped my file" caller) and
  *impersonation* (dressing as an internet tech; posing as a reporter) — these are the
  delivery mechanisms that carry the seven psychological levers.
- **Real-world example:** password-hacking videos attract 3–4 spam comments/day pushing
  fake "hack Instagram/Facebook" tools; the comments use consensus to seem legitimate.
- **Defense — awareness is the only "patch":** you can't patch the human OS, so treat
  the episode as a hotfix. Stay up to date on the latest attacks and attacker patterns;
  becoming familiar with their patterns lets you detect them — same principle as
  antivirus/firewall signatures.
- **Defensive mindset against scarcity/urgency:** "what's a good deal today will be a
  good deal tomorrow" — slow down so you don't do things quickly that you'd reconsider
  with time (he notes limited exceptions like Black Friday).
- **Password hygiene aside:** your password should never be something personal and
  easily learned about you (the Kimmel victim used dog's name + graduation year).
- **Course context:** part of the FREE Security+ series for the new CompTIA SY0-601
  exam, co-produced with David Bombal and Jeremy Cioara, released roughly weekly on
  YouTube (the paid course has more).
