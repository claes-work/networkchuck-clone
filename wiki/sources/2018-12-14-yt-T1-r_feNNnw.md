# The ONE Skill You NEED in IT - Information Technology

## Summary

Chuck Keith argues that the single most valuable skill in IT is **customer service** — the ability to deal with, talk to, and empathize with people. He frames the video for two audiences: people already in IT and people not yet in IT. His thesis is that people skills, not technical genius, are what carry an IT career, because you will always have to communicate with fellow IT staff, project managers, non-technical bosses, and interviewers. He grounds this in his own path from selling toilets (and jobs at Starbucks, Jack in the Box, a call center) to making over $100,000/year as an IT professional within five years, with no college degree — arguing the customer-service skills from those service jobs transferred directly to his first help-desk role.

Along the way he busts stereotypes: you don't have to be a nerd, a child prodigy, or good at math to succeed in IT; there's a wide range of IT roles (project managers, programmers, network engineers, help desk); and he pitches IT as a self-taught, low-cost-of-entry career (a ~$50 book plus cheap video training, no student debt). He notes he is an introvert who had to learn people skills deliberately, so the "one skill" is learnable if you don't already have it. The video also runs day two of a 10-day giveaway (a copy of Boson NetSim for the CCNA) and plugs certification training (David Bombal CCNA, Mike Meyers A+). Chuck was working at CBT Nuggets creating IT training at the time.

## Key claims (dated — the ONE skill + why)

- **[2018-12-14] The ONE skill you need in IT is customer service** — knowing how to deal with people, talk to people, and help them. Chuck calls it "the most valuable skill in IT," at any level. (paraphrase)
- **[2018-12-14] Why it's #1: you always have to deal with people.** No matter how technical the role, you communicate with fellow IT staff, project managers, and (often non-technical) managers about projects you want to implement — so you must learn to communicate. (paraphrase)
- **[2018-12-14] It beats out the "obvious" technical answers.** Programming, cybersecurity, and building websites are amazing, valuable skills worth building — but you'll never reach your full potential as an IT professional without learning how to deal with people. (paraphrase)
- **[2018-12-14] Communication wins interviews.** More and more, the skill employers want is effective communication; you have to show in an interview that you can communicate — a point reinforced by his brother's story of landing a network admin job. (paraphrase)
- **[2018-12-14] It's automation-proof.** Chuck argues people skills are the hardest thing to automate; dealing with and empathizing with people can't be properly automated yet, making it a durable ("killer") skill. (paraphrase)
- **[2018-12-14] Even brilliant technical people fail without it.** Some IT people know an enormous amount but fail to communicate their accomplishments to bosses — including non-IT bosses — so you must explain things in layman's terms. (paraphrase)
- **[2018-12-14] Many people already have this skill.** Anyone who has worked in a call center, Starbucks, McDonald's, or any people-facing job already has the beginnings of the most valuable IT skill. (paraphrase)
- **[2018-12-14] And if you don't have it, it's learnable.** Chuck describes himself as an introvert who had to learn charisma and presence deliberately (by copying other YouTube creators), so the skill can be developed. (paraphrase)
- **[2018-12-14] Career/mindset framing:** Chuck went from selling toilets to $100k+/year in IT within five years, no college; the customer-service skills from his service jobs (Starbucks barista, Jack in the Box, call center) are what got him far, not raw technical skill. (paraphrase, self-reported)
- **[2018-12-14] Stereotype busting:** you don't have to be a nerd, a prodigy, or good at math (Chuck says he "sucks at math"); IT has many role types beyond "programming at Facebook" or "the guy who answers phone calls." (paraphrase)
- **[2018-12-14] Low cost of entry / self-taught:** he broke in by buying a ~$50 book plus ~$50 video training and studying between calls, avoiding student loans — you can learn the rest on the job or via certifications. (paraphrase, self-reported)
