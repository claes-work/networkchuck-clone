# I PASSED THE CCNA EXAM!! - ICND2 Exam Tips

## Summary

An interview-format video in which Chuck sits down with his younger brother Cameron, who has just passed the ICND2 exam (200-105) to complete his CCNA Routing and Switching certification. It is a companion to an earlier ICND1 video. Cameron walks through the ICND2 test experience (hardest topics, simulations, question wording), the study resources he used, how he coped with pre-exam stress, and his career next steps. Chuck layers on his own advice about marketing a new certification, resume placement, and the industry shift toward programmability. Note on the milestone: the video is Cameron's CCNA pass, framed as a process Chuck is documenting; the persona-relevant biographical takeaway is Chuck's own recurring emphasis on the CCNA path and his mentoring role, plus the concrete ICND2 exam tips he endorses.

## Key claims (dated paraphrase)

- (2018-08-11) Cameron passed ICND2, completing the CCNA Routing and Switching; the earlier video covered his ICND1 pass. About eight or nine months elapsed between his ICND1 and ICND2.
- (2018-08-11) The hardest ICND2 area for Cameron was infrastructure services and maintenance (e.g., SNMP, WAN topics), plus PPPoE — which he had trouble practicing because Packet Tracer does not support it.
- (2018-08-11) The exam included two or three questions on APIC-EM and hit virtualization and programmability topics, reflecting Cisco's push to prepare candidates for SDN and infrastructure-as-a-service / cloud.
- (2018-08-11) Both of Cameron's simulations were routing labs (EIGRP and OSPF) rather than a Layer 2 / spanning-tree sim; he credits the simulations with getting him a passing score.
- (2018-08-11) Chuck's stance: don't just memorize facts — understand the concepts, because the exam rephrases material in unexpected ways.
- (2018-08-11) Best single resource per Cameron: Packet Tracer and labbing; his strong recommendation is to build your own labs from scratch using `?` (context help) and show commands rather than following along with someone else's lab, to build real troubleshooting knowledge.
- (2018-08-11) In the sim labs Cameron was not restricted from using the question-mark help or shortened command forms (e.g., `show ip int brief`).
- (2018-08-11) Video course path Cameron used: started ICND2 with INE (found it accurate but dry on advanced topics), then switched to CBT Nuggets with Jeremy Cioara, whom he praises for keeping hard topics engaging; he credits Keith Bogart's Packet Tracer labs (spanning-tree priorities, bridge cost, root bridge election) as especially helpful; also used some David Bombal GNS3/Packet Tracer material and Cisco Press books as the deep reference.
- (2018-08-11) Top three exam-focus topics per Cameron: EIGRP, OSPF, and spanning-tree; know specific show commands and the difference between RSTP and STP.
- (2018-08-11) Cameron found ICND2 harder than ICND1, even though he felt more prepared going in.
- (2018-08-11) Stress-coping advice: ramp down one or two days before the exam instead of cramming; do nothing the night and morning before; Chuck's own ritual is watching a movie (The Hobbit / Wolf of Wall Street) and playing Skyrim, plus a "lucky red hoodie" — superstition that isn't literally effective but gets you in the right mindset and stops you psyching yourself out.
- (2018-08-11) Career context: Cameron's earlier CCENT helped him move from a computer repair shop to a help desk role at a mortgage company; the CCNA is a very popular networking cert, so candidates should differentiate themselves (e.g., pursuing CCNA Collaboration, learning Python/programming).
- (2018-08-11) Chuck's marketing advice: as soon as you pass, post it everywhere (Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn) with hashtags; you don't need to list a license number, and few employers actually verify certs.
- (2018-08-11) Resume advice: put your certifications at the top with your name — including certs you are still working on — because "working on X" makes your resume surface in recruiter searches and signals you keep learning.
- (2018-08-11) Chuck runs a CCNA study group (over ~7,000 members at the time) plus a CCNA Collaboration group; he points viewers there for peer support.
- (2018-08-11) Chuck's framing of the industry shift: programmability is being woven into every track (security, collaboration, wireless) — "API is the new CLI" — but you still need core routing, security, and collaboration knowledge.
