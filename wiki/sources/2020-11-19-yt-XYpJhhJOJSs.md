# my beard isn't real // FREE Security+ // EP 5

Episode 5 of Chuck Keith's FREE CompTIA Security+ (SY0-601 era) course. This entry
fills the previously-noted EP5 gap in the FREE Security+ series. The lesson covers
**hoaxes, influence campaigns, and hybrid warfare** — social-engineering threats that
weaponize public opinion and the audience itself. The jokey title "my beard isn't
real" is a running self-referential bit Chuck uses as the episode's worked example of
how fake news ("beard gate") propagates.

## Summary

Chuck teaches the Security+ domain topic of **hoaxes and misinformation** as a
progression of severity:

1. **Simple hoaxes** — the ones "our grandmas fall for": sextortion/blackmail emails
   claiming malware recorded you (demanding Bitcoin), Nigerian-prince/Bill-Gates
   giveaway scams, and fake "your computer has a virus, call this number" tech-support
   scams that prey on less-technical, often older people. He references scam-baiter
   Kitboga, who poses as an old woman to waste scammers' time.
2. **More sophisticated hoaxes** — "ones that even we fall for": the July 2020 Twitter
   hack, where compromised blue-check accounts (Bezos, Gates, Kanye) tweeted a
   double-your-Bitcoin scam. Because people trust verified accounts as news sources,
   many fell for it — Chuck cites 403 transactions / 12.89 BTC (~$208,000 at recording
   time) on the blockchain. He notes he interviewed Marcus Hutchins about it elsewhere.
3. **Influence campaigns / hybrid warfare** — the most dangerous tier: coordinated
   (often state-sponsored) actors create thousands of free fake social accounts, seed
   fake news, get "organic shares" from real users, and — if lucky — get trusted mass
   media to report it, lending false validity. He cites Russia/China meddling in the
   2016 US election (references a Time magazine article).

Recommended defenses: never blindly trust the internet, verify with snopes.com,
cross-check stories across multiple independent outlets, and critically analyze
evidence rather than trusting a claim because someone said it. He frames coffee as the
tool to "stay vigilant, stay awake, stay alert." The episode uses "network chuck's
beard is not really a beard — it's fake" ("beard gate") as its running joke and the
concrete example of how a fabricated rumor spreads.

Course-series notes: recorded partly at a beach house ("network chuck beach edition"),
so Chuck flags the audio may sound different. Sponsored by a Pluralsight free-weekend
promotion (Nov 20–22). He reminds viewers the Security+ course posts new content "every
single day" with a link below.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated 2020-11-19)

- A hoax is a threat that isn't real; awareness is the primary defense, and the
  audience sharing it is "part of the problem." [beginner Security+ concept]
- Classic hoax emails (fake malware/sextortion, prince scams, giveaway scams) mostly
  fail now because people have grown aware — but attackers are getting better.
- The 2020 Twitter hack worked because people trust verified ("blue check") accounts as
  news sources; the scam netted 403 transactions / 12.89 BTC (~$208K at the time).
- Fake tech-support "you have a virus, call this number" scams prey on non-technical,
  often older users and end in remote access plus a bogus removal fee.
- Influence campaigns hack **public opinion**, not machines: thousands of fake accounts
  seed fake news, real users provide "organic shares," and trusted media picking it up
  gives it false validity.
- Influence campaigns are a form of **hybrid warfare** — attacking a country with
  non-military tools (social media) rather than military force; Russia and China have
  used this against the US, including the 2016 election.
- Two dangers: (1) ordinary users become the weapon by sharing, and (2) growing
  distrust erodes faith in legitimately trusted news sources.
- Defense: distrust the internet by default, verify on snopes.com, corroborate a story
  across multiple independent outlets, and analyze evidence critically.
