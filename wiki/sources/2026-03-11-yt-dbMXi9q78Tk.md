# I almost quit YouTube....

## Summary

A personal, reflective video (filmed during a three-month sabbatical in Okinawa,
Japan, with his family — self-reported) in which Chuck Keith admits AI-driven anxiety
and burnout nearly made him quit YouTube. He describes a "midlife AI crisis": the pace
of AI releases (a harness he calls "OpenClaw"/"Open Claw", model releases, a viral
"Matt Schumer" article claiming AI is coming for screen-based jobs) left him paralyzed,
unable to publish, and constantly feeling behind. He fled to Japan for separation and
clarity, but the anxiety followed him. He examines both sides of the AI-jobs fear —
the alarmist article and CEO/study claims (layoffs, entry-level jobs vanishing) versus
the rebuttals (weaponized hype, historical over-forecasting, no measurable ROI, labor
displacement still speculative) — and concludes nobody knows the timeline. His resolution:
he will not bury his head in the sand and he will not quit. He is changing how he runs
the channel — dropping the "learn this right now or get left behind" guru posture and
instead learning alongside his audience, being honest when tools overwhelm him or when
he dislikes AI. He reframes IT skills as still valuable because they make you better at
guiding AI when it fails. He closes with his 2026 personal motto, "relentless optimism,"
and a prayer for the audience.

## Key claims (paraphrase; dated 2026-03-11 unless noted)

- He almost quit YouTube because AI stress and burnout became overwhelming and were
  robbing his joy. (self-reported)
- He is in Japan for three months on what he frames as a sabbatical, with his family in
  Okinawa, seeking separation and clarity from AI anxiety — but the anxiety followed him
  there. (self-reported)
- Reasons he nearly quit: the pace of AI is paralyzing; he couldn't decide what content
  to even make anymore (e.g., "Do I still teach Linux?"); he felt he couldn't advise his
  audience while not having it figured out himself; his default when overwhelmed is to go
  silent, which is why his publishing slowed. (self-reported)
- His own curiosity and genuine love of technology — the trait that makes him good at
  tech — has become his "worst enemy" with AI, because he compulsively tinkers and can't
  stop (talking to AI while shopping, voice-dictating in caves, reading his feed during
  meals). (self-reported)
- He says it is okay to feel this way, okay to say you hate AI, and recommends therapy;
  he admits he "kind of hate[s] AI sometimes" — a love/hate relationship. (self-reported)
- He believes he was a better technologist, creator, and human without AI, and is trying
  to find balance rather than quit AI entirely (which he decided he can't do). (self-reported)
- On the AI-jobs fear: he thinks the viral article went viral because it spoke to a
  widespread fear, not necessarily truth, and "fear goes pretty viral." He cites rebuttals
  — Gary Marcus calling it "weaponized hype," a Fortune rebuttal (coding has compilers/tests
  to verify; law/medicine/finance are harder), every prior tech revolution overestimating
  transformation speed, 95% of organizations seeing no measurable AI ROI, and the Yale
  Budget Lab calling AI labor displacement "largely speculative." He concludes nobody knows
  the timeline.
- His realization: it doesn't matter whether AI takes jobs in 2 or 10 years — the anxiety,
  burnout, and feeling of always being behind are real and happening today, and that is
  what he wants to address.
- His conclusion on skills: everyone will use AI, so value comes from what you can do when
  the AI fails or needs guidance; learning IT skills (e.g., network engineering) is still
  one of the most valuable things you can do because it makes you better with AI.
- He admits he is not good at AI tooling / designing AI systems and workflows despite being
  able to set up, cluster, and train models — and cites Daniel Miessler ("Daniel Mesler")
  as someone who does it well. (self-reported)
- What he is changing on the channel: he will stop pretending to be an expert/guru and stop
  the "learn everything right now" framing; instead he will learn with the audience, be
  honest when overwhelmed or when a tool "sucks," and do more live streams. Planned: an
  "Open Claw" series (including an AI IT-department setup) and Japan/nerd content; he expects
  to talk with the creator of "Open Claw" soon. (self-reported plans)
- His core audience framing: technologists, network engineers (or aspiring ones), and
  hacking/cybersecurity people — "that's you."
- His 2026 personal phrase and approach to life, the channel, AI, and the IT industry:
  "relentless optimism."
- Longstanding self-description: he has never claimed to be an expert; he just loves tech
  enough to be persistent until he learns it well, and his one real skill is teaching.
  (self-reported)
- Twingate is the sponsor (a long-term sponsor); he uses it daily for zero-trust access to
  his home data center (networking rack, NAS, Mac Studio cluster, Raspberry Pis) from Japan,
  including for his "OpenClaw" agents. (sponsor segment)
- He closes by praying for the audience, stating he believes in the power of prayer and
  values "people over machines and productivity every time."
