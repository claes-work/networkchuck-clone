# 7 CCNA CCNP Study Tips for the New Year - 2017!!

## Summary
An early NetworkChuck video framed as a New Year's resolution guide, laying out seven steps for setting and hitting Cisco CCNA/CCNP certification study goals in 2017. Chuck draws on his own path (finishing CCNP Route/Switch, pursuing voice/collaboration) to cover certification choice, time planning, study materials, motivation, study technique, mindset, and hands-on labbing. It doubles as an early statement of his belief that certifications and experience outweigh degrees.

## Key claims
- (2017-01-02) Step 1 — Pick the right certification for your career. Route/Switch is always needed and always has jobs; voice is "hot" (recruiters constantly reaching out); security is hot due to data leaks. Whatever track you choose, build a Route/Switch foundation first.
- (2017-01-02) Step 2 — Set very specific, non-negotiable time goals. Schedule the actual test if you have the money; be realistic about family, job, and school obligations. Chuck's guideline for serious learners: 2-3 hours of study per day, with rest days built in. Rough timelines: ICND1/CCENT ~4 months from scratch (2 months if experienced), ICND2 ~2-3 months, CCNA in one test ~2-3 months for the experienced, CCNA specialization tracks ~2-3 months, and ~2-3 months per CCNP test (4-5 months if returning after a long break).
- (2017-01-02) Step 3 — Pick quality study material and use multiple mediums (books AND videos, plus charts and flashcards); don't limit yourself to one. Recommends CBT Nuggets (praises Jeremy Cioara as "tops"), INE, Pluralsight, Cisco Press books, and lab books. Do not trust training material alone — pull the official certification blueprint from cisco.com and use it as your guide, since some exam topics aren't covered by courses. Uses todoist to schedule study.
- (2017-01-02) Step 4 — Incentives are the most important step. Keep the long-term reason (career, promotion, raise, better life for family) front of mind, and set short-term rewards: a big reward on finishing a cert (Chuck's example: buying a PS4) and small daily treats (ice cream, coffee, gaming) for hitting study sessions. He frames motivation as brain chemistry you have to "play."
- (2017-01-02) Step 5 — Learn how to study. Use a Pomodoro timer (~20-minute focused blocks with breaks, since sessions past 20-30 min lose effectiveness); make handwritten notes (better retention, then type them up as double review); use flashcards with a spaced-repetition system (SRS) — he uses Anki (free on desktop, paid on mobile but worth it), mentions Quizlet; stay organized with Evernote or OneNote.
- (2017-01-02) Step 6 — Stay positive and stay excited; burnout is inevitable. When slumping, watch YouTube certification success stories, follow motivating people on Twitter/blogs, and find accountability partners. Surround yourself with like-minded or better people.
- (2017-01-02) Step 7 — Lab, lab, lab. Hands-on practice builds the confidence to walk into the exam and the workplace. Tool guidance: Packet Tracer or GNS3 for CCNA/CCNP Route; physical equipment for the CCNP switch exam (GNS3 can't properly emulate a switch); real endpoints/phones (plus virtualized Call Manager, Unity Express) for collaboration. Praises the Cisco Networking Academy lab books.
- (2017-01-02) Warns against "paper certs" / brain dumps — labbing is what separates real skill from someone who just memorized and can't configure anything.
- (2017-01-02) Chuck states he values certifications more than degrees: he stopped his four-year degree at WGU because it no longer added value once he was already working the job it would have gotten him; he holds that certifications plus experience make you "golden" in most companies, especially in IT.
