# Get your CCNA RIGHT NOW!! (before the new CCNA 200-301)

> ⚠️ **VERSION-DATE / HISTORICAL ADVICE (2019-06-23).** This entire video is a
> **time-bound campaign** urging viewers to earn the *old* CCNA (any track) before the
> Cisco certification overhaul on **February 24, 2020** ("cert-pocalypse"). The
> "eight months" deadline, the "get it now" urgency, the crash study plan, and the
> product/pricing recommendations were all specific to the **mid-2019 → Feb-2020 window**
> and are now purely historical. The new CCNA 200-301 has long since shipped; do NOT
> present any of this as current guidance. Chuck's structural claims about how the new
> exam is built (60–70% overlap, absorbed wireless/security, 20% automation) remain
> useful as background on the 200-301's design.

## Summary

Chuck's argument video for why viewers should rush to earn their **current CCNA** (any
of the ~10 existing tracks — routing & switching, security, wireless, collaboration,
etc.) before Cisco retires them on **February 24, 2020**, when a single unified CCNA
(the 200-301) replaces all of them. The video is split into two parts: a fast
"everything you need to know" section (~first 10 minutes) and a set of long recorded
interviews with Cisco's certification leaders (Yusuf and Joe), plus co-host David Bombal.
Sponsored by Boson Software (25% summer sale, code "summer19").

**The core incentive argument (the "why now"):** anyone who is *already certified* on a
current-track CCNA/CCNP at the February 2020 cutover is a "winner" of the migration —
they get to keep three things: (1) their existing certification, valid the normal three
years from their exam date (e.g. pass in Nov 2019 → valid to Nov 2022); (2) the brand-new
CCNA certification, granted automatically; and (3) a new **"training badge"** for their
specialty (routing & switching, security, etc.) they can display on LinkedIn. Someone who
does *nothing* by the cutover — Chuck's cautionary example is a viewer stuck at CCENT /
ICND1 — keeps only the retiring CCENT and gains none of the migration rewards, effectively
"has nothing" and must start the new CCNA fresh.

**The crash study plan (get the CCNA from zero in ~8 months):** Chuck says it's totally
doable and lays out the tools he recommends — a good book, good video training, a good
lab, and (if affordable) practice exams. His specific 2019 picks: **video** — CBT Nuggets
($59/mo) plus David Bombal's Udemy course (~$10); **book** — Wendell Odom's Official Cert
Guide (OCG) or Todd Lammle; **lab** — Boson NetSim (his top pick) or David Bombal's Packet
Tracer labs course (~$10, free Packet Tracer download); **practice exams** — Boson ExSim
and AlphaPrep. He points viewers to his ~15,000-member CCNA R&S study group for community
support. He frames the deadline concretely: 8 months = 246 days = 35 weeks = 5,904 hours.

**What the new CCNA (200-301) will actually contain:** roughly **60–70% overlap** with the
current R&S CCNA, so current study still applies. It absorbs pieces of the retiring tracks:
**wireless** principles and AP modes/architectures (from CCNA Wireless), **security**
fundamentals (from CCNA Security), plus **virtualization** and looking at IP config on
other OSes (macOS/Linux/Windows). OSPF is narrowed to OSPFv2 (IPv4, single/relevant area);
multi-area OSPF and EIGRP/RIP/BGP appear pushed up to CCNP. The biggest change is
**automation & programmability (~20% of the exam)**: DNA Center, REST APIs, JSON, and
config-management tools (Ansible/Puppet/Chef) at a *conceptual* level — **no Python required
for CCNA** (Python lives at CCNP/CCIE and DevNet). The new **DevNet Associate** is explicitly
for software developers (80% programmability / 20% networking), the mirror image of the CCNA
(80% networking / 20% automation) — a "bridge" so network engineers and developers converge.

**Other Q&A points:** No prerequisites for the new CCNP — after Feb 24, 2020 you can jump
straight into any CCNP or concentration. To go deeper into automation, Chuck advises the
CCNP automation *concentration* over the DevNet Associate (unless you're truly a developer).
For cloud, since CCNA Cloud is dead, go to vendor certs (he plugs his Azure AZ-900 CBT
Nuggets course, AZ-103 in progress). Recertification gains a new **continuing-education
credits** option (30 credits over 3 years via training, Cisco Live sessions, writing exam
items) as an alternative to re-taking the exam. On market saturation: Cisco expects more
CCNAs and CCNPs but argues it won't devalue them because the redesign was driven by 2+ years
of interviews with companies reporting a skills gap. Chuck's closing philosophy: "embrace the
change you'll win; if you don't you'll get left behind" — but not to be scared, since IT has
always evolved this way.

## Key claims (dated)

All claims paraphrased and dated **2019-06-23**; all are time-bound to the mid-2019 → Feb-2020
migration window unless noted as structural facts about the 200-301 exam design.

- Chuck challenges viewers to earn their current CCNA (whatever track) **before February 23,
  2020**; the cutover to the new certifications happens **February 24, 2020** ("cert-pocalypse").
- Those already certified (CCNA or CCNP) at the cutover are the "winners" of the migration.
- You can go from **zero to CCNA in ~8 months** — hard but doable; he quantifies 8 months as
  246 days / 35 weeks / 5,904 hours.
- To earn an IT cert you need (up to) four things: a good book, good video training, a good lab,
  and — if you can afford it — good practice exams.
- His 2019 tool recommendations: **CBT Nuggets** ($59/mo) and **David Bombal's Udemy course**
  (~$10) for video; **Wendell Odom's OCG** or **Todd Lammle** for books; **Boson NetSim** (his
  favorite) or David Bombal's Packet Tracer course (~$10) for labs; **Boson ExSim** and
  **AlphaPrep** for practice exams.
- Wendell Odom is authoring the new CCNA (200-301) book, expected September or November 2019.
- Migration rewards for someone *already* certified on a current track at the cutover: they
  keep the old cert until it expires (3 years from their exam date), automatically receive the
  new CCNA cert, and receive a specialty **training badge** (for LinkedIn/social).
- Old certs remain valid the full three years from the exam date, then go "retired"; you cannot
  recertify a retired track (it's end-of-life) — but the new CCNA you were granted can itself be
  recertified for another three years.
- A viewer stuck at **CCENT / ICND1** at the cutover keeps only the retiring CCENT and gains none
  of the three migration rewards — Chuck's strong message: finish your full CCNA before February.
- **Training badges** (100/200/300/400 levels) reflect completing a *training class*, not passing
  an exam; they're a lower tier than CCNP specialist certifications and exist to bridge the
  knowledge gap between the new single CCNA and CCNP (the old "CCNA Security → CCNP Security"
  stepping stone no longer exists). Chuck admits he finds the training-badge concept "weird."
- Even after the tracks retire, **HR / employers will still search for and value** "CCNA Security"
  etc. on resumes for the next year or two while the industry catches up — another reason to earn
  the track cert now.
- **New CCNA 200-301 exam design (structural):** ~60–70% overlap with current R&S CCNA; adds
  looking at IP config on macOS/Linux/Windows, wireless principles + AP modes/architectures
  (absorbed from CCNA Wireless), virtualization, and security fundamentals (absorbed from CCNA
  Security). OSPF narrowed to OSPFv2/IPv4; multi-area OSPF and EIGRP/RIP/BGP pushed to CCNP.
- **Automation & programmability ≈ 20% of the new CCNA**: DNA Center, REST APIs, reading/interpreting
  JSON, and Ansible/Puppet/Chef — at a conceptual level. **No Python required for CCNA** (though
  Chuck says you *should* start learning it); Python is required at CCNP/CCIE and DevNet levels.
- The new **DevNet Associate** is for software developers, not network engineers: ~80%
  programmability / 20% networking — the inverse of the CCNA's ~80% networking / 20% automation.
- After Feb 24, 2020 there are **no prerequisites** for CCNP — you can jump straight to any CCNP
  cert or concentration without holding a CCNA.
- To go deeper into automation as a network engineer, Chuck recommends the **CCNP automation
  concentration** over the DevNet Associate (unless you genuinely want the developer path).
- For cloud (since CCNA Cloud is being killed), get **vendor-specific cloud certs** — he plugs his
  Azure **AZ-900** course on CBT Nuggets, with **AZ-103** in progress.
- New **recertification** option: earn **30 continuing-education credits within 3 years** (via
  training classes, Cisco Live breakout sessions, writing approved exam questions) instead of
  re-taking the exam.
- Cisco (per Yusuf/Joe) expects more CCNAs and CCNPs post-change but argues it won't devalue them —
  the redesign came from 2+ years of interviews with companies reporting an unmet skills gap.
- The redesign replaces ~10 CCNA "flavors" with **one single CCNA** as a foundation; you then
  branch into specializations at the professional (CCNP) level.
