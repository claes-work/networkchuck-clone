# What happened to the CCNA? CCNP? CCIE? - An Overview (2019)

## Summary

A fuller overview of the sweeping 2020 Cisco certification overhaul (all changes take
effect **February 24, 2020**), companion to the shorter sibling video
[[2019-06-10-yt-nD7zD-cpQG0]]. The first half is Chuck's own "TL;DR" walk-through of
what changed across CCNA, CCNP, and CCIE; the second half is a long interview Chuck and
David Bombal recorded at Cisco Live with the Cisco certification team — **Yousef Mai**
(head of the certification program team) and **Joe** (learning/DevNet, 21 years at
Cisco), who explain the "why" and give a track-by-track overview.

Chuck's headline framing: the change is driven by **adaptation** — Cisco is
restructuring so it can rapidly rev certifications to keep pace with fast-moving tech
(ACI, programmability, SD-WAN, automation). The old **CCENT** (ICND1/ICND2 two-exam
path to CCNA) is being retired; there is now **one single CCNA**, one exam, with all
the old associate specializations (CCNA Security, Collaboration, Data Center, CCDA,
etc.) collapsed — described as a brand-new CCNA, not a converged/consolidated one.

The core of this page is the **CCNP/CCIE restructure detail**: specialization moves
**up** from the associate level to the professional level. Every CCNP track is now
**exactly two exams** — a shared **technology core** exam plus **one concentration** of
the candidate's choice — replacing the old inconsistent three-or-four-exam requirements.
Each exam earns a standalone **Cisco Certified Specialist** badge ("incremental /
as-you-go recognition"), so partial progress finally carries market value. The **CCIE
written is retired** and replaced by that same per-track technology core exam; a
CCNP-core holder is then one **lab** away from CCIE. Chuck's stated ambivalence: two
people can both be "CCNP Enterprise" yet have a large knowledge gap because they chose
different concentrations.

## Key claims (dated)

All dated 2019-06-15 (paraphrase unless quoted). Interview claims are attributed to the
named Cisco speakers, not to Chuck.

- Cisco changed **every** certification at once — not just CCNA/CCNP — the biggest change
  in the program's ~two-decade history; the cutoff is **February 2020** (Chuck says
  "February 23rd," the Cisco reps say "February 24th").
- The driving principle is **adaptation/agility**: the old portfolio was "monolithic"
  and slow to reflect new tech; the new modular design lets Cisco add/retire
  concentrations "like a Lego block" without rebuilding the core (Yousef/Joe).
- **CCENT is retired.** The old ICND1 (CCENT) + ICND2 two-test path to CCNA is gone;
  there is no more "halfway point" to CCNA.
- There is now **one CCNA**, a single exam. All old associate specializations — CCNA
  Security, CCNA Collaboration, CCNA Data Center, and the CCDA design cert — are removed.
- The new CCNA is described as **new, not converged** — not a consolidation of the old
  tracks but a new associate-level program giving broad exposure (routing/switching,
  virtualization/data center, security, wireless, programmability, automation) plus
  practical hands-on skills so an engineer can "hit the ground running" and later
  specialize. Chuck likens it to "Network+ a little bit," but with a practical Cisco
  grounding and a path to go deeper.
- **CCNP is now two exams for every track**: a **technology core** exam + **one
  concentration** exam (previously some CCNPs were three exams, some four — e.g. old
  routing/switching = three, collaboration = four).
- There are **five technology tracks**: enterprise, service provider, data center,
  collaboration, security — **each with its own distinct technology core** (the
  enterprise core is nicknamed **ENCOR**; the security core, the "SCOR"/security core).
  Fundamentals overlap, but each core goes deep on its track.
- **Concentrations are unique per track** and not shared between tracks — e.g. security
  offers Firepower, ISE, or VPN concentrations; enterprise offers advanced routing,
  SD-WAN, etc. You pick exactly one to complete the CCNP.
- **Core and concentration can be taken in any order**, and each earns a standalone
  **Cisco Certified Specialist** certification — "incremental / as-you-go recognition."
  A CCNP therefore yields ~three credentials (two specialists + the CCNP).
- **CCNA is not a prerequisite** for CCNP; a candidate can jump straight to, e.g., a
  Firepower specialist exam with no prior cert.
- Every track has an **automation & programmability concentration**; the enterprise one
  "**double-dips**" — it counts toward both the CCNP and the **DevNet Professional**
  (there is a new DevNet Associate and DevNet Professional, each with its own core).
- **The CCIE written exam is retired**, replaced by the track's **technology core** exam
  (the same one used for CCNP). Yousef stresses it's not that "written is eliminated" —
  it's a new product that now covers implementation, troubleshooting, and simulations,
  not just concepts. Passing the core means a CCNP-core holder only needs **the lab** to
  reach CCIE.
- **New CCIE lab = two modules**: a **3-hour design** (scenario-based, low-level design)
  module and a **5-hour** implement/operate/optimize/troubleshoot module, with
  automation & programmability (e.g. NETCONF/RESTCONF/APIs, not just CLI) folded
  throughout. The gap from CCNP to CCIE is framed as **experience**, not just difficulty
  — and per the acronym, CCNP = "network professional" (individual technologies) vs CCIE
  = "internetworking" (how technologies integrate and break each other).
- **Design track migration**: CCDA maps into the new CCNA (credit for design elements);
  **CCDP migrates to CCNP Enterprise with a design specialist/concentration**. Design
  becomes per-track concentrations (design enterprise, design security, etc.) instead of
  the old generic one-size CCDP. **CCDE and the Architect program are unchanged** for now.
- **Migration/equivalency**: currently certified people keep their credentials, get
  equivalency/credit, and can keep certs "fresh" via continuing education.
- Chuck's ambivalence: because two "CCNP Enterprise" holders can pick different
  concentrations, it's harder to know what a given CCNP-track holder actually knows —
  though the specialist badges (shown on e.g. LinkedIn) disambiguate.
- Both Chuck and Cisco's message: **keep going and finish before February 2020** —
  whatever you're studying, complete the whole cert (e.g. don't get stuck with only
  ICND1) or you may lose progress; there's a payoff for finishing.
- Chuck ties it to his long-running advice to **learn Python** to be the "next-gen
  network engineer" — Cisco is now "making it official" by baking automation into the
  certs.

## Notable verbatim quotes

> "Cisco changed all of their certifications. I mean, all of them. Not just one. Not
> just the CCNA or NP, every single one of them."

> "The CCENT is gone. It's dead. They buried it, killed it. Done. Starting February
> 2020, you can no longer test on the CCENT."

> "There is a new CCNA, but there's only one CCNA. ... One CCNA to rule them all."

> "This new CCNA is not converged ... It's a new CCNA is what they say. It's just a new
> way of thinking about what you need as an associate-level engineer to know going into
> the workplace."

> "Whereas we used to be able to specialize at the NA level ... we can no longer do
> that. Now we're going to be specializing at the NP level."

> "Now, all of them only require two exams. Every single one."

> "The technology core plus the advanced routing will give you CCNP enterprise. ... Or
> maybe you want to be super into SD-WAN. They do have a concentration for that, which is
> like, dang, about time."

> "You could have two people, both CCNP enterprise certified, but they could have kind
> of a large gap between what they know."

> "These are unique to each track. The concentrations will not be shared between the
> other NP exams or certifications."

> "The IE no longer has a CCIE written. The CCIE written is gone. Well, maybe not gone
> per se, but it's been replaced by the technology core."

> "If you are already CCNP enterprise certified, all you have to do is take the lab."

> "We have over 1.7 million certified individuals with over 3 million certifications ...
> this is by far one of the biggest change that we have done in all those years." *(Yousef
> Mai)*

> "Previously in our current portfolio things were very monolithic in terms of the swim
> lane. So if you started a journey in a particular technology, you had to continue
> growing that same kind of a line." *(Yousef Mai)*

> "Now with this new what we call incremental recognition or as-you-go recognition, we
> will provide specialist certification for each and every exam that they take." *(Yousef
> Mai)*

> "Every track has an automation and programmability concentration ... that concentration
> double counts in both the network engineer realm for like the CCNP and in the DevNet
> realm ... I call that double dipping." *(Joe)*

> "CCNP is Cisco Certified Network Professional — network. CCIE says Cisco Certified
> Internetworking. ... The CCIE lab is more about how those technologies integrate
> together and they can break each other." *(Yousef Mai)*

> "Two modules in each of the tracks. One will be three hours of design, scenario-based.
> The other module, five hours: implement, operate, optimize, troubleshoot." *(Joe)*

> "I've been saying you should learn Python, right? You want to be that next-generation
> network engineer. And now Cisco's kind of making it official with their
> certifications." *(Chuck)*
