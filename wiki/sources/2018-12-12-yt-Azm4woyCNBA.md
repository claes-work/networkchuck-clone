# How To get a JOB with a CCNA (Network Engineer) | CCNA Routing and Switching

## Summary

Chuck Keith interviews his younger brother Cameron, who landed a network admin
job making **$60,000–$70,000/year** shortly after earning his CCNA — with only a
CompTIA A+, a CCNA, and a little over a year of general IT experience (help desk +
computer repair), and no college degree. The video is framed as a step-by-step,
reproducible playbook: polish the resume, post it on LinkedIn with career interests
turned on, apply broadly, survive a recruiter screen, a phone technical interview,
and an in-person interview (including whiteboarding a network), and stand out on
ambition and passion rather than raw experience. Notably, Cameron didn't apply for
the job he got — a recruiter found him via his fresh CCNA on LinkedIn. Chuck weaves
in his own interview-prep habits throughout. The video is the first day of a 10-day
Christmas giveaway (AlphaPrep CCNA practice exam software).

## Key claims (dated paraphrase — the step-by-step playbook, as of 2018-12-12)

**Timeline / outcome**
- Cameron got an interview within a month of passing the CCNA and was in the job
  within 2–3 months (the employer had hiring delays).
- His pre-job experience: ~9–10 months of help desk + ~4–5 months as a computer
  repair technician — a little over a year of general IT, none of it network-admin
  specific.
- Compensation: $60K–$70K/year on just an A+, a CCNA, ~1 year help desk experience,
  and no college.

**Step 1 — Fix the resume**
- Put certifications at the top; list most recent experience first (ideally a prior
  technical role like help desk / desktop support so employers see you can do
  technical work).
- Don't just copy a generic resume template — you're applying for an IT job, treat it
  accordingly.
- Certifications signal two things: you know your stuff, and you put in the time to
  learn it.
- List the certification you plan to work on next, so employers see you keep learning
  and aren't the type who gets one cert and stops.
- Put a cert you're currently studying on the resume with "(in progress)" in
  parentheses. Chuck does this himself — he listed CCNP "in progress" (exam scheduled
  Jan 15) on all his resumes.
- Why "in progress" works: HR uses automated resume-screening software (Chuck recalls
  a system name like "silk-something") that filters resumes against minimum criteria
  before a human sees them. An "in-progress" cert can get you flagged/passed through
  to the hiring manager, who then reads it as "this guy is continually learning."
- Employers care most that you can come in and learn the job — every company does
  things differently and won't just use Cisco, so you'll have a lot to learn on day
  one regardless of certs.

**Step 2 — Post the resume and get visible**
- Post primarily on **LinkedIn**. Cameron tried Indeed but mostly got spam offers
  (e.g., overnight mobile-phone technician gigs) that didn't match network
  engineering.
- Chuck says he personally got 2–3 jobs from LinkedIn; download the LinkedIn Jobs app
  and set up job alerts.
- Use LinkedIn's "Easy Apply" (one-click apply); some listings redirect to the
  company's own job board.
- Take advantage of the LinkedIn Premium free trial — it shows who viewed your
  profile and how you rank against other applicants (their degrees, experience), so
  you know your chances. (Explicitly not sponsored.)
- Structure your LinkedIn profile much like your resume: a professional-looking photo
  and a clear description of the job you want.
- **Turn on your "career interests"** so recruiters can see you're actively looking —
  Chuck and Cameron call this vital.

**Step 3 — Apply broadly**
- Cameron applied to 30+ jobs; Chuck says that's a normal amount. Apply to anything
  you're even remotely qualified for — few candidates are ever a perfect fit, so you
  might be the one they pick.

**Step 4 — Get found / the recruiter screen**
- Cameron did NOT apply to the job he got — a recruiter (named John) found him because
  he'd added his fresh CCNA to LinkedIn (even before the license was officially
  verified) and turned on career interests, so he surfaced in a search for new CCNAs.
- Employers actively want **fresh CCNAs**: less experience means fewer bad habits to
  untrain, and they know you're ready to learn. (Chuck's caveat: anyone can list an
  unverified 13-year-old CCNA on LinkedIn and still show up, so freshness is partly a
  proxy.)
- The recruiter first confirms your interests match the position, then sets up a phone
  interview with the hiring manager and a technical person. Going through a recruiting
  agency is common if you've never done a technical interview — they vet you and
  connect you to HR.

**Step 5 — The phone (technical) interview**
- Two gates: an initial "are you a real human who can hold a conversation" check
  (Chuck notes conversational ability is rare in IT), then a technical phone screen.
- Expect a barrage of technical questions — often not deep CCNA-exam-level, more
  "buzzword" checks: what is spanning tree, what's your favorite dynamic routing
  protocol, etc.
- They're testing two things: whether you actually know what they're talking about,
  and how well you can *explain* it — communication is a valuable, tested skill in IT.
- Stay fresh even right after passing the exam.
- If asked something you don't know, don't fake it — say you don't know; they respect
  honesty. Cameron admitted he knew nothing about firewalls (CCNA R&S only) and they
  respected it. Chuck has interviewed people who BS'd answers; it's obvious and reads
  as dishonest — not who you want on your team.

**Step 6 — The in-person interview(s)**
- Cameron ended up doing three interviews in one day (abnormal, per the employer):
  the hiring manager + the two phone interviewers, their head security guy, and
  (after driving to corporate) the director of IT projects.
- Handshake: have a firm handshake (not limp). Stand up and shake everyone's hand
  when they enter — a sign of respect.
- Dress the part: Cameron went all out — suit and tie — to show he wanted it.
- Project confidence: you have to *show* confidence, not just act it; give each
  interviewer equal attention and eye contact.
- Body language / hands: open hands read as open and confident; closed/balled fists
  read as nervous or closed off (Chuck deliberately keeps his hands open even though
  it's uncomfortable). They actually liked animated hand motion because it signaled
  passion. Acting nervous makes them worry you'll crumble under pressure — and these
  jobs aren't easy.

**Step 7 — The whiteboard**
- Expect to be handed a marker and asked to diagram a network on a whiteboard, even if
  you've never done it. Cameron drew the three-tier hierarchical network model from
  the CCNA, then they pushed deeper: "where are your firewalls, where are your
  wireless LAN controllers" — they want you thinking beyond CCNA level.
- Technical questions go one level deeper than the exam: e.g., name your favorite
  dynamic routing protocol (Cameron said EIGRP), then explain *why* — the difference
  between link-state and advanced distance-vector protocols and why you'd pick it in
  an enterprise. They want to know you understand the difference, not every detail.

**Interview prep (Chuck's own habit)**
- Chuck doesn't rehearse in a mirror, but runs "a million scenarios" in his head
  beforehand. ~30 minutes before an interview he walks somewhere alone and talks out
  loud, explaining technologies to himself to check he can articulate them and get
  them on the tip of his tongue — so he arrives already thinking technically.

**What sealed it**
- Cameron learned afterward that what set him apart was his **ambition and genuine
  passion** for the technology — not just a guy who got a CCNA to get it. In the
  interview he talked about his **home lab**: DMVPN connections between his and
  Chuck's apartments, Chuck's own data center, VoIP phones, all connected over DMVPN.
  Chuck's takeaway: if you can demonstrate passion (e.g., a home lab), it comes off —
  put your best foot forward.
- On the job title: "systems engineer of networking" — IT titles are random and
  rarely describe what you actually do.
- Reality of the role: they told him on day one he'd focus primarily on **wireless**
  (despite not holding a CCNA Wireless), plus a lot of routing & switching, some
  phone, little firewall (a separate team handles that). They hired him knowing he
  could learn quickly rather than for existing wireless expertise.
