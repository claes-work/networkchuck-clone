# I'm worried about Network Engineers.....

## Summary

Filmed at Cisco Live 2025 (the largest networking conference in the world, ~22,000
attendees; the video is sponsored by Cisco), this is Chuck Keith's opinion/investigation
piece on a question he says has been "keeping me up at night": **Is there a future for
network engineers in the age of AI — will the job still exist in 5 years?** Chuck, himself
a former network engineer, roams the conference floor asking Cisco staff, vendors, and
working network engineers three things: how they feel about AI, whether they use it in
their jobs, and whether they're worried about their careers.

The dominant sentiment he finds is **cautious optimism**, which he adopts as his own
conclusion. Chuck's thesis: AI is *not* going to eliminate network engineering, but it is
going to **change the job**, and the engineers who refuse to adapt will be left behind.
The recurring line he heard "many many times" — and endorses — is **"AI isn't going to
replace you, but someone using AI will."** He frames AI as the *next iteration of
automation*, the same kind of shift the field already lived through with voice-over-IP and
network automation. His practical advice to network engineers: start playing with AI now,
ask "how can AI help me do my current job better?", get into community with fellow
engineers, and keep studying the fundamentals (IP addressing, subnetting, OSPF, EIGRP) —
because humans who understand the protocols are needed to tell AI "when it's being dumb."

The video also documents Cisco's AI announcements as evidence that real change is coming:
the **Deep Network Model** (a purpose-built networking LLM that scored ~20% better than
leading LLMs on CCIE-style lab/exam tasks), **AI Canvas** (an AI-first, multimodal network
control panel that generates real-time dashboards and can produce *and apply* a QoS
config from plain-language intent), and executive framing from Chuck Robbins (the "AI race
is on") and Jeetu (G2) Patel (augmenting 8 billion people to an "80 billion" workforce
throughput, with a future workforce of humans plus AI agents, apps, robotics, humanoids).
Chuck flags the config-apply demo as the genuinely "terrifying" moment — a task a network
engineer would traditionally have done by hand. He also relays a Cisco training view
(Kareem Escander, Joe Clark) that certification exams may need to change: less
multiple-choice memorization, more hands-on config plus judging/interpreting AI output and
catching hallucinations.

## Key claims (dated — thesis + advice)

All dated 2025-06-13 (publication), paraphrased unless quoted.

- **Central worry.** Chuck says the question of whether network engineers will still exist
  in 5 years has been keeping him up at night; it's why he came to Cisco Live to ask
  people directly. He admits "we kind of have a reason to be a little bit worried."
- **Thesis — adapt or be left behind.** AI will not eliminate network engineering, but it
  *will* change the role; the engineers who don't adapt get left behind, while those who
  learn and use AI in their current roles keep their jobs.
- **"Someone using AI will replace you."** A common theme he heard and endorses: AI itself
  won't replace you, but a person who uses AI will out-compete you.
- **AI is the next generation of automation.** Chuck frames AI as simply another iteration
  of the automation shift network engineers already went through (manual CLI → automating
  thousands of devices at once), comparable to the earlier telephony → VoIP migration.
- **The "parity gap" is the real danger.** People who aren't using AI and aren't paying
  attention will "wake up one day to a whole new reality that they're not prepared for" —
  the ones asleep on it are who he's worried about.
- **Practical advice.** Adapt now: if you haven't started playing with AI, become very
  familiar with it; ask yourself "how can AI help me do my current job better?"; get into
  community and talk with fellow engineers about what they're doing.
- **Keep the fundamentals.** No matter how good AI gets, the field needs people who know
  the protocols (IP addressing, subnetting, OSPF, EIGRP) so they can tell AI when it's
  wrong; you can't build or verify a network without actually understanding how networking
  works. Encouragingly, tons of people are still pursuing CCNA/CCNP.
- **Future job shape.** Network engineers of the future will spend less time "in the weeds"
  (outages, log-reading, SSHing into boxes) and more on making the network better and
  automating more of it; the mundane day-to-day tasks go away, likely handled by agents.
- **Skill stack widens.** The future network engineer looks similar to today's but adds
  security/cybersecurity skills (red teaming, pen testing, blue-team incident response and
  threat hunting) and natively understands automation — because AI's real benefits only
  come once you actually automate network operations (interview view attributed to a Cisco
  interviewee, echoed by Chuck).
- **Optimism as a choice.** Chuck repeatedly says the field is *choosing* optimism ("you
  have to be optimistic — what else are you going to do?"), and he adopts that stance while
  admitting no one can truly predict 5 years out; everyone was surprised where AI is now.
- **AI should augment, not replace people.** Chuck's values position: people need to work
  and need purpose; AI should always help but never replace humans. He references the
  Anthropic CEO's "lamplighter"/50%-of-jobs warning as the doomer framing to take
  seriously but not fully adopt.
- **The network powers AI (job security angle).** More AI means more data centers, which
  means more demand for network engineers; "AI runs on the network," so the more AI there
  is, the more it needs network people. Networking may become *more* critical and more
  specialized (supporting HPC, GPUs, LLMs in the data center).
- **Cisco evidence of change (2025-06-13 keynote).** Deep Network Model scored ~20% better
  than leading LLMs on CCIE-style lab/exam tasks; AI Canvas demoed live generating
  real-time multimodal dashboards and producing *and applying* a QoS policy from natural-
  language intent — the task a network engineer would normally do by hand.
- **Certification exams may need to change.** Cisco trainers suggested future exams could
  grant LLM/search access and drop pure multiple-choice memorization in favor of real
  hands-on config plus evaluating and fact-checking AI-generated answers.
- **Timeframe caveats (attributed to interviewees).** One interviewee estimated ~25% of
  jobs "in their current form" will go away while new jobs appear; another said networking
  has roughly a "3 to 5 years safety net" and that beyond that it's hard to predict.
