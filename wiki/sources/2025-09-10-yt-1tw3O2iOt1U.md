# Cisco's Certification Director Explains the Future of CCNA

## Summary

Interview recorded at Cisco Live. Chuck (with Jeremy present) interviews **Ryan Rose**,
the Skills and Certification Director for Learn with Cisco, about where the CCNA and the
broader Cisco certification portfolio are heading. Chuck frames the conversation around
his own mission ("Jeremy and I, we're super focused on making video content that helps
people get their certifications, get their jobs") and drives it with questions about how
certs stay relevant amid fast-moving AI, whether the CCNA is becoming an "everything
cert," how AI tooling (Cisco's "deep network"/Deep Network AI) fits, whether exams will
allow AI collaboration, and what a network engineer looks like in five years.

The bulk of the substance — Cisco's plans, roadmap, and framing — comes from **the guest,
Ryan Rose**, and represents Cisco's official/insider view, NOT Chuck's persona. Only
Chuck's questions and framing train the persona.

## Key claims (attributed per speaker)

**Ryan Rose (Cisco Skills & Certification Director) — Cisco's view / his stated opinion:**

- (2025-09-10) Cisco's certification philosophy starts with **skills first**, then adds
  training, then connects to a certification journey; certs are seen as validating skills
  that "unlock capabilities" rather than being the starting goal.
- (2025-09-10) Cisco emphasizes durable, career-long skills (e.g. **design** — thinking
  about business requirements, outcomes, operations) over teaching specific products,
  because products and technologies change.
- (2025-09-10) The DevNet portfolio was **renamed to reflect CCNA branding**: DevNet
  Associate is now **CCNA Automation**, with **CCNP Automation** and **CCIE Automation**;
  programs are structured into "knowledge domains" built with internal and external
  experts (including talking to Nvidia when building AI content).
- (2025-09-10) CCNA Automation includes an entire domain on **understanding and using
  APIs** as a skill (what an API is, how to apply them, storing network configs in a
  GitHub repo), not vendor-specific product training.
- (2025-09-10) CCNA will **remain the entry point into networking**, but the expectations
  of the CCNA engineer are changing — more OT topics, more security (there's a full
  security domain), and a **troubleshooting/problem-solver mindset** (not IE-level, but a
  troubleshooting mindset), reflecting feedback heard "all week" at Cisco Live.
- (2025-09-10) Cisco already teaches **AI usage in the CCNA** — understanding predictive
  AI vs. generative AI, and knowing which Cisco tools are powered by which (e.g. network
  assurance / AIML; ThousandEyes, Internet Insights vs. the generative "deep model");
  data analytics training has been added to **pre-CCNA / early years** now that Networking
  Academy and certifications are one org.
- (2025-09-10) On AI assistants (Cisco U tutorials exist; Deep Network AI marketed as able
  to pass CCIE): AI is **"another tool in the toolbox"**; Gen Z and Gen Alpha already use
  these tools, so Cisco must show professional use. Human + AI will unlock creativity in
  network architecture/design.
- (2025-09-10) On AI during exams: Cisco is **exploring options** but **exam conditions are
  not changing right now** — students should keep studying and take exams as-is. (Notes
  that the DevNet Associate / CCNA Automation exam already introduced surprising new areas.)
- (2025-09-10) **"Ryan Rose opinion" — the modern network engineer skill split:** ~50%
  secure route & switch (the foundation), ~20% automation (programming, NetDevOps), ~20%
  cloud (on-prem, hybrid, multicloud), ~10% AI and AI infrastructure (including cost
  awareness of AI infra). The CCNA was updated "last April" (April 2025).
- (2025-09-10) **Five-year future cast (his opinion):** the network engineer looks similar
  to today's but with more **security/cybersecurity** skills (red teaming, pen testing,
  blue teaming, incident response, threat hunting) and native **automation** — AI benefits
  are only realized once network operations are actually automated; "it's all hype until
  you really automate that network activity."

**Chuck (NetworkChuck) — questions/framing (persona-relevant):**

- (2025-09-10) States his and Jeremy's mission: making video content to help people get
  certifications and jobs.
- (2025-09-10) Observes the **CCNA is "kind of becoming the everything cert"** — very
  network-focused but bringing in AI skills and API skills — and asks whether it will
  evolve beyond just networking.
- (2025-09-10) Frames AI tools (like Cisco's Deep Network / "CCIE buddy") as something
  engineers will have **alongside them** to troubleshoot, design, and build networks.

**Ryan Rose (Cisco cert director) — context, NOT persona data:**

> With all of this talk about AI, I keep saying this is a time for experts. This is a time

> answer.

> We used to tell people automation's not going to take your job but the people that know
> automation they're the ones that are going to get hired. I feel the same way about AI
> now. The people that know how to use AI they're the ones that are going to get hired.

> I think 50% of your job as a modern network engineer is secure route and switch... I
> think 20% of your job is around automation... I think 20% of your job is around cloud
> skills... And then 10% of your skills are in AI and AI infra.

> Everybody thinks about AI almost like it's an easy button. It's not. If you really want
> to get the benefits of AI... you have to automate more of your network operations and
> activities.
