# the TOP 10 hottest IT Jobs in 2023

## Summary

Chuck runs down his list of the 10 "hottest" IT jobs for 2023, where "hot" means
more demand and higher salaries. His ranking is data-anchored: each job is drawn
from the **2023 Dice Tech Salary Survey**, selected for having the highest
salary increases (which he treats as a proxy for rising demand). For each role he
gives the survey's salary-increase percentage and average salary, describes what
the job actually involves, where it sits on a career progression, which
certifications help you land it, and walks through a real example job posting.

Two big through-lines: (1) **soft skills / customer service** matter everywhere in
IT, and (2) most of these roles are steps on a ladder that starts at the help desk,
not entry points you jump straight into. The video is sponsored by ITProTV / ACI
Learning. Note: Chuck's on-screen numbering is inconsistent (he says "number
eight" twice and skips "number six" verbally), so the list below preserves the
role order as presented.

## Key claims (dated — 2023 picks, per 2023 Dice Tech Salary Survey)

- **"Hot" is defined as more demand + higher salaries**; the list is sourced from
  the 2023 Dice Tech Salary Survey, picking roles with the highest salary increases
  (as of 2023). [paraphrase]
- **#1 (his top pick) — Help Desk Technician**: +6.9% increase, ~$55,000 average.
  Chuck's own first IT job and, in his view, a non-skippable stepping stone. Needs
  little prior experience; the top skill is customer service / soft skills, then
  troubleshooting, then a solid IT foundation. Recommended cert: **CompTIA A+**
  (his first cert) — the only cert he'd focus on until you land that first job.
  Example posting: technical desktop support at Dallas Independent School District.
  [paraphrase]
- **#2 — IT Project Manager**: +15.6% increase, ~$120,000 average. For organized
  planners; owns planning, execution and completion of IT projects on time and on
  budget. Average salary reflects roles needing 5–7 years' experience — you won't
  start there. Recommended certs: **CompTIA Project+** to start, then **PMP** (the
  "gold standard," requires proof of experience — ~60 months + 35 hours training
  without a degree). Chuck says he personally would hate this job. [paraphrase]
- **#3 — Systems Engineer / Sys Admin**: +7.5% increase, ~$120,000 average. Good
  next step after help desk; a broad umbrella (VMware, cloud, server, Linux
  engineers/admins) focused on designing, building and maintaining company
  solutions. No single cert fits — options include Azure Administrator (AZ-104),
  Google Associate Cloud Engineer, AWS Solutions Architect Associate, CompTIA
  Linux+ / LPIC-1/2, plus Python and PowerShell as pluses. [paraphrase]
- **#4 — Cybersecurity Engineer**: +7.7% increase, ~$145,000 average. One of the
  hottest careers anywhere, not just IT. An **advanced** career, not an entry point
  — you combine help desk, sys admin, Linux/Windows and cloud skills first, because
  you must understand how things work before protecting them. Red-team path: EJPT,
  CPTS (Hack The Box), then OSCP. Blue-team path: vendor security certs (e.g.
  Azure Security Engineer Associate). Example: Toyota North America red/purple-team
  role wanting ~5 years' experience. Junior roles (NOC/SOC) exist and are easier
  to land. [paraphrase]
- **#5 — DevOps Engineer ("the unicorn")**: +14.1% increase, ~$136,000 average.
  A rare blend of software programming + system administration; the middleman
  automating systems and building DevOps pipelines. Advanced, later-career role.
  Tools: Puppet, Chef, Jenkins, Ansible, plus cloud pipelines (Google, Azure, AWS).
  Certs: AWS Certified DevOps Engineer, and an Azure equivalent. [paraphrase]
- **MIS / IT Manager** (Chuck labels "number seven"): +10.8% increase, ~$132,000
  average. Management role — high demand, high salary, hard; requires being gifted
  at managing people plus knowing some IT. Usual path is rising up from a support/
  supervisor role; downside is doing more management and less hands-on tech.
  [paraphrase]
- **IT Management / C-suite (CTO, CIO, VP, Director)** (Chuck labels "number
  eight"): +8.4% increase, ~$164,000 average (with a wide range). Leads the
  leaders/managers, sets vision (cloud vs on-prem), owns budgets, answers for
  outages and breaches ("the buck stops with you"). He showed a posting at
  $800,000/year remote wanting ~10 years of management experience. This is the one
  IT area where Chuck says you need a degree — ideally a master's or MBA.
  [paraphrase]
- **Application Support Engineer** (Chuck also labels "number eight"): +6.3%
  increase, ~$96,000 average. Supports a *specific application* (vs. the help
  desk's general user support); needs some programming (Python, Java, C++, .NET),
  software testing, networking/database basics, plus ALM and frameworks like
  Waterfall/Agile/ITIL/Scrum. Advanced. Example: JP Morgan Chase. Certs: ITIL/
  Scrum courses, AWS Certified SysOps Administrator. [paraphrase]
- **#9 — Program Analyst / Program Manager**: +12.9% increase, ~$139,000 average.
  Niche role at big companies with large complex projects; works alongside the
  project manager to keep projects on budget/on time, analyzing risk and monitoring
  compliance/regulation. Often government/military. Example: program analyst at
  FEMA. [paraphrase]
- **#10 (the surprise) — Network Engineer**: +6.1% increase, ~$99,000 average.
  Chuck's own former job. Rebuts "network engineering is dying" — automation
  changes the skillset but the role is very much alive and the numbers prove it.
  Good next step after help desk for those who love packets/routers/switches. Best
  cert: **Cisco CCNA** (which he holds and teaches) — the gold standard, gets you a
  junior network role; senior roles add SD-WAN/SD-access, CCNP/CCDP, Juniper certs
  and coding. [paraphrase]
- Recurring career advice (2023): customer service / soft skills are demanded
  across every IT area; being fun, humble and hardworking beats most peers; job
  postings are "wishlists" — apply if you meet two or three of the requirements
  because employers expect you to learn the rest on the job. [paraphrase]
