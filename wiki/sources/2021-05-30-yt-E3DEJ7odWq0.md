# fiber optic cables (what you NEED to know) // FREE CCNA // EP 13

## Summary

Episode 13 of NetworkChuck's FREE CCNA course, sponsored by Boson Software. Chuck opens with a field trip to his garage to show the AT&T single-mode fiber terminating at his house, which gives him symmetrical gigabit (1000 Mbps) internet. He then teaches how fiber optic cabling works and why it is the medium that connects the world.

The core lesson: unlike copper Ethernet, which sends electrical signals, fiber sends pulses of light (ones and zeros) down a glass or plastic core. Three advantages over Ethernet are given: it is fast (near speed of light), it goes far (low attenuation), and it produces no EMI. The science is explained via refraction, the critical angle, and total internal reflection — light bounces down the core and the cladding keeps it from leaving.

Two fiber types are contrasted. Multi-mode has a large core (50–62.5 microns), usually plastic, letting light bounce/reflect as it travels; higher attenuation limits distance (Chuck's OM3 multi-mode does 10 Gig at up to 300 m). Single-mode has a tiny core (5–9 microns) so light travels straight like a bullet without bouncing, giving lower attenuation, higher bandwidth, and distances up to 100 km (~60 miles). We use both: single-mode is more expensive to manufacture (fine-tuned machines for the small core), so multi-mode is used cheaply inside buildings (floor-to-floor, switch-to-switch, data-center racks) while single-mode connects buildings, campuses, ISPs, and undersea cables.

Practical section (from Boson courseware): fiber connectors — LC (newest, most common), plus older ST and SC. Duplex cables have two strands, one for transmit and one for receive. Since switches/servers often have RJ45 ports, fiber plugs into SFP (small form-factor pluggable) modules — "wildcard" ports that accept either an RJ45 module or a fiber module. Why still use Ethernet? It is cheaper, easier to manage, works with everything (RJ45), you can make your own cables, and it carries PoE (fiber does not). Cables are color-coded: yellow = single-mode, aqua/orange = multi-mode. Video ends with the recurring free-coffee quiz.

## Key claims (dated — the concepts)

All dated 2021-05-30 (paraphrase):

- Fiber optic cable transmits data as pulses of light (ones and zeros) rather than electrical signals like copper Ethernet.
- Three reasons to use fiber over Ethernet: speed (near light speed), distance (goes far), and no EMI.
- Light in fiber does not actually travel at full speed of light — it takes roughly a 31% cut because it passes through a glass/plastic medium rather than a vacuum.
- Fiber has very low attenuation (rate at which a signal decreases); single-mode fiber can run up to 100 km (~60 miles) without hurting the signal.
- Ethernet copper is typically capped around 100 meters because its electrical signal degrades with distance.
- Fiber produces no EMI because there is no electricity/electromagnetic field, so no crosstalk or interference between cables.
- Fiber bandwidth is effectively theoretical/uncapped; speeds of hundreds of terabits per second have been measured.
- Light travels down the core by bouncing off the walls via refraction; finding the critical angle produces total internal reflection, keeping the light within the core medium.
- You should never kink or sharply bend fiber — the glass/plastic breaks and the cable becomes unusable; keep it spooled.
- Cable anatomy: core (light travels through it), cladding (~125 microns for both types; keeps light in the core), plus strengthening material and a jacket/buffer for protection.
- Multi-mode: large core (50–62.5 microns), usually plastic (sometimes glass); light bounces/reflects; higher attenuation, shorter distance.
- Single-mode: tiny core (5–9 microns), usually glass; light goes straight without bouncing; lower attenuation, higher bandwidth, longest distance.
- A micron is one-millionth of a meter, written with the "µ" (micro) symbol.
- Single-mode has higher bandwidth than multi-mode despite its smaller core (counter-intuitive).
- We use both types because single-mode is more expensive to manufacture (the tiny core needs fine-tuned machines); multi-mode is cheaper where extreme distance is not needed.
- Multi-mode use cases: inside buildings, floor-to-floor, switch-to-switch, IDF closets to server room, connecting hosts/VMs to storage in data-center racks.
- Multi-mode has grades OM1, OM2, OM3, OM4, OM5; OM4 can reach 40–100 Gbps, OM3 does 10 Gig; keep runs under ~100 m for best bandwidth even though longer is possible.
- Single-mode use cases: connecting buildings, campuses, ISP-to-customer (AT&T gigabit), data center to data center, and country-to-country undersea cables.
- Undersea fiber cables use repeaters (powered by copper carrying electricity alongside the fiber) to regenerate the light signal over distances beyond ~60 miles.
- Fiber connectors: LC (newest, most common in Chuck's career), ST and SC (older, still seen). Match the connector on both ends to the device (LC-to-LC, LC-to-SC, etc.).
- You cannot make/splice your own fiber cables easily — it requires specialized skills and equipment; buy fiber cables pre-made.
- Duplex fiber has two strands labeled A and B: one dedicated to receiving, one to transmitting.
- SFP (small form-factor pluggable) is a "wildcard" module port on switches/servers; you slide in an RJ45 module or a fiber module to make the port whatever you need. Cables can be SFP-to-SFP (direct-attach).
- Ethernet still has its place: cheaper, easier to manage, universally compatible (RJ45), user-makeable cables, and it carries PoE — you cannot get power over fiber.
- Color coding: yellow cable = single-mode fiber; aqua or orange = multi-mode fiber.
